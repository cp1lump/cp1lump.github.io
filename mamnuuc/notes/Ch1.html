Chapter 1 Limits and continuity

1.1 The limit of a convergent sequence

Recall the key definition from MATH1026 Sets, Sequences and Series (also covered in MATH1055 Numbers and Vectors), that of the limit of a convergent real sequence:

Definition 1.1

A real sequence (an) converges to the limit L if, for each ε>0, there exists N∈ℤ+ such that, for all n≥N, |an-L|<ε. The shorthand for this is an→L or limn→∞⁡an=L.

Of course, it’s also vital that you can convert the picture into a proof:

Example 1.2

Claim: 1/n→0.

Proof.

Let ε∈(0,∞) be given. Then there exists N∈ℤ+ such that N>1/ε (because the set ℤ+ is unbounded above). But then, for all n≥N,

|an-0|=1n≤1N<ε.

∎

In the proof above, we showed that, for any prescribed ε>0, there does indeed exist a positive integer N such that, for all n≥N, |an-0|<ε (namely, any N>1/ε will do). A proof like this is called a proof “from first principles", or an “ε—N proof." The key step was to estimate (that is, bound above) the quantity |an-L|.

Many fundamental properties of convergent sequences follow quickly from Definition 1.1. See if you can prove the following propositions. In each case, there’s a short video to help you out if you get stuck.

Proposition 1.4 (Uniqueness)

The limit of a convergent sequence is unique. [VIDEO]

Definition 1.5

A sequence (an) is bounded above if there exists M∈ℝ such that, for all n∈ℤ+, an≤M. Any such M is called an upper bound on (an). A sequence (an) is bounded below if there exists K∈ℝ such that, for all n∈ℤ+, an≥K. Any such K is called a lower bound on (an). The sequence is bounded if it bounded above and below.

Proposition 1.6 (Boundedness)

If (an) converges then (an) is bounded (that is, there exists K∈ℝ such that, for all n∈ℤ+, |an|≤K). [VIDEO]

Note the converse of Proposition 1.6 is false. E.g. an=(-1)n is bounded, but not convergent. If a sequence does not converge, we say it diverges (or is divergent). So (-1)n diverges. Don’t confuse the concepts of divergence and unboundedness.

Proposition 1.7 (Limits preserve non-strict inequalities)

If an→L and, for all n∈ℤ+, K≤an≤M, then K≤L≤M. [VIDEO]

Another way of stating this is that, if K≤an≤M for all n∈ℤ+ then

K≤limn→∞⁡an≤M,

assuming the limit exists. What if we know that an actually obeys strict inequalities,

K<an<M ?

Can we conclude that

K<limn→∞⁡an<M ?

Sadly, no, and Example 1.2 provides a counterexample: 0<1/n<2 for all n∈ℤ+, but its limit L=0 does not satisfy 0<L<2.

Proposition 1.8 (The Squeeze Rule)

Assume an≤bn≤cn, an→L and cn→L. Then bn→L. [VIDEO]

So if a sequence (bn) is “squeezed" between two sequences which both converge to a common limit L, (bn) converges to L also. We will use this fact very frequently.

Proposition 1.9 (The Algebra of Limits)

If an→A and bn→B then

  1. (i)
    ​

    an+bn→A+B. [VIDEO]

  2. (ii)
    ​

    an⁢bn→A⁢B. [VIDEO]

  3. (iii)
    ​

    an/bn→A/B, provided B≠0 and, for all n∈ℤ+, bn≠0. [VIDEO]

Informally speaking, a subsequence (bk) of a sequence (an) is a sequence all of whose terms occur in (an), in the correct order. More precisely:

Definition 1.10

(bk) is a subsequence of (an) if there exists a strictly increasing sequence of positive integers (nk) such that bk=ank.

For example bk=1/2k is a subsequence of an=1/n: take nk=2k. So is ck=1/(k+50): take nk=k+50. However dk=k/(k+1) is not a subsequence of (an) because, for example, b2=2/3 is not a term in (an). Neither is the sequence (ek)=(1,1/2,1,1/3,1,1/4,…). All the terms of (ek) do appear in (an), but not in the correct order.

The key fact about subsequences is the following:

Proposition 1.11

If an→L and (bk) is a subsequence of (an), then bk→L. [VIDEO]

So to prove that a sequence diverges it’s enough to show that it has a subsequence that diverges, or to find two subsequences which converge to different limits (why? Look at Proposition 1.4).

So far, all the facts we’ve established about limits of sequences follow very directly from Definition 1.1. The following two results are rather deeper. To prove them, one needs to appeal to a fundamental defining property of the set of real numbers called completeness. We will return to this later in the module, when we study the theory of integration, but for the time being you should just take them on trust (or, if you studied MATH1026 last year, consult your notes from there). The first result is called the Montone Convergence Theorem, and to state it we need a preliminary definition:

Definition 1.12

A sequence (an) is increasing if an+1≥an for all n∈ℤ+. It is decreasing if an+1≤an for all n∈ℤ+. It is monotonic (or monotone) if it is increasing or decreasing.

So (1/n) is decreasing, (2⁢n+1) is increasing, and hence both are monotonic. The constant sequence (1,1,1,…) is both increasing and decreasing , hence monotonic. The sequence (-1,1,-1,1,-1,1,…) is neither increasing nor decreasing, so is not monotonic.

Theorem 1.13 (Montone Convergence Theorem)

If (an) is bounded and monotonic, then (an) converges.

Finally we have the wonderfully named Bolzano-Weierstrass11 1 Weiertstraß wenn du Deutsch bist. Theorem:

Theorem 1.14 (Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem)

Every bounded real sequence has a convergent subsequence.

In fact, the Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem follows quite easily from the Monontone Convergence theorem: we just show that every sequence has a monotonic subsequence. If the sequence is bounded, so is its monotonic subsequence, which much converge by Theorem 1.13.

1.2 Convergence of sequences and the Cauchy property

One slightly inconvenient thing about Definition 1.1 is that, to prove a sequence converges from first principles, we must first know what its limit is. In this section we will study a criterion which turns out to be equivalent to convergence, but which makes no mention of limits. This will turn out to be extremely useful later on, when we come to analyze the properties of functions defined by power series.

Definition 1.15

A real sequence (an) is Cauchy (or has the Cauchy property) if, for each ε>0, there exists N∈ℤ+ such that, for all n,m≥N, |an-am|<ε.

Remark This looks very similar to the definition of convergence. It says that, given any ε>0 (no matter how small), there is a point in the sequence beyond which all terms lie closer than distance ε from one another. Note that it makes no mention of any limit.

We will show that real sequences converge if and only if they have the Cauchy property. Once again, this is revision if you studied MATH1026, but is probably new if not.

Lemma 1.16

If (an) converges then (an) is Cauchy.

Proof.

Assume an→L. Let ε>0 be given. Then ε/2>0, so there exists N∈ℤ+ such that, for all n≥N,

|an-L|<ε2.

But then, for all n,m≥N,

|an-am|=|(an-L)-(am-L)|≤|an-L|+|am-L|<ε2+ε2=ε

by the Triangle Inequality. Hence (an) is Cauchy. ∎

Lemma 1.17

If (an) is Cauchy then (an) is bounded.

Proof.

Since (an) is Cauchy, there exists N∈ℤ+ such that, for all n,m≥N, |an-am|<1. In particular, for all n≥N, |an-aN|<1, so |an|<|aN|+1. Consider the finite set

A={|a1|,|a2|,…,|aN-1|,|aN|+1}.

Since A is finite, it has a maximum element, K say. Then, for all n<N, |an|≤K (since each of these numbers appears in the set A) and for all n≥N, |an|<|aN|+1≤K. Hence, for all n∈ℤ+, |an|≤K. ∎

Recall that if a sequence converges to a limit L, every subsequence of that sequence also converges to L (Proposition 1.11). In general, knowing that a single subsequence converges to L tells us nothing about convergence of the original sequence, however. For example an=(1+(-1)n)⁢n has the subsequence bk=a2⁢k+1=(1-1)⁢(2⁢k+1)=0 which converges to 0. The sequence (an), however, does not converge to 0: indeed, it isn’t even bounded.

However, if we know that (an) is Cauchy, and it has a convergent subsequence, we can conclude that (an) converges:

Lemma 1.18

Let (an) be Cauchy, and assume some subsequence of (an) converges to L. Then (an) converges to L.

Proof.

Let (ank) be the subsequence which converges to L. Let ε>0 be given. Since ank→L, there exists N1∈ℤ+ such that, for all k≥N1, |ank-L|<ε/2. Further, since (an) is Cauchy, there exists N2∈ℤ+ such that, for all n,m≥N2, |an-am|<ε/2. Let N=max⁡{N1,N2}. Then, for all n≥N,

|an-L| = |(an-anN)+(anN-L)|                        
≤ |an-anN|+|anN-L|        (Triangle Inequality)
< ε2+|anN-L|        (since n≥N2, and nN≥N≥N2)
< ε2+ε2        (since N≥N1).

Hence, an→L. ∎

Our target theorem (a sequence converges if and only if it is Cauchy) now follows immediately from the Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem (Theorem 1.14).

Theorem 1.19

A real sequence converges if and only if it is Cauchy.

Proof.

If (an) converges, it is Cauchy, by Lemma 1.16. Conversely, if (an) is Cauchy, it is bounded (Lemma 1.17), so has a convergent subsequence (by the Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem) and so converges (Lemma 1.18). ∎

Let’s put this theory to some use.

Example 1.20

Let (an) be the sequence defined so that a1=1, a2=2, and for all n≥3,

an=12⁢(an-1+an-2),

so each subsequent term is generated by averaging the previous two.

Claim: (an) converges.

Proof.

It’s not at all obvious what the limit of this sequence is, so a direct ε—N proof would be a tall order (how do we estimate |an-L| when we don’t know L?). A nice idea would be try to use the Monotone Convergence Theorem, that is, show that (an) is monotone (increasing or decreasing) and bounded. Unfortunately, this sequence isn’t monotone, as you can check by computing the first few terms, so that strategy fails too. Instead, we’ll show that the sequence is Cauchy.

We first show that, for all n∈ℤ+,

|an-an+1|=12n-1. (1.1)

This holds for n=1:

|a1-a2|=|1-2|=1=121-1

Assume it holds for some n=k∈ℤ+. Then, by the definition of (an)

|ak+1-ak+2| = |ak+1-12⁢(ak+1+ak)|
= 12⁢|ak+1-ak|
= 12⁢12k-1=12(k+1)-1

by the induction hypothesis. Hence if (1.1) holds for n=k, it holds for n=k+1. Hence, by induction, (1.1) holds for all n∈ℤ+.

It follows that (an) is Cauchy. Let ε∈(0,∞) be given. We must show that there exists N∈ℤ+ such that, for all n,m≥N, |an-am|<ε. Note that this inequality holds trivially if n=m, and is symmetric under interchange of n and m, so it’s enough to show that there exists N∈ℤ+ such that, for all m>n≥N, |an-am|<ε. Since the sequence 2n is unbounded above, there exists N∈ℤ+ such that, 2N>4/ε. I claim that this N is the one we want. Assume m>n. Then,

|an-am| = |(an-an+1)+(an-1-an-2)+⋯+(am-1-am)|
≤ |an-an+1|+|an+1-an+2|+⋯+|am-1-am|  (by the triangle inequality)
= 12n-1+12n+⋯+12m-2  (by (1.1)
= 12n-1⁢(1+12+122+⋯+12m-n-1)
< 12n-2

since the geometric series ∑n=0∞(1/2)n converges to 2 (if you’ve forgotten why, I’ll remind you in a few weeks). Hence, for all m>n≥N, with 2N>4/ε,

|an-am|<12n-2≤12N-2=42N<ε.

Since (an) is Cauchy, it converges by Theorem 1.19. ∎

1.3 Limits of functions

1.3.1 Limits at infinity

Note it’s not essential in Definition 1.1 that the quantity N is a positive integer. We could just have well decided that

limn→∞⁡an=L if, for each ε>0, there exists K∈ℝ such that, for all n>K, |an-L|<ε.

Both say that, given any positive number ε (no matter how small), there’s a point in the sequence after which all terms lie within distance ε of L. We just use different means (a positive integer N or a real number K) to specify that point.

Having realized this, we see that the definition can be extended immediately to deal with functions of a real variable, not just sequences.

Definition 1.22

Let D⊆ℝ be unbounded above and f:D→ℝ. Then f has limit L at infinity if, for each ε>0, there exists K∈ℝ such that, for all x∈D with x>K, |f⁢(x)-L|<ε. The shorthand for this is limx→∞⁡f⁢(x)=L.

Example 1.23

Let f:ℝ→ℝ, f⁢(x)=(1-x2)/(1+x2). Claim: limx→∞⁡f⁢(x)=-1.

Proof.

Let ε>0 be given. Let K=2ε. Then, for all x>K,

|f⁢(x)-(-1)| = |1-x21+x2+1|
= 21+x2
< 2x2
< 2K2=ε

∎

Again, it’s useful to have a geometric picture of what this means:

Exercise 1.24

Let f:[0,∞)→ℝ such that f⁢(x)=xx+3. Prove that

limx→∞⁡f⁢(x)=0.

The definition says that, given any positive number ε (no matter how small), there’s a real number K such that, for all x>K, f⁢(x) is closer than distance ε from L. This makes precise the informal idea that f⁢(x) gets “arbitrarily close" to L as x gets “sufficiently large." Note we must demand that D, the domain of f, is unbounded above, or else there will exist some real number K such that there are no values of x∈D with x>K. If we allowed this situation, then any real number L would satisfy the definition of limx→∞⁡f⁢(x)!

1.3.2 Cluster points

Limits at infinity are quite useful, but for the purposes of calculus, we really need to make proper sense of limits like

limx→a⁡f⁢(x)

where a is a real number. For example,

f′⁢(a)=limx→a⁡f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)x-a.

Our first task is to clearly define the kind of point a∈ℝ at which it makes sense to define the limit of a given function f. Informally, we know that the limit “as x tends to a" concerns the behaviour of f⁢(x) “sufficiently close" to (but not at) a. Just as limx→∞⁡f⁢(x) only makes sense if the domain of f is unbounded above (so that f⁢(x) is well-defined for x arbitrarily large), limx→a⁡f⁢(x) only makes sense if the domain of f contains points which are “arbitrarily close" to (but different from) a.

Definition 1.26

Let D⊆ℝ. Then a∈ℝ is a cluster point of D if, for each ε>0, there exists x∈D with 0<|x-a|<ε. Equivalently: for each ε>0, the set (D\{a})∩(a-ε,a+ε) is nonempty.

Exercise 1.27

For each of the following subsets of ℝ, write down the set of all cluster points:

A=(0,1],B=ℚ,C={1/n:n∈ℤ+},D=ℤ.

From this we see that it’s possible for a number that is not in a set to be a cluster point of that set (e.g. 0 is a cluster point of (0,1] and 2 is a cluster point of ℚ), and that an element of a set may fail to be a cluster point of the set (e.g. 5 is not a cluster point of ℤ).

We can also characterize cluster points using sequences. This often turns out to be more convenient than the original definition.

Proposition 1.28

a∈ℝ is a cluster point of D⊆ℝ if and only if there is a sequence in D\{a} that converges to a.

Note that the sequence is in D\{a}, that is, it never takes the value a.

1.3.3 Limits of functions: the main definition

Cluster points of the domain of a function are exactly the points at which it makes sense to define a limit of the function.

Definition 1.29

Let D⊆ℝ, f:D→ℝ and a∈ℝ be a cluster point of D. Then f has limit L at a if, for each ε>0, there exists δ>0 such that, for all x∈D with 0<|x-a|<δ, |f⁢(x)-L|<ε. The shorthand for this is limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=L.

Example 1.30

Let f:ℝ→ℝ, f⁢(x)=x2. Claim: limx→2⁡f⁢(x)=4.

Proof.

Let ε>0 be given. Let δ=min⁡{1,ε5}.
Then, for all x∈ℝ with 0<|x-2|<δ,

|f⁢(x)-4| = |x2-4|
= |x+2|⁢|x-2|
≤ 5⁢|x-2|  (since δ≤1 so x∈(1,3))
< 5⁢δ
≤ ε

∎

The definition says that, given any positive number ε (no matter how small), we can find another positive number δ which is so small that, whenever x is within distance δ of a, and different from a, f⁢(x) is within distance ε of L. This makes precise the informal idea that f⁢(x) is “arbitrarily close" to L for all x “sufficiently close" to, but different from, a.

In Example 1.30 and Exercise 1.31, the point a at which we defined the limit was actually in the domain of f (and the limit happened to be f⁢(a)). Limits are more interesting when a is not in the domain of f.

Example 1.32

Let f:ℝ\{-3,3}→ℝ, f⁢(x)=x-3x2-9. Claim: limx→3⁡f⁢(x)=16.

Proof.

Let ε>0 be given. Let δ=min⁡{1,30⁢ε}. Then, for all x∈ℝ\{-3,3} with 0<|x-3|<δ,

|f⁢(x)-16| = |x-3(x-3)⁢(x+3)-16|=|1x+3-16|=|3-x6⁢(x+3)|
≤ 130⁢|x-3|  (since δ≤1 so x∈(2,4) whence x+3>5)
< 130⁢δ
≤ ε

∎

You may be wondering where the heck the number

δ=min⁡{1,30⁢ε}

came from in the above proof. The answer is that, before I started to write the proof down, I did the following rough work: I want to show that I can make f⁢(x) as close as I like to 1/6 by making x sufficiently close to (but different from) 3. So I try to find an upper bound on |f⁢(x)-1/6| in terms of |x-3|:

|f⁢(x)-16| = |x-3(x-3)⁢(x+3)-16|=|1x+3-16|
= |3-x6⁢(x+3)|
= 16⁢1|x+3|⁢|x-3|.

I like the factor of |x-3|: this is the thing I can make as small as I like. The factor of 1/6 is no problem. The factor of 1/|x+3| is not so congenial, however. It can get arbitrarily large – but only if x gets close to -3. So, if I demand that |x-3|<1, for example, then x>2 so |x+3|≥x+3>5. This is helpful, because it forces 1/|x+3|<1/5. So, for all x with 0<|x-3|<1, I know that

|f⁢(x)-16|≤16×5⁢|x-3|.

To make this less than ε, it’s enough to also demand that |x-3|<30⁢ε. So, I need δ≤1 and δ<30⁢ε, hence my choice of δ.

It’s important to understand that there are infinitely many other correct choices of δ. This is the δ that I arrived at by reasoning as above. You may argue differently and come up with something different.

1.3.4 Some basic properties of limits

Since we have a precise definition of limits, we are in a position to prove some basic facts about them.

Theorem 1.33 (Uniqueness of limits)

If f has a limit at a, this limit is unique.

Proof.

Let f:D→ℝ and a be a cluster point of D. Assume, towards a contradiction, that both L1 and L2≠L1 satisfy the definition of limit of f at a. Let ε=|L2-L1|/2>0. Since limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=L1, there exists δ1>0 such that, for all x∈D with 0<|x-a|<δ1, |f⁢(x)-L1|<ε. Similarly, since limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=L2, there exists δ2>0 such that, for all x∈D with 0<|x-a|<δ2, |f⁢(x)-L2|<ε. Let δ=min⁡{δ1,δ2}. Since a is a cluster point of D, there exists x*∈D such that 0<|x*-a|<δ. But then 0<|x*-a|<δ1, so |f⁢(x*)-L1|<ε, and, furthermore, 0<|x*-a|<δ2, so |f⁢(x*)-L2|<ε. Hence

2⁢ε=|L2-L1|=|f⁢(x*)-L1-(f⁢(x*)-L2)|≤|f⁢(x*)-L1|+|f⁢(x*)-L2|<ε+ε

a contradiction. ∎

So it makes sense to talk about the limit of a function at a. Note that this proof made essential use of the condition that a is a cluster point of D, the domain of f (see the text highlighted in red above). Without this assumption, we can’t be sure that the point x*∈D\{a} distance less than δ from a actually exists.

We can also establish that limits of functions obey some basic arithmetic rules:

Theorem 1.34 (Algebra of Limits)

Let a be a cluster point of D, f:D→ℝ, g:D→ℝ, limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=L and limx→a⁡g⁢(x)=K. Then

  1. (i)
    ​

    limx→a⁡(f⁢(x)+g⁢(x))=L+K,

  2. (ii)
    ​

    limx→a⁡f⁢(x)⁢g⁢(x)=L⁢K,

  3. (iii)
    ​

    limx→a⁡1f⁢(x)=1L if, in addition, 0∉f⁢(D) and L≠0.

We could give direct ε—δ proofs of each part of this theorem. They would be structurally very similar to the analogous proofs of Proposition 1.9 (the Algebra of Limits for sequences). This is actually quite a good exercise, and I encourage you to have a go. However, we’re going to prove Theorem 1.34 by exploiting a sneaky and very powerful trick: we can reformulat limits of functions entirely in terms of convergence of sequences:

Theorem 1.35

Let a be a cluster point of D and f:D→ℝ. Then limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=L if and only if, for all sequences (xn) in D\{a} such that xn→a, f⁢(xn)→L.

Proof.

We must prove two things:

  1. (i)
    ​

    If limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=L then, for all sequences (xn) in D\{a} such that xn→a, f⁢(xn)→L, and

  2. (ii)
    ​

    If for all sequences (xn) in D\{a} such that xn→a, f⁢(xn)→L, then limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=L.

(i) Assume limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=L and let (xn) be any sequence in D\{a} such that xn→a. We must show that f⁢(xn)→L. So, let ε>0 be given. By assumption, there exists δ>0 such that, for all x∈D with 0<|x-a|<δ, |f⁢(x)-L|<ε. But xn→a, and δ is a positive number, so there exists N∈ℤ+ such that, for all n≥N, |xn-a|<δ. Furthermore, xn∈D\{a}, so for all n, |xn-a|>0. Hence, for all n≥N, 0<|xn-a|<δ. But then (by the definition of δ), for all n≥N, |f⁢(xn)-L|<ε. Since ε>0 was arbitrary, it follows that f⁢(xn)→L.

(ii) We will prove the contrapositive: if f does not have limit L at a, then there exists a sequence (xn) in D\{a} such that xn→a but f⁢(xn)↛L. So, assume limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=L is false. This means precisely that there exists ε>0 such that, for all δ>0, there is some x∈D with 0<|x-a|<δ such that |f⁢(x)-L|≥ε. Since this holds for each and every δ>0, it holds, in particular, if δ=1/n, where n∈ℤ+. That is, for each n∈ℤ+, there exists xn∈D with 0<|xn-a|<1/n such that |f⁢(xn)-L|≥ε. Now xn→a (by the Squeeze Rule), and xn∈D\{a} (since 0<|xn-a|), but |f⁢(xn)-L|≥ε for all n so certainly f⁢(xn)↛L. ∎

In the course of the proof of part (ii), we had to formulate carefully what it means to say that f does not have limit L at a. That is, we had to negate the statement

limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=L.

Negating statements with several quantifiers (“for all…", “there exists…") is a subtle business, so if this part of the proof wasn’t clear to you, it may help to watch this [VIDEO]

Armed with Theorem 1.35, we can now make short work of proving Theorem 1.34 – it follows almost immediately from Proposition 1.9:

Proof of Theorem 1.34:  Let (xn) be any sequence in D\{a} converging to a. Since limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=L, f⁢(xn)→L by Theorem 1.35. Since limx→a⁡g⁢(x)=K, g⁢(xn)→L by Theorem 1.35. Hence

  1. (i)
    ​

    f⁢(xn)+g⁢(xn)→L+K by Proposition 1.9(i) and

  2. (ii)
    ​

    f⁢(xn)⁢g⁢(xn)→L⁢K by Proposition 1.9(ii).

This holds for any such sequence (xn) so f⁢(x)+g⁢(x) has limit L+K at a, and f⁢(x)⁢g⁢(x) has limit L⁢K at a by Theorem 1.35.

If, in addition, f⁢(x)≠0 for all x∈D and L≠0, then f⁢(xn)≠0 for all n, so

  • (iii)
    ​

    1/f⁢(xn)→1/L by Proposition 1.9(iii).

This holds for any such sequence (xn) so 1/f⁢(x) has limit 1/L at a by Theorem 1.35. □

Note that this proof used both directions of Theorem 1.35: since limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=L we know that f⁢(xn)→L (the “only if" direction), and since f⁢(xn)+g⁢(xn)→L+K we know that limx→a⁡(f⁢(x)+g⁢(x))=L+K (the “if" direction).

1.3.5 Continuity and limits

You may have been told (by some shameless charlatan) that a function is continuous “if you can draw its graph without taking your pen off the paper." It should by now be clear that this is not an acceptable mathematical definition. What if you have no pen, or paper, or hands? What if you’re simply not very good at drawing? Does the continuity of a function depend on your skill as a draughtsman? Clearly this is utter piffle. A mathematical definition should be formulated only in terms of the object being defined, not how we are able to interact with (or think about) it.

Luckily, we can use convergence of sequences to give a precise (and rather elegant) definition of continuity for functions:

Definition 1.37

Let f:D→ℝ and a∈D. Then f is continuous at a if, for all sequences (xn) in D such that xn→a, f⁢(xn)→f⁢(a). f is continuous if it is continuous at a for all a∈D. If f is not continuous, it is discontinuous.

For example, all polynomial functions

f:ℝ→ℝ,f⁢(x)=a0+a1⁢x+⋯+ak⁢xk

are continuous. This follows from the Algebra of Limits for sequences and the rather obvious fact that f⁢(x)=x is continuous.22 2 Proof: If xn→a then f⁢(xn)=xn→a=f⁢(a).

Example 1.38

A simple example of a discontinuous function is a “step function":

f:ℝ→ℝ,f⁢(x)={0x<01x≥0

To see this, note that the sequence xn=-1/n→0, but f⁢(xn)=f⁢(-1/n)=0, which does not converge to f⁢(0)=1.

A step function is discontinuous at a single point. Somewhat counterintuitively, it’s possible for a function f:ℝ→ℝ to be continuous at only a single point:

Example 1.39

Let f:ℝ→ℝ be such that f⁢(x)=x if x∈ℚ and f⁢(x)=0 if x∉ℚ. Claim: f is continuous as 0 and discontinuous everywhere else.

Proof.

Assume that f is continuous at a. Let (rn) be any rational sequence converging to a. Then f⁢(rn)=rn→a. Let (in) be any irrational sequence converging to a. Then f⁢(in)=0→0. But f is continuous at a, so f⁢(rn)→f⁢(a) and f⁢(in)→f⁢(a). Hence f⁢(a)=a and f⁢(a)=0, that is a=0. So if f is continuous at a then a=0. That is, f is discontinuous at every a≠0.

It remains to show that f is continuous at 0 (careful: we have not proved this yet!). Let (xn) be any sequence converging to 0. Then

|f⁢(xn)|={|xn|,if xn∈ℚ,0,if xn∉ℚ,}≤|xn|→0

so f⁢(xn)→0=f⁢(0) by the Squeeze Rule. Hence f is continuous at 0. ∎

Here’s an even more counterintuitive example: a function f:ℝ→ℝ which is continuous at every irrational number, but discontinuous at every rational number! It was invented by Carl Johannes Thomae but, in the anglosphere, is usually referred to by the (somewhat mysterious) name given to it by John Horton Conway:

Example 1.40 (Stars Over Babylon)

Let f:ℝ→ℝ be the function which maps every irrational number to 0, and maps each rational number p/q, expressed in lowest terms (that is, p and q have no common divisors other than 1), to 1/q. So f⁢(2)=0, f⁢(3/12)=f⁢(1/4)=1/4 and f⁢(0)=f⁢(0/1)=1, for example.

Claim: f is continuous at every a∈ℝ\ℚ, and discontinuous at every a∈ℚ.

See if you can prove this. Discontinuity at a∈ℚ is much easier than continuity at a∈ℝ\ℚ. If you get stuck, watch this [VIDEO]

It’s not hard to show that every function f:D→ℝ is automatically continuous at a if a is not a cluster point of D.

Proposition 1.41

Let f:D→ℝ and assume a∈D is not a cluster point of D. Then f is continuous at a.

Proof.

Let (xn) be any sequence in D converging to a. Since a is not a cluster point of D, there exists ε>0 such that no element of D has 0<|x-a|<ε. Hence, if |x-a|<ε and x∈D, then x=a. Now xn→a, so there exists N∈ℤ+ such that, for all n≥N, |xn-a|<ε and hence xn=a. Hence, for all n≥N, f⁢(xn)=f⁢(a), that is, the sequence (f⁢(xn)) is constant and equals f⁢(a), for all n≥N. Hence f⁢(xn)→f⁢(a). ∎

So in considering whether a function is continuous, we may restrict attention only to cluster points of (and in) its domain. For such points we have two useful ways to reformulate continuity:

Theorem 1.42

Let f:D→ℝ, a∈D and a be a cluster point of D. Then the following are equivalent:

  1. (i)
    ​

    f is continuous at a;

  2. (ii)
    ​

    limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=f⁢(a);

  3. (iii)
    ​

    For each ε>0 there exists δ>0 such that, for all x∈D with |x-a|<δ, |f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)|<ε.

Proof.

(i) ⇒ (ii) follows immediately from Theorem 1.35.

(ii) ⇒ (iii): assume limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=f⁢(a) and let ε>0 be given. Then (by Definition 1.29) there exists δ>0 such that, for all x∈D with 0<|x-a|<δ, |f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)|<ε. Clearly, if x=a then |f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)|=0<ε. Hence, for all x∈D with |x-a|<δ, |f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)|<ε.

(iii) ⇒ (i): assume (iii) holds, and let (xn) be any sequence in D converging to a. We must show that f⁢(xn)→L. So, let ε>0 be given. By assumption, there exists δ>0 such that, for all x∈D with |x-a|<δ, |f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)|<ε. Now xn→a and δ>0, so there exists N∈ℤ+ such that, for all n≥N, |xn-a|<δ. But then, for all n≥N, |f⁢(xn)-f⁢(a)|<ε, by definition of δ. Hence f⁢(xn)→f⁢(a). Since this holds for any sequence (xn) in D converging to a, f is continuous at a. ∎

Many mathematicians take condition (iii) in Theorem 1.42 as the definition of continuity at a, instead of the sequential definition, Definition 1.37. It’s important to be able to use both. Here’s an example which demonstrates that the whole “continuous if you can draw its graph without taking your pen off the paper" pseudo-definition isn’t just embarrassingly jejeune, it’s also plain wrong.

Example 1.43

Let f:ℝ\{0}→ℝ, f⁢(x)=1x. Claim: f is continuous.33 3 Try drawing the graph of that without taking your pen off the paper!

Proof.

We must show that f is continuous at a for every a≠0. To do this , we will use Theorem 1.42, that is, show that f,a satisfy the ε—δ criterion of part (iii).

So, choose and fix a≠0 and let some positive number ε be given. We must show that there exists δ>0 such that, for all x∈ℝ\{0} with |x-a|<δ, |f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)|<ε. Now

|f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)| = |1x-1a|=|x-a||a|⁢|x| (1.2)

So first let’s insist that δ≤|a|/2. Then, for all x∈(a-δ,a+δ), |x|≥|a|/2, and hence

1|x|≤2|a|

Given this, we see from (1.2) that, to ensure |f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)|<ε, it suffices to require that |x-a|<|a|2⁢ε/2. We may now write out the argument explicitly.

Given any ε>0, let δ=min⁡{|a|/2,|a|2⁢ε/2}>0. Then for all x∈ℝ\{0} with |x-a|<δ,

|f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)| = |x-a||a|⁢|x|
≤ 2|a|2⁢|x-a|  (since |x|>|a|-δ≥|a|/2)
< 2a2⁢δ
≤ ε.

Hence, by Theorem 1.42, f is continuous at a. Since a∈ℝ\{0} was arbitrary, f is continuous. ∎

This kind of argument is called an ε—δ proof of continuity. Often an argument making direct use of Definition 1.37 is simpler.

Here’s another result which follows very quickly from the sequential definition of continuity (the composition of two continuous functions is continuous):

Theorem 1.45

Let D and E be subsets of ℝ, f:D→E, g:E→ℝ, f be continuous at a and g be continuous at f⁢(a). Then g∘f:D→ℝ is continuous at a.

Proof.

We will show directly that g∘f:D→ℝ, (g∘f)⁢(x)=g⁢(f⁢(x)) satisfies Definition 1.37. So, let (xn) be any sequence in D converging to a. Since f is continuous at a, yn:=f⁢(xn)→f⁢(a). Hence, since g is continuous at f⁢(a), g⁢(yn)→g⁢(f⁢(a)). Hence g⁢(f⁢(xn))→g⁢(f⁢(a)). ∎

We finish this chapter by recalling two famous and important theorems about continuous functions on a closed, bounded interval,

[a,b]={x∈ℝ:a≤x≤b}.
Theorem 1.47 (Intermediate Value Theorem)

Let f:[a,b]→ℝ be continuous and y be any number between f⁢(a) and f⁢(b). Then there exists c∈[a,b] such that f⁢(c)=y.

Theorem 1.48 (Extreme Value Theorem)

Let f:[a,b]→ℝ be continuous. Then f is bounded (above and below) and attains both a minimum and a maximum value.

These two Theorems may look obvious, but it’s important to realize that we can (and, in MATH1026, did) prove them rigorously from Definitions 1.37 and 1.1. The whole point of Real Analysis is to go beyond intuitive, hand waving reasoning, to back up all our assertions with precise, rigorous reasoning. After all, as we shall see, not everything that is “obvious" is true!
