7.1 Convergence tests for series

We begin with a brisk review of the basics concerning convergence of series. All this material was covered in detail in MATH1026. (The main ideas were covered in MATH1055, albeit with some of the proofs omitted.)

Recall that a series is a formal infinite sum

∑n=0∞an=a0+a1+a2+⋯,

that its kth partial sum is the real number

sk:=∑n=0kan=a0+a1+⋯+ak,

and that the series converges precisely if the sequence (sk) converges (in the sense of Definition 1.1). In this case, we also use ∑n=0∞an to denote its limit.

The series converges absolutely if

∑n=0∞|an|

converges. Absolute convergence implies convergence, but convergence does not imply absolute convergence. For example, the alternating harmonic series

∑n=1∞(-1)nn

converges (see Example 3.23), but it does not converge absolutely since the harmonic series

∑n=1∞1n

diverges.

If a series ∑n=0∞an converges then its sequence of terms (an) converges to 0. This fact is often called the Divergence Test since we can apply its contrapositive: if an↛0 then ∑an diverges. Note that the converse is false, as illustrated by the harmonic series: the sequence of terms an=1/n→0, but the corresponding series ∑n=1∞an diverges.

If all the terms of a series are positive, an>0, then the sequence of partial sums sk=∑n=0kan is an increasing sequence,

sk+1=sk+ak+1>sk,

so, by the Monotone Convergence Theorem, the series converges if (and only if) sk is bounded above. This observation allows one to establish several useful convergence tests applicable to the case an>0. The first allows us to prove convergence (or divergence) of a series ∑an by comparing it with a (simpler) series ∑bn that we already know converges (or diverges).

Theorem 7.1 (The Comparison Test)

Let an>0 and bn>0.

  1. (i)
    ​

    If an/bn is bounded above and ∑bn converges, then ∑an converges.

  2. (ii)
    ​

    If bn/an is bounded above and ∑bn diverges, then ∑an diverges.

To use the comparison test effectively, you need a stock of simple example series whose convergence/divergence you’ve already established. Here’s a useful and simple family of examples:

Our next convergence test is perhaps the most useful – as well as being the easiest to use!

Theorem 7.5 (The Ratio Test)

Let an>0 for all n and an+1/an→L. Then

  1. (i)
    ​

    if L<1, the series ∑an converges,

  2. (ii)
    ​

    if L>1, the series ∑an diverges.

Example 7.6

Claim: ∑n=1∞n50⁢(4950)n converges.

Proof.

Let an=n50⁢(49/50)n>0, and note that

an+1an=(n+1)50n50⁢4950=(1+1n)50⁢4950→4950<1.

Hence, by the Ratio Test, ∑n=1∞an converges. ∎

Note that if the terms an of your series aren’t all positive, you can always try applying the Comparison or Ratio Test to ∑|an| to determine whether ∑an converge absolutely. If it does, it certainly converges. Discarding all information about the sign of an does lose us potentially vital knowledge, however. If the terms alternate in sign, we have a particularly neat convergence test:

Theorem 7.7 (The Alternating Series Test)

Let (an) be a positive, decreasing sequence that converges to 0. Then

∑n=1∞(-1)n+1⁢an

converges.

Example 7.8

The series ∑n=1∞(-1)n+1⁢1n converges, by the Alternating Series Test, since an=1/n is positive, decreasing and converges to 0. As usual with convergence tests, we’ve proved that the series converges but have no idea what its limit is.

7.2 Power series and their radius of convergence

A power series is a series of the form

∑n=0∞an⁢xn

where x is interpreted as a real variable. The series depends on the choice of value for x∈ℝ. The terms of the series are an⁢xn, and the kt⁢h partial sum is the polynomial function

fk⁢(x)=∑n=0kan⁢xn=a0+a1⁢x+a2⁢x2+⋯+ak⁢xk,

just as for series in general. Note that, if x=0, then every partial sum is fk⁢(0)=a0, so the series certainly converges (to a0) in that case. In general, a power series may converge for some values of x but diverge for others. If the series converges for a particular choice of x, its limit will, in general, depend on x. So a power series defines a real-valued function on some subset (perhaps all of) ℝ. The following example is fundamental (and, hopefully, familiar).

Example 7.9 (Geometric series)

Claim: The series

∑n=0∞xn

converges to 1/(1-x) if |x|<1, and diverges otherwise.

Proof.

If x=1, the k-th partial sum is sk=k+1, and this sequence clearly diverges. If x≠1 then

sk = 1+x+x2+⋯⁢xk
⇒ x⁢sk = x+x2+x3+⋯+xk+1
⇒ (1-x)⁢sk = 1-xk+1
⇒ sk = 1-xk+11-x.

The sequence xk converges to 0 if |x|<1 and diverges if x=-1. Hence, sk→1/(1-x) if |x|<1 and diverges otherwise. ∎

So if we use the power series to define a function

f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞xn

then f:D→ℝ where D=(-1,1) and for all x∈D,

f⁢(x)=11-x.
Definition 7.10

The radius of convergence of a power series ∑n=0∞an⁢xn is

R:=sup{|x|:∑n=0∞|an⁢xn| converges}.

If this set is unbounded above, we say that R=∞.

The radius of convergence of a power series tells us almost everything about the subset of ℝ on which it converges:

Theorem 7.11

Let a power series have radius of convergence R>0. Then the series converges absolutely for |x|<R and diverges for |x|>R.

This Theorem follows quite quickly once we have proved the following Lemma:

Lemma 7.12

Let ∑n=0∞an⁢xn converge at x=x1. Then it converges absolutely for all x such that |x|<|x1|.

Proof.

Since ∑an⁢x1n converges, an⁢x1n→0 by the Divergence Test, and so is bounded: there exists K>0 such that for all n |an⁢x1n|<K. But then for all n,

|an⁢xn|=|an⁢x1n|⁢|x|n|x1|n<K⁢(|x||x1|)n

so if |x|<|x1|, ∑n=0∞|an⁢xn| converges by comparison with the convergent (geometric) series ∑n=0∞(|x|/|x1|)n. ∎

Proof of Theorem 7.11:  Let A:={|x|:∑n=0∞|anxn| converges}⊂ℝ, so that R=sup⁡A.

If |x|<R then there exists x1 with |x|<|x1|<R such that ∑n=0∞an⁢x1n is absolutely convergent (else R isn’t the least upper bound on A, |x| being smaller), hence convergent, so ∑n=0∞an⁢xn is absolutely convergent by Lemma 7.12.

If |x|>R and ∑n=0∞an⁢xn converges then ∑n=0∞an⁢x2n, where x2=(|x|+R)/2, converges absolutely by Lemma 7.12, since |x2|<|x|. But |x2|>R, a contradiction (R isn’t an upper bound on A at all!), so we conclude that ∑n=0∞an⁢xn diverges. □

So the series converges on (-R,R) and diverges on (-∞,-R)∪(R,∞). The only information not revealed by Theorem 7.11 is whether the series converges at x=±R. So, it’s important to be able to compute radii of convergence. Luckily this can usually be achieved with a simple application of the Ratio Test.

Example 7.13

Claim: ∑n=0∞n⁢x3⁢n+1n2+1 has radius of convergence R=1.

Proof.

Let bn=|n⁢x3⁢n+1/(n2+1)|>0 and note that

bn+1bn = |(n+1)⁢x3⁢n+4|n2+2⁢n+3×n2+1|n⁢x3⁢n+1|        
= (n+1)⁢(n2+1)⁢|x|3(n2+2⁢n+3)⁢n
= (1+1/n)⁢(1+1/n2)1+2/n+3/n2⁢|x|3
→ |x|3.

Hence, by the Ratio Test, the power series converges absolutely if |x|<1, but not if |x|>1. Comparing with Definition 7.10, we see that R=1. ∎

It follows that this series converges absolutely on (-1,1) and diverges on ℝ\[-1,1] (Theorem 7.11).

7.3 Uniform convergence of series of functions

In the language of chapter 6, Theorem 7.11 establishes that the sequence of polynomial functions

fk⁢(x)=∑n=0kan⁢xn

converges pointwise on (-R,R) to some function f⁢(x), which we denote

f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn.

As we learned, pointwise convergence is a rather weak property, from which we can’t usually deduce much about the limit function (e.g. it may be discontinuous, even if each function in the sequence is continuous). We can deduce much more about the limit function if we can establish that the convergence is actually uniform. Recall also that we can show that a sequence of functions converges uniformly even if we have no information about its limit function by showing that the sequence is uniformly Cauchy.

In this section we will develop a test which allows us to establish quickly and easily that a series of functions is uniformly Cauchy, and hence uniformly convergent. We will state the test for general series of functions, not just polynomials, because there are many other useful choices, for example, Fourier series

∑n=1∞an⁢sin⁡n⁢x.
Theorem 7.16 (The Weierstrass M Test)

Let gn:D→ℝ be a sequence of functions and, Mn a sequence of non-negative real numbers such that

  1. (i)
    ​

    for all x∈D, |gn⁢(x)|≤Mn, and

  2. (ii)
    ​

    the series ∑n=0∞Mn converges.

Then the sequence of functions

fk:D→ℝ,fk⁢(x)=∑n=0kgn⁢(x)

converges uniformly.

Proof.

We will prove that (fk) is uniformly Cauchy. Let ε>0 be given. By assumption, the sequence

sk=∑n=0kMn

converges, so is Cauchy (Theorem 1.19). Hence, there exists N∈ℤ+ such that for all k>l≥N, |sk-sl|<ε/2. Since Mn≥0 for all n,

|sk-sl|=sk-sl=∑n=l+1kMn.

Hence, for all k>l≥N,

∑n=l+1kMn<ε2.

But, for all x∈D, and all n, |gn⁢(x)|≤Mn. Hence, for all x∈D, and all k>l≥N

|fk⁢(x)-fl⁢(x)| = |∑n=0kgn⁢(x)-∑n=0lgn⁢(x)|
= |∑n=l+1kgn⁢(x)|
≤ ∑n=l+1k|gn⁢(x)|
≤ ∑n=l+1kMn
< ε2.

Hence, for all k>l≥N,

∥fk-fl∥ = sup{|fk(x)-fl(x)|:x∈D}≤ε2<ε.

So (fk) is uniformly Cauchy and hence uniformly convergent by Theorem 6.26. ∎

Returning to our main focus of study, it would be nice if we could use the M test to prove that power series converge uniformly on (-R,R), where R is the radius of convergence. In fact, that’s a bit too much to hope for, as we can deduce by thinking about the geometric series

∑n=1∞xn.

This has R=1 (as we showed directly in Example 7.9). If it converged uniformly its limit function f:(-1,1)→ℝ would perforce be bounded. But we showed that

f⁢(x)=11-x

which grows unbounded above as x approaches 1. So we deduce that this series does not converge uniformly on (-1,1). Nil desperandum! We can prove something almost as good as uniform convergence on (-R,R):

Theorem 7.18

Let the power series ∑n=1∞an⁢xn have radius of convergence R>0 and ρ be any constant in (0,R). Then the series converges uniformly on [-ρ,ρ].

Proof.

We apply the Weierstrass M Test with gn⁢(x)=an⁢xn, D=[-ρ,ρ] and Mn=|an|⁢ρn. Certainly, for all x∈[-ρ,ρ]

|gn⁢(x)|=|an|⁢|x|n≤|an|⁢ρn=Mn

so condition (i) is satisfied. Since |ρ|<R, the series converges absolutely at x=ρ, that is

∑n=0∞Mn=∑n=0∞|an|⁢ρn

converges, by Theorem 7.11. Hence condition (ii) is satisfied also. So the series converges uniformly on [-ρ,ρ] by the Weierstrass M Test (Theorem 7.16). ∎

Each of the partial sums of a power series is a polynomial, and hence is continuous. It follows from Theorems 6.14 and 7.18 that the limit function

f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn

is continuous on [-ρ,ρ] for every ρ∈(0,R). But that implies that f is continuous on (-R,R) since, at every point a∈(-R,R) we can choose ρ=(a+R)/2, say (or any other ρ∈(a,R)). So we immediately deduce:

Corollary 7.19

Let the power series ∑n=1∞an⁢xn have radius of convergence R>0. Then this series converges to a continuous function f:(-R,R)→ℝ.

Excellent! But we want (much) more: we’re actually going to prove that the limit function is smooth (that is, infinitely differentiable) on (-R,R).

7.4 Differentiability of power series

Given a power series f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn of radius of convergence R, we know that f is a continuous function on (-R,R). Is f differentiable on (-R,R)? If it is, what is f′? Your first guess is probably

f′⁢(x)=∑n=1∞n⁢an⁢xn-1,

the series obtained by differentiating f term by term. Actually, this turns out to be absolutely correct, but proving it is not entirely trivial. Note that, in writing down our formula for f′ we have swapped the order of two limits (the limit defining the derivative, and the limit defining the series). We need to justify this. Also, how do we know that the proposed power series for f′⁢(x) even converges on (-R,R)? Maybe its radius of convergence is less than R? Given the growth given by the extra factor of n, this seems like a genuine worry.

So, our first task will be to prove that a power series and the obvious candidate for its derivative always have the same radius of convergence. Since we will use this “obvious candidate" series a lot, it’s helpful to develop some notation and a name for it.

Definition 7.20

Given a power series f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn, its termwise derivative is the power series

f^⁢(x)=∑n=1∞n⁢an⁢xn-1=∑n=0∞(n+1)⁢an+1⁢xn.

Careful! At the moment, termwise derivative is just a name: we haven’t (yet) established that f^ is the derivative of f.

Lemma 7.21

The power series f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn and its termwise derivative
f^⁢(x)=∑n=1∞n⁢an⁢xn-1 have the same radius of convergence.

You can watch a [VIDEO] of me proving this..

We define the following subsets of [0,∞),

A={|x|:∑|an⁢xn| converges},B={|x|:∑|n⁢an⁢xn-1| converges},

and recall that sup⁡A and sup⁡B are the radii of convergence of f and f^, respectively. We will prove that

  • (i)
    ​

    for all x with |x|<sup⁡B, f⁢(x) converges absolutely (so |x|∈A, and hence sup⁡A≥sup⁡B), and

  • (ii)
    ​

    for all x with |x|<sup⁡A, f^⁢(x) converges absolutely (so |x|∈B, and hence sup⁡B≥sup⁡A).

It follows immediately that sup⁡A=sup⁡B, which is what we seek to prove.

(i) Let x∈ℝ with |x|<sup⁡B. Then sk=∑n=1k|n⁢an⁢xn-1| converges (Theorem 7.11), and hence is bounded above, and

tk=∑n=0k|an⁢xn|=|a0|+|x|⁢∑n=1k|an⁢xn-1|≤|a0|+|x|⁢sk.

So (tk) is increasing and bounded above, and hence converges, by the Monotone Convergence Theorem. Hence, |x|∈A.

(ii) Let x∈ℝ with |x|<sup⁡A. If x=0 then f^⁢(x) certainly converges, so we may assume |x|>0. Choose ρ∈(|x|,sup⁡A). Then tk=∑n=0k|an|⁢ρn converges (Theorem 7.11), and hence is bounded above. Now

sk=∑n=1k|n⁢an⁢xn-1|=|x|-1⁢∑n=1kn⁢(|x|ρ)n⁢|an|⁢ρn.

We know that the sequence n⁢(|x|/ρ)n→0, since (|x|/ρ)<1 (exponentials beat polynomials), so it must be bounded, that is, there exists K>0 such that, for all n, n⁢(|x|/ρ)n≤K. But then

sk≤|x|-1⁢∑n=0kK⁢|an|⁢ρn=K|x|⁢tk.

Hence, sk is increasing and bounded above, so converges, by the Monotone Convergence Theorem. Hence, |x|∈B. ∎

We now show that the termwise derivative f^⁢(x) really is the derivative of f⁢(x). We do this by applying Theorem 6.19 to the sequence of partial sums of f⁢(x). It’s a good idea to read the statement of Theorem 6.19 carefully before attempting to understand the next proof.

Theorem 7.22

Suppose that f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn has radius of convergence R>0. Then f is differentiable on (-R,R), and

f′⁢(x)=f^⁢(x):=∑n=1∞n⁢an⁢xn-1.
Proof.

Choose and fix ρ∈(0,R), and consider the sequence of functions

fk:[-ρ,ρ]→ℝ,fk⁢(x)=∑n=0kan⁢xn.

This is a sequence of continuously differentiable functions which converges pointwise to f (Theorem 7.11), and its sequence of derivatives

fk′:[-ρ,ρ]→ℝ,fk′⁢(x)=∑n=1kn⁢an⁢xn-1

converges uniformly to f^ (Lemma 7.21 and Theorem 7.18). Hence, by Theorem 6.19, f is differentiable and f′=f^. This holds for all x∈[-ρ,ρ], but ρ∈(0,R) was arbitrary, so holds for all x∈(-R,R). ∎

This is the most important theorem in this chapter. It says that the derivative of a power series exists on its open interval of convergence and is just the power series obtained by termwise differentiation.

Since f′⁢(x) is also a power series with radius of convergence R, we can apply Theorem 7.22 to f′⁢(x) and deduce that f:(-R,R)→ℝ is actually twice differentiable. Further, f′′⁢(x) also has radius of convergence R, so is differentiable on (-R,R), that is, f is three times differentiable. In fact, we can keep applying Theorem 7.22 as often as we like, and we conclude that f is smooth:

Corollary 7.23

Let f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn have radius convergence R>0. Then f:(-R,R)→ℝ is a smooth function, and its kt⁢h derivative is given by the power series

f(k)⁢(x)=∑n=k∞n!(n-k)!⁢an⁢xn-k,

which also has radius of convergence R. In particular,

ak=f(k)⁢(0)k!

for all k≥0.

Proof.

For each integer k≥0, define

gk⁢(x)=∑n=0∞(n+k)!n!⁢an+k⁢xn.

I claim that each power series gk⁢(x) has radius of convergence R and that, for all x∈(-R,R), f(k)⁢(x)=gk⁢(x). We prove this by induction on k.

Certainly the claim holds for k=0, since g0=f. So, assume that the claim holds for some value k≥0, and consider gk+1. Defining coefficients bn=(n+k)!n!⁢an+k so that gk⁢(x)=∑n=0∞bn⁢xn, then

gk+1⁢(x) = ∑n=0∞(n+k+1)!n!⁢an+k+1⁢xn=∑m=1∞(m+k)!(m-1)!⁢am+k⁢xm-1
= ∑m=1∞m⁢bm⁢xm-1.

Hence, by Lemma 7.21, gk+1 has the same radius of convergence as gk, and by Theorem 7.22, gk+1⁢(x) coincides with the derivative of gk. But, by our induction hypothesis, gk has radius of convergence R and coincides with f(k), so gk+1 has radius of convergence R and coincides with f(k+1). Hence, if the claim holds for some k≥0, it also holds for k+1. Hence, by induction, the claim holds for all integers k≥0.

It follows that f(k)⁢(0)=gk⁢(0)=k!⁢ak, which completes the proof. ∎

Corollary 7.23 applies to any power series with non-zero radius of convergence so, in particular, it applies to the series defining exp, sin, and cos.

Proposition 7.24

The functions exp:ℝ→ℝ, sin:ℝ→ℝ, cos:ℝ→ℝ defined in Definition 7.14 are smooth, and their derivatives are

exp′=exp,sin′=cos,cos′=-sin.
Proof.

That these functions are smooth follows immediately from Corollary 7.23. Furthermore, by Theorem 7.22

exp′⁡(x)=∑n=1∞n⁢(1n!)⁢xn-1=∑n=1∞xn-1(n-1)!=∑m=0∞xmm!=exp⁡(x)

for all x∈ℝ, so exp′=exp. The formulae for sin′ and cos′ follow from similar arguments. ∎

With a bit of ingenuity, we can often use power series to exactly sum (that is, exactly compute the limit of) a given series. Here’s a simple example.

Example 7.25

Claim: ∑n=1∞n7n=736.

Proof.

The geometric series f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞xn has radius of convergence R=1 and, for all x∈(-1,1),

f⁢(x)=11-x.

Hence, by Theorem 7.22, the power series

∑n=1∞n⁢xn-1

also has radius of convergence R=1 and, for all x∈(-1,1) converges to

f′⁢(x)=1(1-x)2.

Evaluating this at x=1/7 yields

∑n=1∞n7n-1=f′⁢(1/7)=(76)2.

Hence

∑n=1∞n7n=17⁢∑n=1∞n7n-1=736.

∎

7.5 Properties of the exponential function

In this section we will prove that the function exp:ℝ→ℝ, defined as a power series, has all the properties you’re familiar with. We begin with the following fundamental identity.

Lemma 7.27

For all x,y∈ℝ, exp⁡(x+y)=exp⁡(x)⁢exp⁡(y).

Proof.

For each fixed number b∈ℝ, define the function f:ℝ→ℝ,

f⁢(x)=exp⁡(x)⁢exp⁡(b-x).

This is a product of differentiable functions, so is differentiable, and by the Product and Chain Rules, and Proposition 7.24,

f′⁢(x)=exp′⁡(x)⁢exp⁡(b-x)-exp⁡(x)⁢exp′⁡(b-x)=0.

Since f′⁢(x)=0 for all x in the interval ℝ, it follows that f is constant (Proposition 3.11). Hence, for all x∈ℝ and any b∈ℝ, f⁢(x)=f⁢(b), that is,

exp⁡(x)⁢exp⁡(b-x)=exp⁡(b).

Applying this in the case where b=x+y establishes the claim. ∎

Remark Using similar arguments, it’s quite straightforward to prove trigonometric identities such as

cos2⁡x+sin2⁡x=1.

Just define f⁢(x)=cos2⁡x+sin2⁡x and show that f′⁢(x)=0 for all x, whence f is constant (Proposition 3.11), and observe that f⁢(0)=1.

sin⁡(x+y) = sin⁡x⁢cos⁡y+cos⁡x⁢sin⁡y.

Lemma 7.27 explains why exp⁡(x) is often denoted ex and thought of as the constant e=exp⁡(1)=∑n=0∞1n! “raised to the power x”. The point is that, written in this alternative notation, Lemma 7.27 looks like one of the standard algebraic rules of integer exponents:

ex+y=ex×ey  in analogy with  an+m=an×am.

This analogy, and the associated notation, are a useful mnemonic, but it’s important to realize that they are just that, an analogy. We don’t literally define exp⁡(x) to be the irrational number e “raised to the power x”, for obvious reasons: what on earth does it mean to raise e to the power 2, for example? In fact, the logic works in the opposite direction. Having proved that the function exp, defined as a power series, satisfies a rule analogous to the behaviour of integer (and rational) exponents, we define ex, for any exponent x (rational or irrational) to be exp⁡(x).

Proposition 7.28

For all x∈ℝ, exp⁡(x)>0.

Proof.

We first note that, for all x∈ℝ, exp⁡(x)≠0 since, if there exists x∈ℝ such that exp⁡(x)=0, then Lemma 7.27 in the case y=-x implies that

1=exp⁡(0)=0×exp⁡(-x)=0.

Now exp⁡(0)=1>0, and exp is differentiable, hence continuous, so it follows from the Intermediate Value Theorem that exp⁡(x)>0 for all x∈ℝ (if exp⁡(x)<0 then there exists c between x and 0 such that exp⁡(c)=0). ∎

So exp:ℝ→(0,∞). I claim that exp is bijective and that ln:(0,∞)→ℝ is its inverse function. Recall (Definition 5.8) that

ln:(0,∞)→ℝ,ln⁡x:=∫1x1t⁢𝑑t.
Proposition 7.29

exp:ℝ→(0,∞) is bijective and ln:(0,∞)→ℝ is its inverse function.

Proof.

We must show that, for all x∈ℝ, ln⁡(exp⁡(x))=x, and for all y∈(0,∞), exp⁡(ln⁡y)=y. Consider the function

f:ℝ→ℝ,f⁢(x)=ln⁡(exp⁡(x))-x.

By the Chain Rule,

f′⁢(x)=exp⁡(x)exp⁡(x)-1=0

for all x, so f is constant (Corollary 3.11). Hence, for all x, f⁢(x)=f⁢(0)=ln⁡1-0=0, that is, ln⁡(exp⁡(x))=x. Now, for all y∈(0,∞),

ln⁡(exp⁡(ln⁡y))=ln⁡y,

as we have just shown. But ln is injective, so if ln⁡z=ln⁡y then z=y. Hence exp⁡(ln⁡y)=y, as was to be shown.

It follows immediately that exp is surjective:

∀y∈(0,∞),exp⁡(ln⁡(y))=y,

and injective:

exp⁡(x)=exp⁡(x′) ⇒ ln⁡(exp⁡(x))=ln⁡(exp⁡(x′)) ⇒ x=x′.

∎

So, for each positive number y, ln⁡y is the real number whose exponential is y. Recall that Euler’s number is

e=exp⁡(1)=∑n=0∞1n!.

It follows that ln⁡e=1. There is an alternative way to define e, as the limit of the sequence (1+1n)n. You are now in a position to prove this.

Proposition 7.29 implies that, for all a>0, ln⁡a is the unique real number y such that exp⁡(y)=a. This motivates the following definition:

Definition 7.31

Let a>0 and x∈ℝ. Then a to the power x is

ax=exp⁡(x⁢ln⁡a).

It follows from Proposition 5.10 that this definition of ax coincides with the more obvious definition of ax in the case where x is an integer.

So now we can make sense of the function f⁢(x)=xr for all x>0 and any constant r∈ℝ (even if r is irrational!). It is not hard to prove that f:(0,∞)→ℝ is differentiable, and has the derivative we expect.

Proposition 7.32

Let f:(0,∞)→ℝ be the function f⁢(x)=xr, where r is any real constant. Then f is differentiable, and for all x∈(0,∞),

f′⁢(x)=r⁢xr-1.

7.6 Analyticity versus smoothness

So far we have only considered power series based at 0, but the whole theory extends immediately to power series with some other base point x0∈ℝ, that is, series of the form

f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢(x-x0)n.

The point is that f⁢(x)=g⁢(x-x0), where g⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn, so (by the Chain Rule) f is smooth on (x0-R,x0+R) where R is the radius of convergence of g, and the derivative of f is again its termwise derivative. We will refer to R as the radius of convergence of f.

A function f is analytic if it locally coincides with some convergent power series, everywhere in its domain. More precisely:

Definition 7.33

Let U⊆ℝ be open. Then f:U→ℝ is analytic if, for each x0∈U, there exists ε>0 and a power series

∑n=0∞an⁢(x-x0)n

which converges to f⁢(x) for all x∈(x0-ε,x0+ε).

Example 7.34

exp:ℝ→ℝ is analytic since

exp⁡(x)=exp⁡(x0)⁢exp⁡(x-x0)=∑n=0∞exp⁡(x0)n!⁢(x-x0)n

for all x,x0∈ℝ (so for any x0 we can take any ε>0 in the definition above).

If f:U→ℝ is analytic, it is certainly smooth, by Corollary 7.23. Rather remarkably, the converse is false, that is, not all smooth functions are analytic. This section is devoted to constructing a class of functions f:ℝ→ℝ which are smooth but not analytic.

Definition 7.36

Given a polynomial p⁢(x)=a0+a1⁢x+⋯+ak⁢xk, we define the function fp:ℝ→ℝ such that

fp⁢(x)={0,x≤0p⁢(1/x)⁢exp⁡(-1/x),x>0.

We will prove that the function fp is smooth but not (except in the trivial case p⁢(x)=0) analytic. This follows fairly quickly once we establish that limx→0⁡fp⁢(x)=0.

Lemma 7.37 (Exponentials beat powers – sequences)

For any k∈ℕ the sequence

an=nk⁢exp⁡(-n)→0.
Proof.
an+1an=(1+1n)k⁢exp⁡(-1)→1e<1

so ∑n=1∞an converges by the Ratio Test. Hence an→0 by the Divergence Test. ∎

Lemma 7.38 (Exponentials beat powers – functions)

For any k∈ℕ,

limx→∞⁡xk⁢exp⁡(-x)=0.
Proof.

Let g⁢(x)=xk⁢exp⁡(-x). We must show that, for each ε>0, there exists K∈ℝ such that, for all x>K, |g⁢(x)-0|<ε.

So, let ε>0 be given. Since the sequence g⁢(n)=nk⁢exp⁡(-n)→0 (Lemma 7.37), there exists N∈ℤ+ such that, for all n≥N, |g⁢(n)-0|<ε, and hence 0<g⁢(n)<ε. Let K=max⁡{k,N}. Then for all x>K,

g′⁢(x)=(k⁢xk-1-xk)⁢exp⁡(-x)=-xk-1⁢(x-k)⁢exp⁡(-x)<0,

(by the Chain and Product Rules), so g is strictly decreasing (by Proposition 3.11). Hence, for all x>K, 0<g⁢(x)<g⁢(K)<ε (since K≥N). ∎

Lemma 7.39

Let fp:ℝ→ℝ be the function associated to a polynomial p, as in Definition 7.36. Then

limx→0⁡fp⁢(x)=0.
Proof.

Let p⁢(x)=a0+a1⁢x+⋯+ak⁢xk, and for each j∈ℕ, denote by mj the monomial of degree j (so mj⁢(x)=xj). Then

fp=a0⁢fm0+a1⁢fm1+⋯+ak⁢fmk

so, by the Algebra of Limits, it suffices to prove the claim in the case where p⁢(x)=mk⁢(x)=xk, a general monomial.

Let ε>0 be given. By Lemma 7.38, there exists K>0 such that, for all x>K, |xk⁢exp⁡(-x)-0|<ε. Let δ=1/K>0. Then, for all x∈(0,δ),

0≤fp⁢(x)=(1/x)k⁢exp⁡(-1/x)<ε,

since 1/x>1/δ=K, and for all x∈(-δ,0),

0=fp⁢(x)<ε.

Hence, for all x∈ℝ with 0<|x-0|<δ, |fp⁢(x)-0|<ε. ∎

Theorem 7.40

Let fp:ℝ→ℝ be the function associated to a polynomial p, as in Definition 7.36. Then fp is differentiable and fp′=fq where q⁢(x)=x2⁢(p⁢(x)-p′⁢(x)).

Proof.

fp coincides with the differentiable function p⁢(1/x)⁢exp⁡(-1/x) on the open set (0,∞), so is differentiable on (0,∞) with derivative

f′⁢(x)=p′⁢(x-1)⁢(-x-2)⁢exp⁡(-x-1)+p⁢(x-1)⁢exp⁡(-x-1)⁢(x-2)=q⁢(x-1)⁢exp⁡(-x-1)

by the Chain and Product Rules, and the Localization Lemma (Lemma 2.24). Similarly, fp coincides with the differentiable function 0 on the open set (-∞,0), so is differentiable on (-∞,0) with derivative 0 by the Localization Lemma. It remains to show that fp is differentiable at 0 and fp′⁢(0)=0. That is, we must prove that

limx→0⁡fp⁢(x)-f⁢(0)x-0=limx→0⁡fp⁢(x)x=0.

But fp⁢(x)/x=fP⁢(x) where P⁢(x)=x⁢p⁢(x), so this follows immediately from Lemma 7.39. ∎

Corollary 7.41

The function fp defined in Definition 7.36 is smooth and has fp(n)⁢(0)=0 for all n≥0.

Proof.

Denote by X the set of functions {fp:p a polynomial}. Then for all fp∈X, fp⁢(0)=0 (by definition), fp is differentiable, and fp′∈X (Theorem 7.40). Hence every element of X is infinitely differentiable and has all its derivatives zero at 0. ∎

It follows that almost every function fp is smooth, but not analytic. (The only exception is f0, where we take p⁢(x) to be the trivial polynomial p=0. Clearly f0≡0 which is trivially analytic.) Let’s consider the simplest example.

Example 7.42

Consider the special case p⁢(x)=1. The corresponding function is

f⁢(x)={exp⁡(-1/x),x>00,x≤0.

By Corollary 7.41, this is smooth and all its derivatives at 0 are 0. I claim that f is not analytic.

Assume, towards a contradiction, that f is analytic. Then there exist ε>0 and {an:n∈ℕ} such that

f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn

for all x∈(-ε,ε). But then, by Corollary 7.23, an=f(n)⁢(0)/n!=0. Hence f⁢(x)=0 for all x∈(-ε,ε). But f⁢(ε/2)=exp⁡(-2/ε)>0, a contradiction.

□

It’s not hard to construct from Example 7.42 smooth functions which vanish exactly outside some bounded interval (a,b) but are strictly positive on (a,b). Such functions are often called bump functions.

Example 7.43

Given an interval [a,b] define g:ℝ→ℝ by

g⁢(x)=f⁢(x-a)⁢f⁢(b-x)

where f is the smooth function of Example 7.42. Then g is smooth by the product and chain rules. If x≤a, x-a≤0 so f⁢(x-a)=0. If x≥b, b-x≤0 so f⁢(b-x)=0. Hence, g⁢(x)=0 for all x∉(a,b). If x∈(a,b), both x-a and b-x are positive, so f⁢(x-a)>0 and f⁢(b-x)>0, whence g⁢(x)>0. So g is strictly positive on (a,b). Here’s a plot of g in the case [a,b]=[0,1]:

Summary

  • •
    ​

    A power series is a series of the form ∑n=0∞an⁢xn where x is a real variable. Its radius of convergence is

    R=sup{|x|:∑n=0∞an⁢xn converges absolutely}.
  • •
    ​

    If a power series has radius of convergence R, it converges absolutely for all |x|<R and diverges for all |x|>R.

  • •
    ​

    The Weierstrass M Test: if |gn⁢(x)|≤Mn for all x∈D and the series ∑n=0∞Mn converges, then the series ∑n=0∞gn⁢(x) converges uniformly on D.

  • •
    ​

    Using the M test, we can prove that a power series with radius of convergence R>0 converges uniformly on every interval [-ρ,ρ], where ρ∈(0,R).

  • •
    ​

    A power series f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn with radius of convergence R>0 is differentiable on (-R,R) and its derivative is

    f′⁢(x)=∑n=1∞n⁢an⁢xn-1.

    This power series also has radius of convergence R.

  • •
    ​

    Convergent power series are, in fact, smooth.

  • •
    ​

    The exponential, sine, and cosine functions are defined by the power series

    exp⁡(x) = ∑n=0∞xnn!,
    sin⁡x = ∑n=0∞(-1)n⁢x2⁢n+1(2⁢n+1)!,
    cos⁡x = ∑n=0∞(-1)n⁢x2⁢n(2⁢n)!.

    These converge for all x∈ℝ and have the properties one expects.

  • •
    ​

    For all a>0 and x∈ℝ we define a to the power x to be

    ax=exp⁡(x⁢ln⁡a).

    This satisfies all the usual algebraic rules of exponents and coincides with (a1/q)p when x=p/q is rational.

  • •
    ​

    It follows from this definition that

    dd⁢x⁢xr=r⁢xr-1

    for all r∈ℝ.

  • •
    ​

    A function f:U→ℝ, where U⊆ℝ is an open set, is analytic if for each x0∈U there is ε>0 and a power series ∑n=0∞an⁢(x-x0)n which converges to f⁢(x) for all x∈(x0-ε,x0+ε).

  • •
    ​

    All analytic functions are smooth, but not all smooth functions are analytic. An example of a smooth but non-analytic function is

    f:ℝ→ℝ,f⁢(x)={exp⁡(-1/x),x>00,x≤0.
