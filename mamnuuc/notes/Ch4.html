4.1 Suprema and infima

In this section we will develop a mathematcally rigorous theory of integration. This will make frequent use of the idea of the supremum and infimum of a bounded set A⊂ℝ. Recall that A is bounded above if there exists K∈ℝ such that, for all x∈A, x≤K. Any such K is called an upper bound on A. “Bounded below" and “lower bound" are defined analogously: A⊂ℝ is bounded below if there exists L∈ℝ such that, for all x∈A, x≥L, and any such L is called a lower bound on A. A set is bounded if it is bounded above and below.

Definition 4.1

Let A be a nonempty subset of ℝ. The supremum of A, denoted sup⁡A, is the least upper bound on A, if this exists. That is, sup⁡A is a real number with two properties:

  1. (i)
    ​

    sup⁡A is an upper bound on A (i.e. for all x∈A, x≤sup⁡A), and

  2. (ii)
    ​

    no number less than sup⁡A is an upper bound on A.

Similarly, the infimum of A, denoted inf⁡A, is the greatest lower bound on A, if this exists. That is, inf⁡A is a real number with two properties:

  1. (i)
    ​

    inf⁡A is a lower bound on A (i.e. for all x∈A, x≥inf⁡A), and

  2. (ii)
    ​

    no number greater than inf⁡A is a lower bound on A.

One of the defining properties of ℝ is:

The Axiom of Completeness Every nonempty subset of ℝ which is bounded above has a supremum in ℝ.

Example 4.2

Let A=(-∞,1). This is nonempty and bounded above, by 1, for example. Hence, by the Axiom of Completeness, it must have a supremum. In fact, sup⁡A=1.

Proof.

We’ve already observed that 1 is an upper bound on A. Let K<1. Then x=(K+1)/2<(1+1)/2=1 so x∈A. But x>(K+K)/2=K, so K is not an upper bound on A. Hence 1 is the least upper bound on A. ∎

Although “supremum" and “maximum" are not the same thing, they behave similarly in many respects. Here’s an example. If sets A and B both have maxima, and A is a subset of B, then clearly max⁡A≤max⁡B. The same holds for suprema. If B is bounded above and A⊆B, then any upper bound on B is also an upper bound on A. In particular, sup⁡B is an upper bound on A. Since sup⁡A is the least upper bound on A, it follows that sup⁡A≤sup⁡B. That is

A⊆B  ⇒  sup⁡A≤sup⁡B.

By similar reasoning,

A⊆B  ⇒  inf⁡A≥inf⁡B.

We can use suprema to give a useful definition of the “width" of a bounded subset of ℝ:

Definition 4.5

The diameter of a bounded set A⊂ℝ is

diamA=sup{|x-y|:x,y∈A}.

Proposition 4.7

diam⁢A=sup⁡A-inf⁡A.

Proof.

diam⁢A is the supremum of

B={|x-y|:x,y∈A}={x-y:x,y∈A,x≥y}.

Now, for all x,y∈A, x≤sup⁡A and y≥inf⁡A, so x-y≤sup⁡A-inf⁡A. Hence sup⁡A-inf⁡A is an upper bound on B. Clearly, 0∈B, so no K<0 can be an upper bound on B. Let 0≤K<sup⁡A-inf⁡A. Then ε=(sup⁡A-inf⁡A-K)/2>0, so sup⁡A-ε is not an upper bound on A, and inf⁡A+ε is not a lower bound on A. Hence there exist x∈A with x>sup⁡A-ε and y∈A with y<inf⁡A+ε. But then x-y>sup⁡A-ε-(inf⁡A+ε)=K≥0, so x≥y, whence x-y∈B. But x-y>K, so K is not an upper bound on B. Hence sup⁡A-inf⁡A=sup⁡B. ∎

In a little while we will need the following lemma.

Lemma 4.8

Let A⊂ℝ be bounded and |A|={|x|:x∈A}. Then

diam⁢|A|≤diam⁢A.

The proof relies on a useful inequality:

Lemma 4.9 (The reverse Triangle Inequality)

For all x,y∈ℝ,

|x-y|≥||x|-|y||.
Proof.

Let a=x-y and b=y. Then by the usual triangle inequality

|x| = |a+b|≤|a|+|b|=|x-y|+|y|
⇒ |x-y| ≥ |x|-|y|.

Repeating this argument with a=y-x and b=x yields |x-y|≥|y|-|x|. Hence

|x-y|≥max⁡{|x|-|y|,|y|-|x|}=||x|-|y||.

∎

Proof of Lemma 4.8:  For all x,y∈A,

||x|-|y|| ≤ |x-y|  (by Lemma 4.9)
≤ diam⁢A,

so diam⁢A is an upper bound on {||x|-|y||:x,y,∈A}. Hence diam|A|=sup{||x|-|y||:x,y∈A}≤diamA. □

4.2 Dissections and Riemann sums

How do we define the area of a bounded subset of the plane ℝ2? If the subset is a rectangle, the answer is easy: its area is its length times its width. Similarly, if the subset is a union of non-overlapping rectangles it’s easy: we just add up the areas of all the constituent rectangles. But what if the subset is more complicated: the region bounded by the x-axis, the vertical lines x=a and x=b>a and the graph y=f⁢(x) of some non-constant function, for example? One approach is to define the area of such a region to be the unique real number (if it exists) which is no bigger than the total area of any collection of rectangles which covers the region, and no smaller than the total area of any collection of rectangles which is covered by the region. This is the underlying idea that leads to the Riemann integral.

We begin by identifying the collections of rectangles we will use. These are determined by dissecting the interval [a,b] into a finite collection of subintervals.

Definition 4.10

A dissection of a closed bounded interval [a,b] is a finite subset 𝒟 of [a,b] containing both a and b. By convention, if 𝒟 has n+1 elements, we label these a0,a1,…,an, so that

a=a0<a1<a2<⋯<an=b,

and say that 𝒟 is a dissection of size n. We say that 𝒟 is a regular dissection if aj-aj-1=(b-a)/n for all j, that is, if the points in the dissection are regularly spaced.

Remark The size of a dissection is one less than the number of elements it contains. This is the number of subintervals [aj-1,aj] into which the dissection divides the interval [a,b].

Definition 4.11

Let f:[a,b]→ℝ be a bounded function and 𝒟 be a dissection of size n of [a,b]. For each j∈{1,2,…,n}, let

mj = inf⁡{f⁢(x):aj-1≤x≤aj},
and Mj = sup⁡{f⁢(x):aj-1≤x≤aj}.

Note that these numbers exist, since f is bounded. The lower Riemann sum of f with respect to 𝒟 is

l𝒟⁢(f)=∑j=1nmj⁢(aj-aj-1),

and the upper Riemann sum of f with respect to 𝒟 is

u𝒟⁢(f)=∑j=1nMj⁢(aj-aj-1).

□

The idea is that a dissection of size n divides [a,b] into n subintervals, [aj-1,aj] for j=1,2,…,n. If f⁢(x)≥0 for all x, then the lower Riemann sum can be visualized as the total area of the tallest rectangles with bases [aj-1,aj] which fit under the graph y=f⁢(x) between x=a and x=b. So l𝒟⁢(f) is an underestimate of the area under the graph. Similarly, the upper Riemann sum can be visualized as the total area of the shortest rectangles with bases [aj-1,aj] which the graph y=f⁢(x) between x=a and x=b fits under and is thus an overestimate of the area under the graph. This is illustrated below

Proposition 4.13

Let f:[a,b]→ℝ be bounded above by M and below by m, and let 𝒟 be any dissection of [a,b]. Then

m⁢(b-a)≤l𝒟⁢(f)≤u𝒟⁢(f)≤M⁢(b-a).
Proof.

Let 𝒟={a0,a1,…,an} and mj, Mj be defined as in Definition 4.11. Then, for all j, m≤mj≤Mj≤M, so

l𝒟⁢(f)=∑j=1nmj⁢(aj-aj-1)≤∑j=1nMj⁢(aj-aj-1)=u𝒟⁢(f)

and

l𝒟⁢(f) ≥ ∑j=1nm⁢(aj-aj-1)
= m⁢{(a1-a0)+(a2-a1)+(a3-a2)+⋯+(an-an-1)}=m⁢(an-a0)
= m⁢(b-a)

and

u𝒟⁢(f) ≤ ∑j=1nM⁢(aj-aj-1)
= M⁢{(a1-a0)+(a2-a1)+(a3-a2)+⋯+(an-an-1)}=M⁢(an-a0)
= M⁢(b-a)

since the sums “telescope." ∎

4.3 Definition of the Riemann integral

It follows from Proposition 4.13 that the set of all lower Riemann sums

{l𝒟⁢(f):𝒟 any dissection of [a,b]}

is bounded above, by M⁢(b-a), and the set of all upper Riemann sums

{u𝒟⁢(f):𝒟 any dissection of [a,b]}

is bounded below, by m⁢(b-a), where

m=inf⁡{f⁢(x):x∈[a,b]},M=sup⁡{f⁢(x):x∈[a,b]}.

Hence, by the Axiom of Completeness and Theorem 4.4, the following definition makes sense.

Definition 4.14

Let f:[a,b]→ℝ be a bounded function. Then its lower Riemann integral is

l⁢(f)=sup⁡{l𝒟⁢(f):𝒟 any dissection of [a,b]},

and its upper Riemann integral is

u⁢(f)=inf⁡{u𝒟⁢(f):𝒟 any dissection of [a,b]}.

We say that f is Riemann integrable (on [a,b]) if l⁢(f)=u⁢(f). In that case, we denote this commmon value by

∫abf or ∫abf⁢(x)⁢𝑑x,

and call this number the Riemann integral of f (over [a,b]). □

Geometrically, we can think of l⁢(f) as the least upper bound on the collection of all underestimates of the area under the curve y=f⁢(x), and u⁢(f) as the greatest lower bound on the collection of overestimates of the area under the curve. Then ∫abf is, loosely, the unique number (where this exists) which is smaller than every overestimate and larger than every underestimate. As it stands, to compute this number, or even show that it exists, we need to consider the collection of all possible dissections of [a,b]. This is a very large and complicated set, so, before proceeding further, we need to develop some tools for handling l⁢(f) and u⁢(f) which allow us to avoid considering all possible dissections. The key idea is that of refinement of a dissection.

Definition 4.15

Given dissections 𝒟,𝒟′ of [a,b], we say that 𝒟′ is a refinement of 𝒟 if 𝒟⊆𝒟′. If 𝒟′\𝒟 contains k points, we say that 𝒟′ is a k-point refinement of 𝒟. Note that 𝒟 is the unique 0-point refinement of itself.

The idea is that a refinement 𝒟′ of 𝒟 contains all the points in 𝒟 and (unless 𝒟′=𝒟) some more points too, so it splits [a,b] up into more subintervals, at least some of which are narrower. Intuitively, one expects that passing from 𝒟 to a refinement of 𝒟 can only improve, that is, increase, the underestimate l𝒟⁢(f). Similarly, passing to a refinement of 𝒟, one expects, can only reduce the overestimate u𝒟⁢(f). This expectation turns out to be essentially correct and is fundamental.

Lemma 4.16 (Refinement Lemma)

Let f:[a,b]→ℝ be bounded, 𝒟,𝒟′ be dissections of [a,b], and 𝒟′ be a refinement of 𝒟. Then

l𝒟⁢(f)≤l𝒟′⁢(f)≤u𝒟′⁢(f)≤u𝒟⁢(f).

Before reading through the proof of this fundamental result, it may help to watch the following [VIDEO]

Proof.

We first prove this in the special case where 𝒟′ is a 1-point refinement of 𝒟. So, let 𝒟={a=a0,a1,…,an=b} and 𝒟′=𝒟∪{z} where z∉𝒟. Then there exists k∈{1,…,n} such that z∈(ak-1,ak). Let

mj = inf⁡{f⁢(x):aj≤x≤aj-1}
m′ = inf⁡{f⁢(x):ak-1≤x≤z}
m′′ = inf⁡{f⁢(x):z≤x≤ak}
Mj = sup⁡{f⁢(x):aj≤x≤aj-1}
M′ = sup⁡{f⁢(x):ak-1≤x≤z}
M′′ = sup⁡{f⁢(x):z≤x≤ak}

and note that, since [ak-1,z] and [z,ak] are subsets of [ak-1,ak], we know immediately that m′,m′′≥mk, and M′,M′′≤Mk. Now

l𝒟′⁢(f) = ∑j∈{1,2,…,n}\{k}mj⁢(aj-aj-1)+m′⁢(z-ak-1)+m′′⁢(ak-z)
= l𝒟⁢(f)-mk⁢(ak-ak-1)+m′⁢(z-ak-1)+m′′⁢(ak-z)
≥ l𝒟⁢(f)-mk⁢(ak-ak-1)+mk⁢(z-ak-1)+mk⁢(ak-z)
= l𝒟⁢(f),

and

u𝒟′⁢(f) = ∑j∈{1,2,…,n}\{k}Mj⁢(aj-aj-1)+M′⁢(z-ak-1)+M′′⁢(ak-z)
= u𝒟⁢(f)-Mk⁢(ak-ak-1)+M′⁢(z-ak-1)+M′′⁢(ak-z)
≤ u𝒟⁢(f)-Mk⁢(ak-ak-1)+Mk⁢(z-ak-1)+Mk⁢(ak-z)
= u𝒟⁢(f).

Hence, by Proposition 4.13,

l𝒟⁢(f)≤l𝒟′⁢(f)≤u𝒟′⁢(f)≤u𝒟⁢(f),

that is, the Refinement Lemma holds for every 1-point refinement of every dissection.

Consider now the case where 𝒟′=𝒟∪{z1,z2,…,zp} is a p-point refinement of 𝒟. Then we can define a chain of dissections 𝒟i, i=0,…,p, by 𝒟0=𝒟 and 𝒟i=𝒟i-1∪{zi} for each i=1,…,p. Note that 𝒟p=𝒟′, and that each 𝒟i is a 1-point refinement of 𝒟i-1. Hence, by the result just proved,

l𝒟⁢(f)=l𝒟0⁢(f)≤l𝒟1⁢(f)≤l𝒟2⁢(f)≤⋯≤l𝒟p⁢(f)=l𝒟′⁢(f)

and

u𝒟⁢(f)=u𝒟0⁢(f)≥u𝒟1⁢(f)≥u𝒟2⁢(f)≥⋯≥u𝒟p⁢(f)=u𝒟′⁢(f).

By Proposition 4.13, l𝒟′⁢(f)≤u𝒟′⁢(f), and so

l𝒟⁢(f)≤l𝒟′⁢(f)≤u𝒟′⁢(f)≤u𝒟⁢(f),

as was to be proved. ∎

It follows immediately from the Refinement Lemma that every upper Riemann sum is at least as large as every lower Riemann sum, whatever (possibly different) dissections we use to compute them.

Lemma 4.17

Let 𝒟,𝒟^ be two dissections of [a,b] and f:[a,b]→ℝ be bounded. Then l𝒟⁢(f)≤u𝒟^⁢(f).

Proof.

𝒟′=𝒟∪𝒟^ is a refinement of both 𝒟 and 𝒟^, so by the Refinement Lemma,

l𝒟⁢(f)≤l𝒟′⁢(f)≤u𝒟′⁢(f)≤u𝒟^⁢(f).

∎

From this, it follows that the upper Riemann integral is no less than the lower Riemann integral.

Lemma 4.18

Let f:[a,b]→ℝ be bounded. Then l⁢(f)≤u⁢(f).

We are now, finally, in a position to compute some nontrivial Riemann integrals.

Example 4.19

Let f:[0,2]→ℝ be the “step” function

f⁢(x)={1 if 0≤x<1,2 if 1≤x≤2.

Show that f is Riemann integrable on [0,2] and compute ∫02f.

Solution For each r∈(0,1) define the dissection 𝒟r={0,1-r,1+r,2} of 𝒟r

Then the lower and upper Riemann sums of f with respect to this dissection are

l𝒟r⁢(f) = 1⁢(1-r-0)+1⁢((1+r)-(1-r))+2⁢(2-(1+r))=3-r
u𝒟r⁢(f) = 1⁢(1-r-0)+2⁢((1+r)-(1-r))+2⁢(2-(1+r))=3+r.

Hence, l⁢(f) is the supremum of a set (the set of all lower sums) which contains {3-r: 0<r<1}=(2,3), so l⁢(f)≥3. Similarly, u⁢(f) is the infimum of a set (the set of all upper sums) which contains {3+r: 0<r<1}=(3,4), so u⁢(f)≤3. Hence l⁢(f)≥u⁢(f). But by Lemma 4.18, l⁢(f)≤u⁢(f), so l⁢(f)=u⁢(f), that is, f is Riemann integrable. Furthermore

∫02f=l⁢(f)≥3 and ∫02f=u⁢(f)≤3

so we conclude that ∫02f=3. □

Example 4.20

Let f:[0,1]→ℝ be the function f⁢(x)=x2. Show that f is Riemann integrable on [0,1] and compute ∫01f.

Solution For each integer n≥1 let 𝒟n be the regular dissection of [0,1] of size n, that is, the dissection that divides [0,1] into n subintervals of equal width,

𝒟n={0,1n,2n,…,n-1n,1}.

Each subinterval [aj-1,aj]=[(j-1)/n,j/n] has width 1/n, and, since the function f is increasing,

mj = inf⁡{f⁢(x):j-1n≤x≤jn}
= f⁢(j-1n)=(j-1)2n2,
Mj = sup⁡{f⁢(x):j-1n≤x≤jn}
= f⁢(jn)=j2n2,

so the lower and upper Riemann sums with respect to 𝒟n are

l𝒟n⁢(f) = 1n⁢∑j=1n(j-1)2n2=1n3⁢∑k=1n-1k2,
u𝒟n⁢(f) = 1n⁢∑j=1nj2n2=1n3⁢∑j=1nj2.

I claim that, for all n∈ℤ+,

∑j=1nj2=16⁢n⁢(n+1)⁢(2⁢n+1),

and leave the proof of this as an exercise (hint: use induction!). Hence, for each n∈ℤ+,

l𝒟n⁢(f) = 16⁢(1-1n)⁢(2-1n),
u𝒟n⁢(f) = 16⁢(1+1n)⁢(2+1n).

By definition, l⁢(f)≥l𝒟n⁢(f) for all n (it is an upper bound on a set containing every l𝒟n⁢(f)), and l𝒟n⁢(f)→13. Hence l⁢(f)≥13 (Theorem 1.7).

Similarly, u⁢(f)≤u𝒟n⁢(f) for all n (it is a lower bound on a set containing every u𝒟n⁢(f)), and u𝒟n⁢(f)→13. Hence u⁢(f)≤13 (Proposition 1.7).

Hence u⁢(f)≤13≤l⁢(f). But, by Lemma 4.18, l⁢(f)≤u⁢(f), so we conclude that l⁢(f)=u⁢(f), that is, f is Riemann integrable. Furthermore,

∫01f=l⁢(f)≥13 and ∫01f=u⁢(f)≤13,

whence ∫01f=13. □

It is convenient to extend the definition of dissection to include the case where the interval is [a,a]={a}. The only dissection of [a,a] is the singleton set 𝒟={a} (a dissection of size 0). Every function f:[a,a]→ℝ is bounded, above and below by f⁢(a), so the one and only lower Riemann sum is

l{a}⁢(f)=f⁢(a)⁢(a-a)=0,

and the one and only upper Riemann sum is

u{a}⁢(f)=f⁢(a)⁢(a-a)=0.

It follows that every function is integrable on [a,a], and

∫aaf=0.

Given a Riemann integrable function f on [a,b] where a≤b, it is also convenient to define

∫baf=-∫abf.

I leave it as an exercise to verify that all the results we prove about ∫abf trivially extend to the case a≥b with these conventions.

4.4 A sequential characterization of integrability

We can generalize the argument used in Example 4.20 to give a rather elegant (and useful) characterization of Riemann integrability in terms of sequences of dissections.

Theorem 4.21

Let f:[a,b]→ℝ be a bounded function. Then f is Riemann integrable if and only if there exists a sequence (𝒟n) of dissections of [a,b] such that

u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f)→0.

In this case,

∫abf=lim⁡l𝒟n⁢(f)=lim⁡u𝒟n⁢(f).

Before reading through the proof, watch this [VIDEO]

Proof.

Let 𝒟 be the set of all dissections of [a,b], ℒ={l𝒟⁢(f):𝒟∈𝒟} the set of all lower Riemann sums of f, and 𝒰={u𝒟⁢(f):𝒟∈𝒟} the set of all upper Riemann sums of f, so l⁢(f)=sup⁡ℒ and u⁢(f)=inf⁡𝒰.

We first prove the “only if" (⇒) direction. So assume that u⁢(f)=l⁢(f) (that is, f is Riemann integrable). For each n∈ℤ+, u⁢(f)+1/n>u⁢(f) the infimum of 𝒰, so is not a lower bound on 𝒰. Hence there exists 𝒟n′∈𝒟 such that u𝒟n′⁢(f)<u⁢(f)+1/n. Similarly, l⁢(f)-1/n<l⁢(f)=sup⁡ℒ, so is not an upper bound on ℒ. Hence there exists 𝒟n′′∈𝒟 such that l𝒟n′′⁢(f)>l⁢(f)-1/n. Let 𝒟n=𝒟n′∪𝒟n′′ and note this is a refinement of both 𝒟n′ and 𝒟n′′. Hence, by the Refinement Lemma,

u⁢(f)≤ u𝒟n⁢(f) ≤u𝒟n′⁢(f)<u⁢(f)+1n
l⁢(f)-1n<l𝒟n′′⁢(f)≤ l𝒟n⁢(f) ≤l⁢(f).

Hence, by the Squeeze Rule, u𝒟n⁢(f)→u⁢(f) and l𝒟n⁢(f)→l⁢(f). But u⁢(f)=l⁢(f), so u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f)→0 by the Algebra of Limits. Furthermore

∫abf=u⁢(f)=lim⁡u𝒟n⁢(f)=lim⁡l𝒟n⁢(f).

We now prove the “if" (⇐) direction. So assume that a sequence of dissections 𝒟n exists such that u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f)→0. For all n, u𝒟n⁢(f)≥u⁢(f) and l𝒟n⁢(f)≤l⁢(f), so u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f)≥u⁢(f)-l⁢(f). Hence, by Proposition 1.7

0=lim⁡(u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f))≥u⁢(f)-l⁢(f),

that is, l⁢(f)≥u⁢(f). But l⁢(f)≤u⁢(f) by Proposition 4.17, so l⁢(f)=u⁢(f), that is, f is Riemann integrable. It remains to show that u⁢(f)=lim⁡u𝒟n⁢(f) (from which it follows, by the Algebra of Limits that l⁢(f)=lim⁡l𝒟n⁢(f)). Now

0 ≤ u𝒟n⁢(f)-u⁢(f)≤u𝒟n⁢(f)-u⁢(f)+l⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f)=u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f)→0

so, by the Squeeze Rule, u𝒟n⁢(f)-u⁢(f)→0. Hence u𝒟n⁢(f)→u⁢(f).

∎

To illustrate the power of Theorem 4.21, let’s use it to prove that every monotonic (i.e. increasing or decreasing) function is Riemann integrable.

Theorem 4.22

Let f:[a,b]→ℝ be increasing. Then f is Riemann integrable.

Proof.

First note that since f is increasing, it is bounded (below by f⁢(a) and above by f⁢(b), so l⁢(f) and u⁢(f) exist. For each n∈ℤ+ let 𝒟n be the regular dissection of [a,b] of size n, that is, the dissection which divides [a,b] into n subintervals [aj-1,aj] each of width (b-a)/n.

Then, for each j=1,…,n,

mj = inf⁡{f⁢(x):aj-1≤x≤aj}=f⁢(aj-1)
Mj = sup⁡{f⁢(x):aj-1≤x≤aj}=f⁢(aj),

and so

u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f) = b-an⁢∑j=1n(f⁢(aj)-f⁢(aj-1))
= b-an⁢(f⁢(b)-f⁢(a))

since the sum telescopes. Hence u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f)→0, so f is Riemann integrable on [a,b] by Theorem 4.21. ∎

Note that Theorem 4.22 partially covers both Examples 4.19 and 4.20: in both these examples the function in question is increasing so Theorem 4.22 implies that they’re both Riemann integrable. It does not, however, tell us anything about the value of ∫abf, only that it exists. Note also that the function in Example 4.19 is discontinuous, at a single point (x=1), which illustrates that while a discontinuous function cannot be differentiable (Proposition 2.8), it certainly can be integrable. In fact, we can construct examples of functions which are discontinuous at infinitely many points in [a,b] and yet are still Riemann integrable on [a,b].

Example 4.24

Consider the function f:[0,1]→ℝ defined so that, f⁢(0)=0 and, for all x∈(1n+1,1n], where n∈ℤ+, f⁢(x)=1n. By construction, f is increasing and so is Riemann integrable on [0,1] by Theorem 4.22. Note, however, that f is discontinuous at every point 1n for n≥2.

If even such an extreme example as this is Riemann integrable, you might wonder whether there are any bounded functions which fail to be integrable. Fear not: such functions certainly do exist. Here is an example.

Example 4.25

Let f:[0,1]→ℝ such that f⁢(x)=0 if x∈ℚ and f⁢(x)=1 if x∉ℚ. I claim that f is not Riemann integrable.

Proof.

Let 𝒟={a0,…,an} be any dissection of [0,1]. Then, every subinterval [aj-1,aj] contains both rational and irrational members, so mj=0 and Mj=1 for all j. Hence

l𝒟⁢(f) = ∑j=1n0⁢(aj-aj-1)=0
u𝒟⁢(f) = ∑j=1n1⁢(aj-aj-1)=an-a0=1.

Since this is true for all dissections of [0,1], l⁢(f)=sup⁡{0}=0 and u⁢(f)=inf⁡{1}=1, so l⁢(f)≠u⁢(f), that is, f is not Riemann integrable. ∎

Theorem 4.22 gives us one interesting class of functions f:[a,b]→ℝ that are Riemann integrable: those that are increasing. Our next theorem gives us another rather more useful class: continuous functions.

Theorem 4.26

Let f:[a,b]→ℝ be continuous. Then f is Riemann integrable.

Proof.

First note that, since f is continuous, it is certainly bounded (by the Extreme Value Theorem), so l⁢(f) and u⁢(f) exist.

Consider the sequence (𝒟n) of regular dissections of [a,b] of size 2n. I claim that u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f)→0, so f is Riemann integrable by Theorem 4.21. Note that 𝒟n⊂𝒟n+1 for all n, so by the Refinement Lemma, l𝒟n⁢(f) is an increasing sequence and u𝒟n⁢(f) is a decreasing sequence. Hence l𝒟n⁢(f)→K and u𝒟n⁢(f)→L for some numbers K and L by the Monotone Convergence Theorem, so u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f)→K-L≥0 by the Algebra of Limits and Proposition 1.7. We must show that K-L=0.

Assume, towards a contradiction, that ε=K-L>0. As usual, let

mj = inf⁡{f⁢(x):aj-1≤x≤aj}
and Mj = sup⁡{f⁢(x):aj-1≤x≤aj}.

Then

u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f)=∑j=12nb-a2n⁢(Mj-mj)≥ε.

This sum consists of 2n terms, each non-negative, so at least one term must be greater than or equal to ε/2n. That is, there must exist j∈{1,2,3,…,2n} such that

Mj-mj≥εb-a.

But f:[aj-1,aj]→ℝ is continuous so, by the Extreme Value Theorem, f attains both a maximum and minimum value on [aj-1,aj], that is, there are points, xn and yn say, in [aj-1,aj] such that f⁢(xn)=Mj and f⁢(yn)=mj. Hence, for each n∈ℤ+ there exist xn,yn∈[a,b] such that

|xn-yn| ≤ b-a2n, (4.1)

since both lie in an interval of this width, and

f⁢(xn)-f⁢(yn) ≥ εb-a. (4.2)

Consider the sequence (xn). It is bounded, so, by the Bolzano–Weierstrass Theorem (Theorem 1.14), it has a convergent subsequence xnk→c∈[a,b]. By (4.1),

xnk-b-a2nk≤ynk≤xnk+b-a2nk

so ynk→c also, by the Squeeze Rule. Now f is continuous, so f⁢(xnk)→f⁢(c) and f⁢(ynk)→f⁢(c), and hence f⁢(xnk)-f⁢(ynk)→f⁢(c)-f⁢(c)=0. But this contradicts (4.2) and Proposition 1.7. ∎

4.5 Elementary properties of the Riemann integral

Theorem 4.21 is a convenient tool for establishing many useful properties of the Riemann integral.

Proposition 4.27

Let f be Riemann integrable on [a,b] and on [b,c]. Then f is Riemann integrable on [a,c] and

∫acf=∫abf+∫bcf.
Proof.

By Theorem 4.21, there exist sequences of dissections (𝒟n′) of [a,b] and (𝒟n′′) of [b,c] such that l𝒟n′⁢(f)→∫abf, u𝒟n′⁢(f)→∫abf, l𝒟n′′⁢(f)→∫bcf, and u𝒟n′′⁢(f)→∫bcf. Let 𝒟n=𝒟n′∪𝒟n′′. Then 𝒟n is a dissection of [a,c] and it follows directly from Definition 4.11 that l𝒟n⁢(f)=l𝒟n′⁢(f)+l𝒟n′′⁢(f) and u𝒟n⁢(f)=u𝒟n′⁢(f)+u𝒟n′′⁢(f). Hence, by the Algebra of Limits, l𝒟n⁢(f)→∫abf+∫bcf and u𝒟n⁢(f)→∫abf+∫bcf, so the claim follows from Theorem 4.21. ∎

We next prove that Riemann integration preserves inequalities.

Proposition 4.28

Let f,g:[a,b]→ℝ be Riemann integrable and assume f⁢(x)≤g⁢(x) for all x∈[a,b]. Then

∫abf≤∫abg.
Proof.

By Theorem 4.21 there exist sequences of dissections 𝒟n′, 𝒟n′′ such that l𝒟n′⁢(f)→∫abf and l𝒟n′′→∫abg. Let 𝒟n=𝒟n′∪𝒟n′′. Then, by the Refinement Lemma (and the Squeeze Rule) l𝒟n⁢(f)→∫abf and l𝒟n→∫abg also. But, for each n, l𝒟n⁢(g)≥l𝒟n⁢(f) since inf⁡g≥inf⁡f on any subset of [a,b]. Hence, ∫abg-∫abf, being the limit of a convergent non-negative sequence, is ≥0 (Proposition 1.7). ∎

From this we can deduce a kind of “triangle inequality" for Riemann integrals.

Proposition 4.29 (Integral Triangle Inequality)

Let f:[a,b]→ℝ be Riemann integrable. Then |f|:[a,b]→ℝ is Riemann integrable, and

|∫abf|≤∫ab|f|.
Proof.

We first note that, if |f| is Riemann integrable then, since -|f⁢(x)|≤f⁢(x)≤|f⁢(x)| for all x∈[a,b],

-∫ab|f|≤∫abf≤∫ab|f|

by Proposition 4.28, so the claimed inequality follows. It remains to show that |f| is Riemann integrable.

First note that, for any bounded function g:[a,b]→ℝ and any fixed dissection 𝒟={a=a0,a1,…,aN=b} of [a,b],

0≤u𝒟⁢(g)-l𝒟⁢(g) = ∑i=1N(ai-ai-1)⁢(sup⁡{g⁢(x):x∈[ai-1,ai]}-inf⁡{g⁢(x):x∈[ai-1,ai]})
= ∑i=1N(ai-ai-1)⁢diam⁢{g⁢(x):x∈[ai-1,ai]}

by Proposition 4.7 (recall the diameter of a bounded set was defined in Definition 4.5). Hence

0≤u𝒟⁢(|f|)-l𝒟⁢(|f|) = ∑i=1N(ai-ai-1)diam{|f(x)|:x∈[ai-1,ai]})
≤ ∑i=1N(ai-ai-1)diam{f(x):x∈[ai-1,ai]})  (by Lemma 4.8)
= u𝒟⁢(f)-l𝒟⁢(f).

This holds for any dissection 𝒟. Since f is Riemann integrable, there exists (by Theorem 4.21) a sequence of dissections (𝒟n) such that u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f)→0. But then

0≤u𝒟n⁢(|f|)-l𝒟n⁢(|f|)≤u𝒟n⁢(f)-l𝒟n⁢(f)

for all n, so u𝒟n⁢(|f|)-l𝒟n⁢(|f|)→0 by the Squeeze Rule. Hence |f| is Riemann Integrable (Theorem 4.21 again). ∎

We next want to prove that the Riemann integral is linear, that is,

∫ab(α⁢f+β⁢g)=α⁢∫abf+β⁢∫abg,

for any constants α,β and any integrable functions f,g. To do this, we need the following:

Lemma 4.30

Let f,g:[a,b]→ℝ be bounded functions and 𝒟 be a dissection of [a,b]. Then

l𝒟⁢(f+g)≥l𝒟⁢(f)+l𝒟⁢(g) 𝑎𝑛𝑑 u𝒟⁢(f+g)≤u𝒟⁢(f)+u𝒟⁢(g).

Theorem 4.31 (Linearity of the Riemann Integral)

Let f,g be Riemann integrable on [a,b], and α∈ℝ be a constant. Then

  • (i)
    ​

    α⁢f is Riemann integrable on [a,b], and ∫abα⁢f=α⁢∫abf,

  • (ii)
    ​

    f+g is Riemann integrable on [a,b], and ∫ab(f+g)=∫abf+∫abg.

Proof.

(i) By Theorem 4.21, there is a sequence of dissections (𝒟n) such that l𝒟n⁢(f)→∫abf and u𝒟n⁢(f)→∫abf. It follows directly from Definition 4.11 that l𝒟n⁢(α⁢f)=α⁢l𝒟n⁢(f) and u𝒟n⁢(α⁢f)=α⁢u𝒟n⁢(f) if α≥0, and l𝒟n⁢(α⁢f)=α⁢u𝒟n⁢(f) and u𝒟n⁢(α⁢f)=α⁢l𝒟n⁢(f) if α<0. In either case, l𝒟n⁢(α⁢f)→α⁢∫abf and u𝒟n⁢(α⁢f)→α⁢∫abf by the Algebra of Limits, and the claim follows from Theorem 4.21.

(ii) Let (𝒟n) be as defined above. By Theorem 4.21, there is also a sequence of dissections (𝒟n′) such that l𝒟n′⁢(g)→∫abg and u𝒟n′⁢(g)→∫abg. Let 𝒟n′′=𝒟n∪𝒟n′. This is a refinement of both 𝒟n and 𝒟n′ and so, by the Refinement Lemma

l𝒟n⁢(f)≤l𝒟n′′⁢(f)≤∫abf,
l𝒟n′⁢(g)≤l𝒟n′′⁢(g)≤∫abg,
∫abf≤u𝒟n′′⁢(f)≤u𝒟n⁢(f),
∫abf≤u𝒟n′′⁢(g)≤u𝒟n′⁢(g).

Hence, by the Squeeze Rule, l𝒟n′′⁢(f)→∫abf, l𝒟n′′⁢(g)→∫abg, u𝒟n′′⁢(f)→∫abf and u𝒟n′′⁢(f)→∫abg. Now, by Lemma 4.30,

l𝒟n′′⁢(f)+l𝒟n′′⁢(g)≤l𝒟n′′⁢(f+g)≤l⁢(f+g),

and

u⁢(f+g)≤u𝒟n′′⁢(f+g)≤u𝒟n′′⁢(f)+u𝒟n′′⁢(g).

But both l𝒟n′′⁢(f)+l𝒟n′′⁢(g) and u𝒟n′′⁢(f)+u𝒟n′′⁢(g) converge to ∫abf+∫abg so, by Proposition 1.7, ∫abf+∫abg≤l⁢(f+g) and u⁢(f+g)≤∫abf+∫abg. It follows that l⁢(f+g)≥u⁢(f+g) and hence, by Lemma 4.18, that l⁢(f+g)=u⁢(f+g), that is, f+g is Riemann integrable. Furthermore

∫ab(f+g)=l⁢(f+g)≥∫abf+∫abg
and ∫ab(f+g)=u⁢(f+g)≤∫abf+∫abg,

so ∫ab(f+g)=∫abf+∫abg, as claimed. ∎

Let’s denote by L⁢([a,b]) the set of Riemann integrable functions on [a,b]. Theorem 4.31 establishes that L⁢([a,b]) is in fact a vector space over ℝ: it is closed under the obvious (vector) addition and scalar multiplication operations. Better still, it shows that the map

L⁢([a,b])→ℝ,f↦∫abf

is a linear map between vector spaces.

Summary

  • •
    ​

    A dissection of [a,b] is a finite subset 𝒟={a0,a1,…,an} of [a,b] such that

    a=a0<a1<a2<⋯<an=b.
  • •
    ​

    Given a bounded function f:[a,b]→ℝ, and a dissection 𝒟, the lower Riemann sum is

    l𝒟⁢(f)=∑j=1ninf⁡{f⁢(x):aj-1≤x≤aj}⁢(aj-aj-1),

    and the upper Riemann sum is

    u𝒟⁢(f)=∑j=1nsup⁡{f⁢(x):aj-1≤x≤aj}⁢(aj-aj-1).
  • •
    ​

    The lower Riemann integral of f is

    l⁢(f)=sup⁡{l𝒟⁢(f):𝒟 any dissection of [a,b]},

    and the upper Riemann integral of f is

    u⁢(f)=inf⁡{u𝒟⁢(f):𝒟 any dissection of [a,b]}.
  • •
    ​

    f is Riemann integrable if l⁢(f)=u⁢(f), and in this case we denote their common value by

    ∫abf

    and call it the Riemann integral of f (on, or over, [a,b]).

  • •
    ​

    A bounded function f:[a,b]→ℝ is Riemann integrable if and only if there exists some sequence (𝒟n) of dissections of [a,b] such that l𝒟n⁢(f)-u𝒟n⁢(f)→0 (and, in this case, ∫abf=lim⁡l𝒟n⁢(f)=lim⁡u𝒟n⁢(f)).

  • •
    ​

    We used this theorem to prove that all continuous functions, and all monotonic functions are Riemann integrable. We also used it to prove that

    ∫abf+∫bcf=∫acf,∫ab(α⁢f+β⁢g)=α⁢∫abf+β⁢∫abg,and  |∫abf|≤∫ab|f|

    where α,β are constants.
