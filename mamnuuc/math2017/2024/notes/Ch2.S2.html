The rules of differentiation

2.2 The rules of differentiation

In principle, if we are given an explicit function, like

f:ℝ→ℝ,f⁢(x)=(x3+1)2,f:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad f(x)=(x^{3}+1)^{2},

we can show it is differentiable and compute its derivative by directly applying Definition 2.1. This quickly becomes complicated and tedious, however. So we next develop some “derivative theorems” which will allow us to reduce differentiation of many functions to an algorithmic process. You are (I would hope) already familiar with these and quite fluent in applying them. The point of this section is to show that, now we have a mathematically precise definition of the derivative, we can rigorously prove them, thus putting differential calculus on a solid foundation.

Proposition 2.10 (Linearity)

Let f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, g:D→ℝg:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be differentiable at a∈Da\in D with derivatives f′⁢(a)f^{\prime}(a) and g′⁢(a)g^{\prime}(a), respectively, and cc be a real constant. Then

  1. (i)
    ​

    c⁢f:D→ℝcf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is differentiable at aa with derivative c⁢f′⁢(a)cf^{\prime}(a).

  2. (ii)
    ​

    f+g:D→ℝf+g:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is differentiable at aa with derivative f′⁢(a)+g′⁢(a)f^{\prime}(a)+g^{\prime}(a).

Proof.

These both follow immediately from the Algebra of Limits (Theorem 1.35):

limx→ac⁢f⁢(x)−c⁢f⁢(a)x−a\displaystyle\lim_{x\rightarrow a}\frac{cf(x)-cf(a)}{x-a} =\displaystyle= limx→ac⁢(f⁢(x)−f⁢(a)x−a)=c⁢f′⁢(a)\displaystyle\lim_{x\rightarrow a}c\left(\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\right)=cf^{% \prime}(a)
limx→af⁢(x)+g⁢(x)−(f⁢(a)+g⁢(a))x−a\displaystyle\lim_{x\rightarrow a}\frac{f(x)+g(x)-(f(a)+g(a))}{x-a} =\displaystyle= limx→a(f⁢(x)−f⁢(a)x−a+g⁢(x)−g⁢(a)x−a)=f′⁢(a)+g′⁢(a)\displaystyle\lim_{x\rightarrow a}\left(\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}+\frac{g(x)-g(a)}% {x-a}\right)=f^{\prime}(a)+g^{\prime}(a)

∎

Remark Actually the first part of this proof assumes that limx→ac=c\displaystyle\lim_{x\rightarrow a}c=c. Have we actually proved this? Not directly. (You should compose a direct ε\varepsilon–δ\delta proof in your head now. For a given ε>0\varepsilon>0, how small must we take δ\delta?) We have proved it indirectly however: constant functions are differentiable (Example 2.2), hence continuous (Proposition 2.8), so equal their limit at each point (Theorem 1.43).

Proposition 2.10 followed easily from the Algebra of Limits. Our next differentiation rule, and its proof, are considerably more subtle. Recall that the composition of two functions f:A→Bf:A\rightarrow B and g:B→Cg:B\rightarrow C, denoted g∘f:A→Cg\circ f:A\rightarrow C is the function which first “does” ff, then feeds the result into gg, that is

(g∘f)⁢(x)=g⁢(f⁢(x)).(g\circ f)(x)=g(f(x)).

This only makes sense if the range of ff is a subset of the domain of gg. We’ve already proved that the composition of two continuous functions is continuous (Theorem 1.46). There’s a very famous rule which tells us how to compute the derivative of a composition of two functions, if we know the derivatives of its component pieces.

Theorem 2.11 (Chain Rule)

Let f:D→Ef:D\rightarrow E be differentiable at a∈Da\in D and g:E→ℝg:E\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be differentiable at f⁢(a)∈Ef(a)\in E. Then g∘f:D→ℝg\circ f:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is differentiable at aa, and

(g∘f)′⁢(a)=g′⁢(f⁢(a))⁢f′⁢(a).(g\circ f)^{\prime}(a)=g^{\prime}(f(a))f^{\prime}(a).

To prove this, we will need to slightly alter the way we think of the derivative. The idea is that, if a function g:ℝ\{a}→ℝg:{\mathbb{R}}{\backslash}\{a\}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} has limit LL at aa, we can extend the function to the domain ℝ{\mathbb{R}} by defining g⁢(a)=Lg(a)=L, and this extended function is continuous at aa by Theorem 1.43. Conversely, if the function g:ℝ\{a}→ℝg:{\mathbb{R}}{\backslash}\{a\}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} has a continuous extension to ℝ{\mathbb{R}}, it has a limit at aa and that limit is g⁢(a)g(a). So we can reinterpret “gg has a limit at aa” as saying that “gg has an extension which is continuous at aa.” Applying this idea to the difference quotient of a function ff,

g⁢(x):=f⁢(x)−f⁢(a)x−a,g(x):=\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a},

we obtain Carathéodory’s criterion for differentiablity:

Proposition 2.12 (Carathéodory’s Criterion)

Let f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} and a∈Da\in D be a cluster point of DD. Then ff is differentiable at a∈Da\in D if and only if there exists a function ϕ:D→ℝ\phi:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} that is continuous at aa and satisfies

f⁢(x)−f⁢(a)=ϕ⁢(x)⁢(x−a)(♣)\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad f(x)-f(a)=\phi(x)(x-a)\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad(\clubsuit)

for all x∈Dx\in D. In this case, f′⁢(a)=ϕ⁢(a)f^{\prime}(a)=\phi(a).

Proof.

(⇒\Rightarrow): Assume ff is differentiable at aa with derivative f′⁢(a)f^{\prime}(a). Define the function

ϕ:D→ℝ,ϕ⁢(x)={f⁢(x)−f⁢(a)x−aif x≠af′⁢(a)if x=a.\phi:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad\phi(x)=\left\{\begin{array}[]{ll}\frac{f(% x)-f(a)}{x-a}&\mbox{if $x\neq a$}\\ f^{\prime}(a)&\mbox{if $x=a$.}\end{array}\right.

Clearly ϕ\phi satisifes (♣)(\clubsuit) for all x∈D\{a}x\in D{\backslash}\{a\}, and (♣)(\clubsuit) holds automatically at x=ax=a since both sides equal 0. Furthermore,

limx→aϕ⁢(x)=limx→af⁢(x)−f⁢(a)x−a=f′⁢(a)=ϕ⁢(a),\lim_{x\rightarrow a}\phi(x)=\lim_{x\rightarrow a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}=f^{% \prime}(a)=\phi(a),

so ϕ\phi is continuous at aa by Theorem 1.43.

(⇐\Leftarrow): Assume that a function continuous at aa satisfying (♣)(\clubsuit) exists. Then, dividing (♣)(\clubsuit) by (x−a)(x-a),

limx→af⁢(x)−f⁢(a)x−a=limx→aϕ⁢(x)=ϕ⁢(a)\lim_{x\rightarrow a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}=\lim_{x\rightarrow a}\phi(x)=\phi(a)

by Theorem 1.43, since ϕ\phi is continuous at aa. Hence, ff is differentiable at aa, with derivative ϕ⁢(a)\phi(a). ∎

Proof of the Chain Rule:  By Carathéodory’s Criterion, there exist functions ϕ:D→ℝ\phi:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} and ψ:E→ℝ\psi:E\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} such that ϕ\phi is continuous at aa, ψ\psi is continuous at f⁢(a)f(a) and, for all x∈Dx\in D, and y∈Ey\in E,

ϕ⁢(x)⁢(x−a)=f⁢(x)−f⁢(a),ψ⁢(y)⁢(y−f⁢(a))=g⁢(y)−g⁢(f⁢(a)).\phi(x)(x-a)={f(x)-f(a)},\qquad\psi(y)(y-f(a))={g(y)-{g(f(a))}}.

Define the function

Φ:D→ℝ,Φ⁢(x)=ψ⁢(f⁢(x))⁢ϕ⁢(x).\Phi:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad\Phi(x)=\psi(f(x))\phi(x).

By Theorem 1.46 (and the Algebra of Limits), Φ\Phi is continuous at aa. Furthermore, for all x∈Dx\in D,

Φ⁢(x)⁢(x−a)\displaystyle\Phi(x)(x-a) =\displaystyle= ψ⁢(f⁢(x))⁢ϕ⁢(x)⁢(x−a)\displaystyle\psi(f(x))\phi(x)(x-a)
=\displaystyle= ψ⁢(f⁢(x))⁢(f⁢(x)−f⁢(a))\displaystyle\psi(f(x))(f(x)-f(a))
=\displaystyle= g⁢(f⁢(x))−g⁢(f⁢(a)).\displaystyle g(f(x))-g(f(a)).

Hence, by Carathéodory’s Criterion, g∘fg\circ f is differentiable at aa, and its derivative is

Φ⁢(a)=ψ⁢(f⁢(a))⁢ϕ⁢(a)=g′⁢(f⁢(a))⁢f′⁢(a).\Phi(a)=\psi(f(a))\phi(a)=g^{\prime}(f(a))f^{\prime}(a).

□\Box

Example 2.13

Let f:ℝ→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} such that f⁢(x)=(x2+1)2f(x)=(x^{2}+1)^{2}. Then ff is differentiable and f′⁢(x)=4⁢(x2+1)⁢xf^{\prime}(x)=4(x^{2}+1)x.

Proof.

f=h∘gf=h\circ g where h,g:ℝ→ℝh,g:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}

g⁢(x)\displaystyle g(x) =\displaystyle= x2+1\displaystyle x^{2}+1
h⁢(x)\displaystyle h(x) =\displaystyle= x2.\displaystyle x^{2}.

Now, by Examples 2.4 and 2.2 and the Linearity Property (Proposition 2.10) g,hg,h are differentiable, with derivatives

g′⁢(x)\displaystyle g^{\prime}(x) =\displaystyle= 2⁢x+0\displaystyle 2x+0
h′⁢(x)\displaystyle h^{\prime}(x) =\displaystyle= 2⁢x.\displaystyle 2x.

Hence, by the Chain Rule, ff is differentiable, and

f′⁢(x)=h′⁢(g⁢(x))⁢g′⁢(x)=2⁢g⁢(x)⁢2⁢x=4⁢(x2+1)⁢x.f^{\prime}(x)=h^{\prime}(g(x))g^{\prime}(x)=2g(x)2x=4(x^{2}+1)x.

∎

Remark You are probably more familiar with the Chain Rule expressed something like the following:

d⁢yd⁢x=d⁢yd⁢ud⁢ud⁢x.(*)\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy}{du}\frac{du}{dx}.\qquad\qquad% \qquad\qquad(*)

Certainly, this has a look of plausibility about it (don’t the “d⁢udu’s” just cancel?) and is arguably more memorable than

(g∘f)′(a)=g′(f(a))f′(a).(†)\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad(g\circ f)^{\prime}(a)=g^{\prime}(f(a))f^{\prime}(a).% \qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad(\dagger)

Nonetheless, it is vastly inferior. Ask yourself what, exactly, does (*)(*) mean? The derivative of a function yy (where? At xx?) equals the derivative (where? At uu?) of the same (?) function – but with respect to a different variable, whatever that means — times the derivative of some other function uu (but isn’t uu a variable? Can we use the same symbol to denote both a function and a variable?) evaluated somewhere (xx? Maybe?). Note that “the function yy thought of as a function of uu instead of xx,” where “uu is a function of xx,” is actually a different function! And to interpret (*)(*) we’re forced to talk about how we think about the function yy, instead of what it actually is. This is the hallmark of imprecise, badly formulated mathematics.

By contrast (†)(\dagger) is a completely unambiguous, precise statement standing on its own. We don’t need to explain separately “how we’re thinking of” the terms it contains in order to make sense of it. Be warned: if ever I ask for a statement of the Chain Rule, it is Theorem 2.11 I want, not wooly, ambiguous stuff in the style of (*)(*). □\Box

You may have expected us to prove the Product Rule before the Chain Rule: it is, after all, considerably simpler. The reason we proved the Chain Rule first is that we can deduce the Product Rule from it.

Proposition 2.15 (Product Rule)

Let f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, g:D→ℝg:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be differentiable at a∈Da\in D with derivatives f′⁢(a)f^{\prime}(a) and g′⁢(a)g^{\prime}(a), respectively. Then f⁢g:D→ℝfg:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is differentiable at aa with derivative f′⁢(a)⁢g⁢(a)+f⁢(a)⁢g′⁢(a)f^{\prime}(a)g(a)+f(a)g^{\prime}(a).

Proof.

Let s:ℝ→ℝs:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} such that s⁢(x)=x2s(x)=x^{2} and recall that this is differentiable with derivative s′⁢(x)=2⁢xs^{\prime}(x)=2x by Example 2.4. Hence, by the Chain Rule and the Linearity Property, s∘fs\circ f, s∘gs\circ g and s∘(f+g)s\circ(f+g) are all differentiable at aa, with derivatives

(s∘f)′⁢(a)\displaystyle(s\circ f)^{\prime}(a) =\displaystyle= 2⁢f⁢(a)⁢f′⁢(a)\displaystyle 2f(a)f^{\prime}(a)
(s∘g)′⁢(a)\displaystyle(s\circ g)^{\prime}(a) =\displaystyle= 2⁢g⁢(a)⁢g′⁢(a)\displaystyle 2g(a)g^{\prime}(a)
(s∘(f+g))′⁢(a)\displaystyle(s\circ(f+g))^{\prime}(a) =\displaystyle= 2⁢(f⁢(a)+g⁢(a))⁢(f′⁢(a)+g′⁢(a)).\displaystyle 2(f(a)+g(a))(f^{\prime}(a)+g^{\prime}(a)).

Hence, by Proposition 2.10,

f⁢g=12⁢{(f+g)2−f2−g2}=12⁢{s∘(f+g)−s∘f−s∘g}fg=\frac{1}{2}\left\{(f+g)^{2}-f^{2}-g^{2}\right\}=\frac{1}{2}\{s\circ(f+g)-s% \circ f-s\circ g\}

is differentiable at aa with derivative

(f⁢g)′⁢(a)\displaystyle(fg)^{\prime}(a) =\displaystyle= 12⁢{2⁢(f⁢(a)+g⁢(a))⁢(f′⁢(a)+g′⁢(a))−2⁢f⁢(a)⁢f′⁢(a)−2⁢g⁢(a)⁢g′⁢(a)}\displaystyle\frac{1}{2}\{2(f(a)+g(a))(f^{\prime}(a)+g^{\prime}(a))-2f(a)f^{% \prime}(a)-2g(a)g^{\prime}(a)\}
=\displaystyle= f′⁢(a)⁢g⁢(a)+f⁢(a)⁢g′⁢(a).\displaystyle f^{\prime}(a)g(a)+f(a)g^{\prime}(a).

∎

Having established the Linearity Property and the Product Rule, it’s not hard to prove that all polynomial functions are differentiable, with the derivative we expect.

Proposition 2.17

Every polynomial function p:ℝ→ℝp:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, p⁢(x)=a0+a1⁢x+⋯+am⁢xmp(x)=a_{0}+a_{1}x+\cdots+a_{m}x^{m} is differentiable, and its derivative is another polynomial function p′:ℝ→ℝp^{\prime}:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, namely

p′⁢(x)=a1+2⁢a2⁢x+⋯+m⁢am⁢xm−1.p^{\prime}(x)=a_{1}+2a_{2}x+\cdots+ma_{m}x^{m-1}.
Proof.

Example 2.18

Let mm be a positive integer and f:ℝ\{0}→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}{\backslash}\{0\}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be the function f⁢(x)=1/xmf(x)=1/x^{m}. Then ff is differentiable, and f′⁢(x)=−m/xm+1f^{\prime}(x)=-m/x^{m+1}.

Proof.

f=h∘gf=h\circ g where g:ℝ\{0}→ℝg:{\mathbb{R}}{\backslash}\{0\}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, g⁢(x)=1/xg(x)=1/x and h:ℝ→ℝh:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, h⁢(x)=xmh(x)=x^{m}. By Examples 2.5 and 2.17, both gg and hh are differentiable with derivatives

g′⁢(x)\displaystyle g^{\prime}(x) =\displaystyle= −1x2\displaystyle-\frac{1}{x^{2}}
h′⁢(x)\displaystyle h^{\prime}(x) =\displaystyle= m⁢xm−1.\displaystyle mx^{m-1}.

Hence, by the Chain Rule, ff is diffentiable, and

f′⁢(x)=h′⁢(g⁢(x))⁢g′⁢(x)=m⁢g⁢(x)m−1⁢(−1x2)=−m⁢(1x)m−1⁢(1x)2=−mxm+1f^{\prime}(x)=h^{\prime}(g(x))g^{\prime}(x)=mg(x)^{m-1}\left(-\frac{1}{x^{2}}% \right)=-m\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)^{m-1}\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)^{2}=-\frac{m}% {x^{m+1}}

by Example 2.5 and Proposition 2.17. ∎

Generalizing the trick used in this proof, we can obtain another useful rule of differentiation:

Proposition 2.19 (Quotient Rule)

Let f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} and g:D→ℝ\{0}g:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}{\backslash}\{0\}, be differentiable at a∈Da\in D. Then f/gf/g is differentiable at aa and

(fg)′⁢(a)=f′⁢(a)⁢g⁢(a)−f⁢(a)⁢g′⁢(a)g⁢(a)2.\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)^{\prime}(a)=\frac{f^{\prime}(a)g(a)-f(a)g^{\prime}(a)% }{g(a)^{2}}.
Proof.

So we now know that the function f⁢(x)=xkf(x)=x^{k} is differentiable for all integers kk (everywhere it is well-defined, i.e. everywhere except 0 if k<0k<0), and that its derivative is f′⁢(x)=k⁢xk−1f^{\prime}(x)=kx^{k-1}. We can also define f:(0,∞)→ℝf:(0,\infty)\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, f⁢(x)=xrf(x)=x^{r} where rr is any rational number: if r=p/qr=p/q, we define xr=(x1/q)px^{r}=(x^{1/q})^{p}. It turns out that this function is also differentiable, with the expected derivative:

Proposition 2.20

Let rr be a rational number and f:(0,∞)→ℝf:(0,\infty)\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be the function f⁢(x)=xrf(x)=x^{r}. Then ff is differentiable and

f′⁢(x)=r⁢xr−1.f^{\prime}(x)=rx^{r-1}.
Proof.

Postponed. We’ll actually prove something more general once we’ve introduced (rigorously) the natural logarithm. ∎

It may occur to you that one could prove Proposition 2.20 by arguing as follows. Let r=p/qr=p/q where p,q∈ℤ+p,q\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+}, and define g⁢(x)=f⁢(x)q=xpg(x)=f(x)^{q}=x^{p}. Then this is certainly differentiable, with derivative p⁢xp−1px^{p-1}. Hence, by the Chain Rule,

p⁢xp−1\displaystyle px^{p-1} =\displaystyle= q⁢f⁢(x)q−1⁢f′⁢(x)\displaystyle qf(x)^{q-1}f^{\prime}(x)
⇒f′⁢(x)\displaystyle\Rightarrow\qquad f^{\prime}(x) =\displaystyle= p⁢xp−1q⁢(xp/q)q−1=pq⁢xp−1−p⁢(q−1)q\displaystyle\frac{px^{p-1}}{q(x^{p/q})^{q-1}}=\frac{p}{q}x^{p-1-\frac{p(q-1)}% {q}}
=\displaystyle= pq⁢xpq−1=r⁢xr−1.\displaystyle\frac{p}{q}x^{\frac{p}{q}-1}=rx^{r-1}.

The only thing wrong with this “proof” is that, in applying the Chain Rule (to F=h∘fF=h\circ f where h⁢(x)=xqh(x)=x^{q}) you are presupposing that ff is differentiable, that is, you’re assuming what you want to prove! So all this argument really shows is that if ff is differentiable, then its derivative must be f′⁢(x)=r⁢xr−1f^{\prime}(x)=rx^{r-1}.

Note that the claimed formula is certainly true for r=1/2r=1/2 (see Exercise 2.6).
