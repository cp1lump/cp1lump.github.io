1.3 Limits of functions

1.3.1 Limits at infinity

Note it’s not essential in Definition 1.1 that the quantity NN is a positive integer. We could just as have well decided that

limn→∞an=L\displaystyle{\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}a_{n}=L} if, for each ε>0\varepsilon>0, there exists K∈ℝK\in{\mathbb{R}} such that, for all n>Kn>K, |an−L|<ε|a_{n}-L|<\varepsilon.

Both say that, given any positive number ε\varepsilon (no matter how small), there’s a point in the sequence after which all terms lie within distance ε\varepsilon of LL. We just use different means (a positive integer NN or a real number KK) to specify that point.

Having realized this, we see that the definition can be extended immediately to deal with functions of a real variable, not just sequences.

Definition 1.22

Let D⊆ℝD\subseteq{\mathbb{R}} be unbounded above and f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}. Then ff has limit LL at infinity if, for each ε>0\varepsilon>0, there exists K∈ℝK\in{\mathbb{R}} such that, for all x∈Dx\in D with x>Kx>K, |f⁢(x)−L|<ε|f(x)-L|<\varepsilon. The shorthand for this is limx→∞f⁢(x)=L\displaystyle{\lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}f(x)=L}.

Example 1.23

Let f:ℝ→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, f⁢(x)=(1−x2)/(1+x2)f(x)=(1-x^{2})/(1+x^{2}). Claim: limx→∞f⁢(x)=−1\displaystyle{\lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}f(x)=-1}.

Proof.

Let ε>0\varepsilon>0 be given. Let K=2εK=\displaystyle\sqrt{\frac{2}{\varepsilon}}. Then, for all x>Kx>K,

|f⁢(x)−(−1)|\displaystyle|f(x)-(-1)| =\displaystyle= |1−x21+x2+1|\displaystyle\left|\frac{1-x^{2}}{1+x^{2}}+1\right|
=\displaystyle= 21+x2\displaystyle\frac{2}{1+x^{2}}
<\displaystyle< 2x2\displaystyle\frac{2}{x^{2}}
<\displaystyle< 2K2=ε\displaystyle\frac{2}{K^{2}}=\varepsilon

∎

This makes precise the informal idea that f⁢(x)f(x) gets “arbitrarily close” to LL as xx gets “sufficiently large.” Note we must demand that DD, the domain of ff, is unbounded above, or else there will exist some real number KK such that there are no values of x∈Dx\in D with x>Kx>K. 1.3.2 Cluster points

Limits at infinity are quite useful, but for the purposes of calculus, we really need to make proper sense of limits like

limx→af⁢(x)\lim_{x\rightarrow a}f(x)

where aa is a real number. For example,

f′⁢(a)=limx→af⁢(x)−f⁢(a)x−a.f^{\prime}(a)=\lim_{x\rightarrow a}\frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}.

Our first task is to clearly define the kind of point a∈ℝa\in{\mathbb{R}} at which it makes sense to define the limit of a given function ff. Informally, we know that the limit “as xx tends to aa” concerns the behaviour of f⁢(x)f(x) “sufficiently close” to (but not at) aa. Just as limx→∞f⁢(x)\displaystyle{\lim_{x\rightarrow\infty}f(x)} only makes sense if the domain of ff is unbounded above (so that f⁢(x)f(x) is well-defined for xx arbitrarily large), limx→af⁢(x)\displaystyle{\lim_{x\rightarrow a}f(x)} only makes sense if the domain of ff contains points which are “arbitrarily close” to (but different from) aa.

Definition 1.26

Let D⊆ℝD\subseteq{\mathbb{R}}. Then a∈ℝa\in{\mathbb{R}} is a cluster point of DD if, for each ε>0\varepsilon>0, there exists x∈Dx\in D with 0<|x−a|<ε0<|x-a|<\varepsilon. Equivalently: for each ε>0\varepsilon>0, the set (D\{a})∩(a−ε,a+ε)(D{\backslash}\{a\})\cap(a-\varepsilon,a+\varepsilon) is nonempty.

1.3.3 Limits of functions: the main definition

Cluster points of the domain of a function are exactly the points at which it makes sense to define a limit of the function.

Definition 1.29

Let D⊆ℝD\subseteq{\mathbb{R}}, f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} and a∈ℝa\in{\mathbb{R}} be a cluster point of DD. Then ff has limit LL at aa if, for each ε>0\varepsilon>0, there exists δ>0\delta>0 such that, for all x∈Dx\in D with 0<|x−a|<δ0<|x-a|<\delta, |f⁢(x)−L|<ε|f(x)-L|<\varepsilon. The shorthand for this is limx→af⁢(x)=L\displaystyle{\lim_{x\rightarrow a}f(x)=L}.

Example 1.30

Let f:ℝ→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, f⁢(x)=x2f(x)=x^{2}. Claim: limx→2f⁢(x)=4\displaystyle{\lim_{x\rightarrow 2}f(x)=4}.

Proof.

Let ε>0\varepsilon>0 be given. Let δ=min⁡{1,ε5}\delta=\min\{1,\frac{\varepsilon}{5}\}.
Then, for all x∈ℝx\in{\mathbb{R}} with 0<|x−2|<δ0<|x-2|<\delta,

|f⁢(x)−4|\displaystyle|f(x)-4| =\displaystyle= |x2−4|\displaystyle|x^{2}-4|
=\displaystyle= |x+2|⁢|x−2|\displaystyle|x+2||x-2|
≤\displaystyle\leq 5⁢|x−2|(since δ≤1 so x∈(1,3))\displaystyle 5|x-2|\qquad\mbox{(since $\delta\leq 1$ so $x\in(1,3)$)}
<\displaystyle< 5⁢δ\displaystyle 5\delta
≤\displaystyle\leq ε\displaystyle\varepsilon

∎

The definition says that, given any positive number ε\varepsilon (no matter how small), we can find another positive number δ\delta which is so small that, whenever xx is within distance δ\delta of aa, and different from aa, f⁢(x)f(x) is within distance ε\varepsilon of LL. This makes precise the informal idea that f⁢(x)f(x) is “arbitrarily close” to LL for all xx “sufficiently close” to, but different from, aa.

1.3.4 Some basic properties of limits

Since we have a precise definition of limits, we are in a position to prove some basic facts about them.

Theorem 1.34 (Uniqueness of limits)

If ff has a limit at aa, this limit is unique.

Proof.

Let f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} and aa be a cluster point of DD. Assume, towards a contradiction, that both L1L_{1} and L2≠L1L_{2}\neq L_{1} satisfy the definition of limit of ff at aa. Let ε=|L2−L1|/2>0\varepsilon=|L_{2}-L_{1}|/2>0. So it makes sense to talk about the limit of a function at aa. 1.3.5 Continuity and limits

You may have been told (by some shameless charlatan) that a function is continuous “if you can draw its graph without taking your pen off the paper.” It should by now be clear that this is not an acceptable mathematical definition. What if you have no pen, or paper, or hands? What if you’re simply not very good at drawing? Does the continuity of a function depend on your skill as a draughtsman? Clearly this is utter piffle. A mathematical definition should be formulated only in terms of the object being defined, not how we are able to interact with (or think about) it.

Luckily, we can use convergence of sequences to give a precise (and rather elegant) definition of continuity for functions:

Definition 1.38

Let f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} and a∈Da\in D. Then ff is continuous at aa if, for all sequences (xn)(x_{n}) in DD such that xn→ax_{n}\rightarrow a, f⁢(xn)→f⁢(a)f(x_{n})\rightarrow f(a). ff is continuous if it is continuous at aa for all a∈Da\in D. If ff is not continuous, it is discontinuous.

For example, all polynomial functions

f:ℝ→ℝ,f⁢(x)=a0+a1⁢x+⋯+ak⁢xkf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad f(x)=a_{0}+a_{1}x+\cdots+a_{k}x^{k}

are continuous. This follows from the Algebra of Limits for sequences and the rather obvious fact that f⁢(x)=xf(x)=x is continuous.22 2 Proof: If xn→ax_{n}\rightarrow a then f⁢(xn)=xn→a=f⁢(a)f(x_{n})=x_{n}\rightarrow a=f(a).

A step function is discontinuous at a single point. Somewhat counterintuitively, it’s possible for a function f:ℝ→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} to be continuous at only a single point:

Example 1.40

Let f:ℝ→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be such that f⁢(x)=xf(x)=x if x∈ℚx\in{\mathbb{Q}} and f⁢(x)=0f(x)=0 if x∉ℚx\notin{\mathbb{Q}}. Claim: ff is continuous as 0 and discontinuous everywhere else.

Proof.

Assume that ff is continuous at aa. Let (rn)(r_{n}) be any rational sequence converging to aa. Then f⁢(rn)=rn→af(r_{n})=r_{n}\rightarrow a. Let (in)(i_{n}) be any irrational sequence converging to aa. Then f⁢(in)=0→0f(i_{n})=0\rightarrow 0. But ff is continuous at aa, so f⁢(rn)→f⁢(a)f(r_{n})\rightarrow f(a) and f⁢(in)→f⁢(a)f(i_{n})\rightarrow f(a). Hence f⁢(a)=af(a)=a and f⁢(a)=0f(a)=0, that is a=0a=0. So if ff is continuous at aa then a=0a=0. That is, ff is discontinuous at every a≠0a\neq 0.

It remains to show that ff is continuous at 0 (careful: we have not proved this yet!). Let (xn)(x_{n}) be any sequence converging to 0. Then

|f⁢(xn)|={|xn|,if xn∈ℚ,0,if xn∉ℚ,}≤|xn|→0|f(x_{n})|=\left\{\begin{array}[]{cc}|x_{n}|,&\mbox{if $x_{n}\in{\mathbb{Q}}$,% }\\ 0,&\mbox{if $x_{n}\notin{\mathbb{Q}}$,}\end{array}\right\}\leq|x_{n}|\rightarrow 0

so f⁢(xn)→0=f⁢(0)f(x_{n})\rightarrow 0=f(0) by the Squeeze Rule. Hence ff is continuous at 0. ∎

Here’s an even more counterintuitive example: a function f:ℝ→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} which is continuous at every irrational number, but discontinuous at every rational number! It was invented by Carl Johannes Thomae but, in the anglosphere, is usually referred to by the (somewhat mysterious) name given to it by John Horton Conway:

Example 1.41 (Stars Over Babylon)

Let f:ℝ→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be the function which maps every irrational number to 0, and maps each rational number p/qp/q, expressed in lowest terms (that is, pp and qq have no common divisors other than 11), to 1/q1/q. It’s not hard to show that every function f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is automatically continuous at aa if aa is not a cluster point of DD.

Proposition 1.42

Let f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} and assume a∈Da\in D is not a cluster point of DD. Then ff is continuous at aa.

Proof.

Let (xn)(x_{n}) be any sequence in DD converging to aa. Since aa is not a cluster point of DD, there exists ε>0\varepsilon>0 such that no element of DD has 0<|x−a|<ε0<|x-a|<\varepsilon. Hence, if |x−a|<ε|x-a|<\varepsilon and x∈Dx\in D, then x=ax=a. Now xn→ax_{n}\rightarrow a, so there exists N∈ℤ+N\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} such that, for all n≥Nn\geq N, |xn−a|<ε|x_{n}-a|<\varepsilon and hence xn=ax_{n}=a. Hence, for all n≥Nn\geq N, f⁢(xn)=f⁢(a)f(x_{n})=f(a), that is, the sequence (f⁢(xn))(f(x_{n})) is constant and equals f⁢(a)f(a), for all n≥Nn\geq N. Hence f⁢(xn)→f⁢(a)f(x_{n})\rightarrow f(a). ∎

So in considering whether a function is continuous, we may restrict attention only to cluster points of (and in) its domain. For such points we have two useful ways to reformulate continuity:

    ff is continuous at aa;

Many mathematicians take condition (iii) in Theorem 1.43 as the definition of continuity at aa, instead of the sequential definition, Definition 1.38. It’s important to be able to use both. Here’s another result which follows very quickly from the sequential definition of continuity (the composition of two continuous functions is continuous):

Theorem 1.46

Let DD and EE be subsets of ℝ{\mathbb{R}}, f:D→Ef:D\rightarrow E, g:E→ℝg:E\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, ff be continuous at aa and gg be continuous at f⁢(a)f(a). Then g∘f:D→ℝg\circ f:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is continuous at aa.

Since ff is continuous at aa, yn:=f⁢(xn)→f⁢(a)y_{n}:=f(x_{n})\rightarrow f(a). Hence, since gg is continuous at f⁢(a)f(a), g⁢(yn)→g⁢(f⁢(a))g(y_{n})\rightarrow g(f(a)). Hence g⁢(f⁢(xn))→g⁢(f⁢(a))g(f(x_{n}))\rightarrow g(f(a)). ∎

We finish this chapter by recalling two famous and important theorems about continuous functions on a closed, bounded interval,

[a,b]={x∈ℝ:a≤x≤b}.[a,b]=\{x\in{\mathbb{R}}\>:\>a\leq x\leq b\}.
Theorem 1.48 (Intermediate Value Theorem)

Let f:[a,b]→ℝf:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be continuous and yy be any number between f⁢(a)f(a) and f⁢(b)f(b). Then there exists c∈[a,b]c\in[a,b] such that f⁢(c)=yf(c)=y.

Theorem 1.49 (Extreme Value Theorem)

Let f:[a,b]→ℝf:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be continuous. Then ff is bounded (above and below) and attains both a minimum and a maximum value.

These two Theorems may look obvious, but it’s important to realize that we can (and, in MATH1026, did) prove them rigorously from Definitions 1.38 and 1.1. The whole point of Real Analysis is to go beyond intuitive, hand waving reasoning, to back up all our assertions with precise, rigorous reasoning. After all, as we shall see, not everything that is “obvious” is true!
