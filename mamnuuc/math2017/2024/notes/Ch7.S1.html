Convergence tests for series

7.1 Convergence tests for series

We begin with a brisk review of the basics concerning convergence of series. All this material was covered in detail in MATH1026. (The main ideas were covered in MATH1055, albeit with some of the proofs omitted.)

Recall that a series is a formal infinite sum

∑n=0∞an=a0+a1+a2+⋯,\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}=a_{0}+a_{1}+a_{2}+\cdots,

that its kthk^{\rm th} partial sum is the real number

sk:=∑n=0kan=a0+a1+⋯+ak,s_{k}:=\sum_{n=0}^{k}a_{n}=a_{0}+a_{1}+\cdots+a_{k},

and that the series converges precisely if the sequence (sk)(s_{k}) converges (in the sense of Definition 1.1). In this case, we also use ∑n=0∞an\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n} to denote its limit.

The series converges absolutely if

∑n=0∞|an|\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}|a_{n}|

converges. Absolute convergence implies convergence, but convergence does not imply absolute convergence. For example, the alternating harmonic series

∑n=1∞(−1)nn\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{n}}{n}

converges (see Example 3.23), but it does not converge absolutely since the harmonic series

∑n=1∞1n\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n}

diverges.

If a series ∑n=0∞an\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n} converges then its sequence of terms (an)(a_{n}) converges to 0. This fact is often called the Divergence Test since we can apply its contrapositive: if an↛0a_{n}\nrightarrow 0 then ∑an\sum a_{n} diverges. Note that the converse is false, as illustrated by the harmonic series: the sequence of terms an=1/n→0a_{n}=1/n\rightarrow 0, but the corresponding series ∑n=1∞an\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_{n} diverges.

If all the terms of a series are positive, an>0a_{n}>0, then the sequence of partial sums sk=∑n=0kans_{k}=\sum_{n=0}^{k}a_{n} is an increasing sequence,

sk+1=sk+ak+1>sk,s_{k+1}=s_{k}+a_{k+1}>s_{k},

so, by the Monotone Convergence Theorem, the series converges if (and only if) sks_{k} is bounded above. This observation allows one to establish several useful convergence tests applicable to the case an>0a_{n}>0. The first allows us to prove convergence (or divergence) of a series ∑an\sum a_{n} by comparing it with a (simpler) series ∑bn\sum b_{n} that we already know converges (or diverges).

Theorem 7.1 (The Comparison Test)

Let an>0a_{n}>0 and bn>0b_{n}>0.

  1. (i)
    ​

    If an/bna_{n}/b_{n} is bounded above and ∑bn\sum b_{n} converges, then ∑an\sum a_{n} converges.

  2. (ii)
    ​

    If bn/anb_{n}/a_{n} is bounded above and ∑bn\sum{b_{n}} diverges, then ∑an\sum a_{n} diverges.

Claim: ∑n=1∞nn2+1\displaystyle{\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{n^{2}+1}} diverges.

Proof.

Let an=n/(n2+1)a_{n}=n/(n^{2}+1) and bn=1/nb_{n}=1/n. Then

bnan=n2+1n2=1+1n2≤2,\frac{b_{n}}{a_{n}}=\frac{n^{2}+1}{n^{2}}=1+\frac{1}{n^{2}}\leq 2,

so the sequence (bn/an)(b_{n}/a_{n}) is bounded above. Since ∑n=1∞bn\displaystyle{\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}b_{n}} diverges, we conclude from part (ii) of the Comparison Test that s⁢u⁢mn=1∞⁢ansum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_{n} also diverges. ∎

Our next convergence test is perhaps the most useful – as well as being the easiest to use!

Theorem 7.5 (The Ratio Test)

Let an>0a_{n}>0 for all nn and an+1/an→La_{n+1}/a_{n}\rightarrow L. Then

  1. (i)
    ​

    if L<1L<1, the series ∑an\sum a_{n} converges,

  2. (ii)
    ​

    if L>1L>1, the series ∑an\sum a_{n} diverges.

Example 7.6

Claim: ∑n=1∞n50⁢(4950)n\displaystyle{\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}n^{50}\left(\frac{49}{50}\right)^{n}} converges.

Proof.

Let an=n50⁢(49/50)n>0a_{n}=n^{50}(49/50)^{n}>0, and note that

an+1an=(n+1)50n50⁢4950=(1+1n)50⁢4950→4950<1.\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_{n}}=\frac{(n+1)^{50}}{n^{50}}\frac{49}{50}=\left(1+\frac{1}{% n}\right)^{50}\frac{49}{50}\rightarrow\frac{49}{50}<1.

Hence, by the Ratio Test, ∑n=1∞an\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_{n} converges. ∎

Note that if the terms ana_{n} of your series aren’t all positive, you can always try applying the Comparison or Ratio Test to ∑|an|\sum|a_{n}| to determine whether ∑an\sum a_{n} converge absolutely. If it does, it certainly converges. Discarding all information about the sign of ana_{n} does lose us potentially vital knowledge, however. If the terms alternate in sign, we have a particularly neat convergence test:

Theorem 7.7 (The Alternating Series Test)

Let (an)(a_{n}) be a positive, decreasing sequence that converges to 0. Then

∑n=1∞(−1)n+1⁢an\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(-1)^{n+1}a_{n}

converges.

Example 7.8

The series ∑n=1∞(−1)n+1⁢1n\displaystyle{\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(-1)^{n+1}\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}} converges, by the Alternating Series Test, since an=1/na_{n}=1/\sqrt{n} is positive, decreasing and converges to 0. As usual with convergence tests, we’ve proved that the series converges but have no idea what its limit is.
