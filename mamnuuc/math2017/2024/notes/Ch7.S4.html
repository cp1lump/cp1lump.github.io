Differentiability of power series

7.4 Differentiability of power series

Given a power series f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn\displaystyle{f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n}} of radius of convergence RR, we know that ff is a continuous function on (−R,R)(-R,R). Is ff differentiable on (−R,R)(-R,R)? If it is, what is f′f^{\prime}? Your first guess is probably

f′⁢(x)=∑n=1∞n⁢an⁢xn−1,f^{\prime}(x)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}na_{n}x^{n-1},

the series obtained by differentiating ff term by term. Actually, this turns out to be absolutely correct, but proving it is not entirely trivial. Note that, in writing down our formula for f′f^{\prime} we have swapped the order of two limits (the limit defining the derivative, and the limit defining the series). We need to justify this. Also, how do we know that the proposed power series for f′⁢(x)f^{\prime}(x) even converges on (−R,R)(-R,R)? Maybe its radius of convergence is less than RR? Given the growth given by the extra factor of nn, this seems like a genuine worry.

So, our first task will be to prove that a power series and the obvious candidate for its derivative always have the same radius of convergence. Since we will use this “obvious candidate” series a lot, it’s helpful to develop some notation and a name for it.

Definition 7.20

Given a power series f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn\displaystyle{f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n}}, its termwise derivative is the power series

f^⁢(x)=∑n=1∞n⁢an⁢xn−1=∑n=0∞(n+1)⁢an+1⁢xn.\widehat{f}(x)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}na_{n}x^{n-1}=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(n+1)a_{n+1% }x^{n}.

Careful! At the moment, termwise derivative is just a name: we haven’t (yet) established that f^\widehat{f} is the derivative of ff.

Lemma 7.21

The power series f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn\displaystyle{f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n}} and its termwise derivative
f^⁢(x)=∑n=1∞n⁢an⁢xn−1\displaystyle{\widehat{f}(x)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}na_{n}x^{n-1}} have the same radius of convergence.

We now show that the termwise derivative f^⁢(x)\widehat{f}(x) really is the derivative of f⁢(x)f(x). We do this by applying Theorem 6.19 to the sequence of partial sums of f⁢(x)f(x). It’s a good idea to read the statement of Theorem 6.19 carefully before attempting to understand the next proof.

Theorem 7.22

Suppose that f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn\displaystyle{f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n}} has radius of convergence R>0R>0. Then ff is differentiable on (−R,R)(-R,R), and

f′⁢(x)=f^⁢(x):=∑n=1∞n⁢an⁢xn−1.f^{\prime}(x)=\widehat{f}(x):=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}na_{n}x^{n-1}.
Proof.

Choose and fix ρ∈(0,R)\rho\in(0,R), and consider the sequence of functions

fk:[−ρ,ρ]→ℝ,fk⁢(x)=∑n=0kan⁢xn.f_{k}:[-\rho,\rho]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad f_{k}(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{k}a_{n}x% ^{n}.

This is a sequence of continuously differentiable functions which converges pointwise to ff (Theorem 7.11), and its sequence of derivatives

fk′:[−ρ,ρ]→ℝ,fk′⁢(x)=∑n=1kn⁢an⁢xn−1f_{k}^{\prime}:[-\rho,\rho]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad f_{k}^{\prime}(x)=% \sum_{n=1}^{k}na_{n}x^{n-1}

converges uniformly to f^\widehat{f} (Lemma 7.21 and Theorem 7.18). Hence, by Theorem 6.19, ff is differentiable and f′=f^f^{\prime}=\widehat{f}. This holds for all x∈[−ρ,ρ]x\in[-\rho,\rho], but ρ∈(0,R)\rho\in(0,R) was arbitrary, so holds for all x∈(−R,R)x\in(-R,R). ∎

This is the most important theorem in this chapter. It says that the derivative of a power series exists on its open interval of convergence and is just the power series obtained by termwise differentiation.

Since f′⁢(x)f^{\prime}(x) is also a power series with radius of convergence RR, we can apply Theorem 7.22 to f′⁢(x)f^{\prime}(x) and deduce that f:(−R,R)→ℝf:(-R,R)\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is actually twice differentiable. Further, f′′⁢(x)f^{\prime\prime}(x) also has radius of convergence RR, so is differentiable on (−R,R)(-R,R), that is, ff is three times differentiable. In fact, we can keep applying Theorem 7.22 as often as we like, and we conclude that ff is smooth:

Corollary 7.23

Let f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xnf(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n} have radius convergence R>0R>0. Then f:(−R,R)→ℝf:(-R,R)\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is a smooth function, and its kt⁢hk^{th} derivative is given by the power series

f(k)⁢(x)=∑n=k∞n!(n−k)!⁢an⁢xn−k,f^{(k)}(x)=\sum_{n=k}^{\infty}\frac{n!}{(n-k)!}a_{n}x^{n-k},

which also has radius of convergence RR. In particular,

ak=f(k)⁢(0)k!a_{k}=\frac{f^{(k)}(0)}{k!}

for all k≥0k\geq 0.

Proof.

For each integer k≥0k\geq 0, define

gk⁢(x)=∑n=0∞(n+k)!n!⁢an+k⁢xn.g_{k}(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(n+k)!}{n!}a_{n+k}x^{n}.

I claim that each power series gk⁢(x)g_{k}(x) has radius of convergence RR and that, for all x∈(−R,R)x\in(-R,R), f(k)⁢(x)=gk⁢(x)f^{(k)}(x)=g_{k}(x). We prove this by induction on kk.

Certainly the claim holds for k=0k=0, since g0=fg_{0}=f. So, assume that the claim holds for some value k≥0k\geq 0, and consider gk+1g_{k+1}. Defining coefficients bn=(n+k)!n!⁢an+kb_{n}=\frac{(n+k)!}{n!}a_{n+k} so that gk⁢(x)=∑n=0∞bn⁢xng_{k}(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}b_{n}x^{n}, then

gk+1⁢(x)\displaystyle g_{k+1}(x) =\displaystyle= ∑n=0∞(n+k+1)!n!⁢an+k+1⁢xn=∑m=1∞(m+k)!(m−1)!⁢am+k⁢xm−1\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(n+k+1)!}{n!}a_{n+k+1}x^{n}=\sum_{m=1}^{% \infty}\frac{(m+k)!}{(m-1)!}a_{m+k}x^{m-1}
=\displaystyle= ∑m=1∞m⁢bm⁢xm−1.\displaystyle\sum_{m=1}^{\infty}mb_{m}x^{m-1}.

Hence, by Lemma 7.21, gk+1g_{k+1} has the same radius of convergence as gkg_{k}, and by Theorem 7.22, gk+1⁢(x)g_{k+1}(x) coincides with the derivative of gkg_{k}. But, by our induction hypothesis, gkg_{k} has radius of convergence RR and coincides with f(k)f^{(k)}, so gk+1g_{k+1} has radius of convergence RR and coincides with f(k+1)f^{(k+1)}. Hence, if the claim holds for some k≥0k\geq 0, it also holds for k+1k+1. Hence, by induction, the claim holds for all integers k≥0k\geq 0.

It follows that f(k)⁢(0)=gk⁢(0)=k!⁢akf^{(k)}(0)=g_{k}(0)=k!a_{k}, which completes the proof. ∎

Corollary 7.23 applies to any power series with non-zero radius of convergence so, in particular, it applies to the series defining exp\exp, sin\sin, and cos\cos.

Proposition 7.24

The functions exp:ℝ→ℝ\exp:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, sin:ℝ→ℝ\sin:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, cos:ℝ→ℝ\cos:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} defined in Definition 7.14 are smooth, and their derivatives are

exp′=exp,sin′=cos,cos′=−sin.\exp^{\prime}=\exp,\qquad\sin^{\prime}=\cos,\qquad\cos^{\prime}=-\sin.
Proof.

That these functions are smooth follows immediately from Corollary 7.23. Furthermore, by Theorem 7.22

exp′⁡(x)=∑n=1∞n⁢(1n!)⁢xn−1=∑n=1∞xn−1(n−1)!=∑m=0∞xmm!=exp⁡(x)\exp^{\prime}(x)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}n\left(\frac{1}{n!}\right)x^{n-1}=\sum_{n=% 1}^{\infty}\frac{x^{n-1}}{(n-1)!}=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\frac{x^{m}}{m!}=\exp(x)

for all x∈ℝx\in{\mathbb{R}}, so exp′=exp\exp^{\prime}=\exp. The formulae for sin′\sin^{\prime} and cos′\cos^{\prime} follow from similar arguments. ∎

With a bit of ingenuity, we can often use power series to exactly sum (that is, exactly compute the limit of) a given series. Here’s a simple example.

Example 7.25

Claim: ∑n=1∞n7n=736\displaystyle{\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{7^{n}}=\frac{7}{36}}.

Proof.

The geometric series f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞xnf(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}x^{n} has radius of convergence R=1R=1 and, for all x∈(−1,1)x\in(-1,1),

f⁢(x)=11−x.f(x)=\frac{1}{1-x}.

Hence, by Theorem 7.22, the power series

∑n=1∞n⁢xn−1\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}nx^{n-1}

also has radius of convergence R=1R=1 and, for all x∈(−1,1)x\in(-1,1) converges to

f′⁢(x)=1(1−x)2.f^{\prime}(x)=\frac{1}{(1-x)^{2}}.

Evaluating this at x=1/7x=1/7 yields

∑n=1∞n7n−1=f′⁢(1/7)=(76)2.\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{7^{n-1}}=f^{\prime}(1/7)=\left(\frac{7}{6}\right)^% {2}.

Hence

∑n=1∞n7n=17⁢∑n=1∞n7n−1=736.\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{7^{n}}=\frac{1}{7}\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{7^{n% -1}}=\frac{7}{36}.

∎
