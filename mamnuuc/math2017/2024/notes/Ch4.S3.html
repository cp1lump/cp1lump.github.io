‚à´abfor‚à´abf‚Å¢(x)‚Å¢ùëëx,\int_{a}^{b}f\quad\mbox{or}\quad\int_{a}^{b}f(x)dx,

Geometrically, we can think of l‚Å¢(f)l(f) as the least upper bound on the collection of all underestimates of the area under the curve y=f‚Å¢(x)y=f(x), and u‚Å¢(f)u(f) as the greatest lower bound on the collection of overestimates of the area under the curve. Then ‚à´abf\int_{a}^{b}f is, loosely, the unique number (where this exists) which is smaller than every overestimate and larger than every underestimate. As it stands, to compute this number, or even show that it exists, we need to consider the collection of all possible dissections of [a,b][a,b]. This is a very large and complicated set, so, before proceeding further, we need to develop some tools for handling l‚Å¢(f)l(f) and u‚Å¢(f)u(f) which allow us to avoid considering all possible dissections. The key idea is that of refinement of a dissection.

Definition 4.10

Given dissections ùíü,ùíü‚Ä≤\mathscr{D},\mathscr{D}^{\prime} of [a,b][a,b], we say that ùíü‚Ä≤\mathscr{D}^{\prime} is a refinement of ùíü\mathscr{D} if ùíü‚äÜùíü‚Ä≤\mathscr{D}\subseteq\mathscr{D}^{\prime}. Note that ùíü\mathscr{D} is the unique 0-point refinement of itself.

The idea is that a refinement ùíü‚Ä≤\mathscr{D}^{\prime} of ùíü\mathscr{D} contains all the points in ùíü\mathscr{D} and (unless ùíü‚Ä≤=ùíü\mathscr{D}^{\prime}=\mathscr{D}) some more points too, so it splits [a,b][a,b] up into more subintervals, at least some of which are narrower. Intuitively, one expects that passing from ùíü\mathscr{D} to a refinement of ùíü\mathscr{D} can only improve, that is, increase, the underestimate lùíü‚Å¢(f)l_{\mathscr{D}}(f). Similarly, passing to a refinement of ùíü\mathscr{D}, one expects, can only reduce the overestimate uùíü‚Å¢(f)u_{\mathscr{D}}(f). This expectation turns out to be essentially correct and is fundamental.
