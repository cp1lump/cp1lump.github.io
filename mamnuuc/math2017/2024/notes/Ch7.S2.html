Power series and their radius of convergence

7.2 Power series and their radius of convergence

A power series is a series of the form

∑n=0∞an⁢xn\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n}

where xx is interpreted as a real variable. The series depends on the choice of value for x∈ℝx\in{\mathbb{R}}. The terms of the series are an⁢xna_{n}x^{n}, and the kt⁢hk^{th} partial sum is the polynomial function

fk⁢(x)=∑n=0kan⁢xn=a0+a1⁢x+a2⁢x2+⋯+ak⁢xk,f_{k}(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{k}a_{n}x^{n}=a_{0}+a_{1}x+a_{2}x^{2}+\cdots+a_{k}x^{k},

just as for series in general. Note that, if x=0x=0, then every partial sum is fk⁢(0)=a0f_{k}(0)=a_{0}, so the series certainly converges (to a0a_{0}) in that case. In general, a power series may converge for some values of xx but diverge for others. If the series converges for a particular choice of xx, its limit will, in general, depend on xx. So a power series defines a real-valued function on some subset (perhaps all of) ℝ{\mathbb{R}}. The following example is fundamental (and, hopefully, familiar).

Example 7.9 (Geometric series)

Claim: The series

∑n=0∞xn\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}x^{n}

converges to 1/(1−x)1/(1-x) if |x|<1|x|<1, and diverges otherwise.

Proof.

If x=1x=1, the kk-th partial sum is sk=k+1s_{k}=k+1, and this sequence clearly diverges. If x≠1x\neq 1 then

sk\displaystyle s_{k} =\displaystyle= 1+x+x2+⋯⁢xk\displaystyle 1+x+x^{2}+\cdots x^{k}
⇒x⁢sk\displaystyle\Rightarrow\quad xs_{k} =\displaystyle= x+x2+x3+⋯+xk+1\displaystyle x+x^{2}+x^{3}+\cdots+x^{k+1}
⇒(1−x)⁢sk\displaystyle\Rightarrow\quad(1-x)s_{k} =\displaystyle= 1−xk+1\displaystyle 1-x^{k+1}
⇒sk\displaystyle\Rightarrow\quad s_{k} =\displaystyle= 1−xk+11−x.\displaystyle\frac{1-x^{k+1}}{1-x}.

The sequence xkx^{k} converges to 0 if |x|<1|x|<1 and diverges if x=−1x=-1. Hence, sk→1/(1−x)s_{k}\rightarrow 1/(1-x) if |x|<1|x|<1 and diverges otherwise. ∎

So if we use the power series to define a function

f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞xnf(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}x^{n}

then f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} where D=(−1,1)D=(-1,1) and for all x∈Dx\in D,

f⁢(x)=11−x.f(x)=\frac{1}{1-x}.
Definition 7.10

The radius of convergence of a power series ∑n=0∞an⁢xn\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n} is

R:=sup{|x|:∑n=0∞|an⁢xn| converges}.R:=\sup\{|x|\>:\>\mbox{$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}|a_{n}x^{n}|$ converges}\}.

If this set is unbounded above, we say that R=∞R=\infty.

The radius of convergence of a power series tells us almost everything about the subset of ℝ{\mathbb{R}} on which it converges:

Theorem 7.11

Let a power series have radius of convergence R>0R>0. Then the series converges absolutely for |x|<R|x|<R and diverges for |x|>R|x|>R.

This Theorem follows quite quickly once we have proved the following Lemma:

Lemma 7.12

Let ∑n=0∞an⁢xn\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n} converge at x=x1x=x_{1}. Then it converges absolutely for all xx such that |x|<|x1||x|<|x_{1}|.

Proof.

Since ∑an⁢x1n\sum a_{n}x_{1}^{n} converges, an⁢x1n→0a_{n}x_{1}^{n}\rightarrow 0 by the Divergence Test, and so is bounded: there exists K>0K>0 such that for all nn |an⁢x1n|<K|a_{n}x_{1}^{n}|<K. But then for all nn,

|an⁢xn|=|an⁢x1n|⁢|x|n|x1|n<K⁢(|x||x1|)n|a_{n}x^{n}|=|a_{n}x_{1}^{n}|\frac{|x|^{n}}{|x_{1}|^{n}}<K\left(\frac{|x|}{|x_% {1}|}\right)^{n}

so if |x|<|x1||x|<|x_{1}|, ∑n=0∞|an⁢xn|\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}|a_{n}x^{n}| converges by comparison with the convergent (geometric) series ∑n=0∞(|x|/|x1|)n\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(|x|/|x_{1}|)^{n}. ∎

If |x|<R|x|<R then there exists x1x_{1} with |x|<|x1|<R|x|<|x_{1}|<R such that ∑n=0∞an⁢x1n\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x_{1}^{n} is absolutely convergent (else RR isn’t the least upper bound on AA, |x||x| being smaller), hence convergent, so ∑n=0∞an⁢xn\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n} is absolutely convergent by Lemma 7.12.

If |x|>R|x|>R and ∑n=0∞an⁢xn\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n} converges then ∑n=0∞an⁢x2n\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x_{2}^{n}, where x2=(|x|+R)/2x_{2}=(|x|+R)/2, converges absolutely by Lemma 7.12, since |x2|<|x||x_{2}|<|x|. But |x2|>R|x_{2}|>R, a contradiction (RR isn’t an upper bound on AA at all!), so we conclude that ∑n=0∞an⁢xn\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n} diverges. □\Box

So the series converges on (−R,R)(-R,R) and diverges on (−∞,−R)∪(R,∞)(-\infty,-R)\cup(R,\infty). The only information not revealed by Theorem 7.11 is whether the series converges at x=±Rx=\pm R. So, it’s important to be able to compute radii of convergence. Luckily this can usually be achieved with a simple application of the Ratio Test.
