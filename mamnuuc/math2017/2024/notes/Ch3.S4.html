3.4 The Extended Mean Value Theorem and L’Hospital’s Rule

Sometimes a more general version of the Mean Value Theorem is useful.

Theorem 3.16 (Extended Mean Value Theorem)

Let ff and gg be real functions which are continuous on [a,b][a,b] and differentiable on (a,b)(a,b), and assume that, for all x∈(a,b)x\in(a,b), g′⁢(x)≠0g^{\prime}(x)\neq 0. Then there exists c∈(a,b)c\in(a,b) such that

f′⁢(c)g′⁢(c)=f⁢(b)−f⁢(a)g⁢(b)−g⁢(a).\frac{f^{\prime}(c)}{g^{\prime}(c)}=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{g(b)-g(a)}.

We can give a geometric interpretation to the Extended Mean Value Theorem similar to the interpretation of the ordinary Mean Value Theorem, but to do so we need to consider not the graph y=f⁢(x)y=f(x) but rather the parametrized curve with components (g,f)(g,f). That is, consider the map

γ:[a,b]→ℝ2,γ⁢(t)=(g⁢(t),f⁢(t)).\gamma:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}^{2},\qquad\gamma(t)=(g(t),f(t)).

The tangent line to γ\gamma at the point γ⁢(t)=(g⁢(t),f⁢(t))\gamma(t)=(g(t),f(t)) points parallel to the vector γ′⁢(t)=(g′⁢(t),f′⁢(t))\gamma^{\prime}(t)=(g^{\prime}(t),f^{\prime}(t)). Hence, its slope is f′⁢(t)/g′⁢(t)f^{\prime}(t)/g^{\prime}(t). So the Extended Mean Value Theorem says that there is a point on the curve γ\gamma, somewhere between its start point γ⁢(a)\gamma(a) and its end point γ⁢(b)\gamma(b), at which its tangent line is parallel to the straight line connecting γ⁢(a)\gamma(a) to γ⁢(b)\gamma(b).

If we apply Theorem 3.16 in the case where g⁢(x)=xg(x)=x, we immediately deduce the usual Mean Value Theorem22 2 Or, even better, apply it in the case g⁢(x)=x−ag(x)=x-a. Then the proof of Theorem 3.16 reduces to the proof of Theorem 3.7.. We will see that other choices of gg can be useful. For example, we can use Theorem 3.16 to rigorously justify a popular trick for computing limits called “L’Hospital’s Rule”.

Theorem 3.17 (L’Hospital’s Rule)

Let I⊆ℝI\subseteq{\mathbb{R}} be an open interval, f,g:I→ℝf,g:I\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be differentiable functions, a∈Ia\in I, and f⁢(a)=g⁢(a)=0f(a)=g(a)=0. Assume that, for all x∈Ix\in I, g′⁢(x)≠0g^{\prime}(x)\neq 0 and all x∈I\{a}x\in I{\backslash}\{a\}, g⁢(x)≠0g(x)\neq 0. Then

limx→af⁢(x)g⁢(x)=limx→af′⁢(x)g′⁢(x)\lim_{x\rightarrow a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\lim_{x\rightarrow a}\frac{f^{\prime}(x% )}{g^{\prime}(x)}

provided the limit on the right exists.

Proof.

Let (xn)(x_{n}) be any sequence in I\{a}I{\backslash}\{a\} converging to aa, and for each nn, consider f,gf,g restricted to the closed interval with endpoints aa and xnx_{n}. These functions satisfy the hypotheses of Theorem 3.16, so there exists cnc_{n}, between aa and xnx_{n}, such that

f′⁢(cn)g′⁢(cn)=f⁢(xn)−f⁢(a)g⁢(xn)−g⁢(a)=f⁢(xn)g⁢(xn).\frac{f^{\prime}(c_{n})}{g^{\prime}(c_{n})}=\frac{f(x_{n})-f(a)}{g(x_{n})-g(a)% }=\frac{f(x_{n})}{g(x_{n})}.

By the Squeeze Rule, cn→ac_{n}\rightarrow a, and so, assuming the limit

limx→af′⁢(x)g′⁢(x)\lim_{x\rightarrow a}\frac{f^{\prime}(x)}{g^{\prime}(x)}

exists, call it L∈ℝL\in{\mathbb{R}}, say, f′⁢(cn)/g′⁢(cn)→Lf^{\prime}(c_{n})/g^{\prime}(c_{n})\rightarrow L (by the definition of limit). Hence f⁢(xn)/g⁢(xn)→Lf(x_{n})/g(x_{n})\rightarrow L, as was to be shown. ∎
