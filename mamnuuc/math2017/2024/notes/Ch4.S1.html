Suprema and infima

4.1 Suprema and infima

In this section we will develop a mathematcally rigorous theory of integration. This will make frequent use of the idea of the supremum and infimum of a bounded set A⊂ℝA\subset{\mathbb{R}}. Recall that AA is bounded above if there exists K∈ℝK\in{\mathbb{R}} such that, for all x∈Ax\in A, x≤Kx\leq K. Any such KK is called an upper bound on AA. “Bounded below” and “lower bound” are defined analogously: A⊂ℝA\subset{\mathbb{R}} is bounded below if there exists L∈ℝL\in{\mathbb{R}} such that, for all x∈Ax\in A, x≥Lx\geq L, and any such LL is called a lower bound on AA. A set is bounded if it is bounded above and below.

Definition 4.1

Let AA be a nonempty subset of ℝ{\mathbb{R}}. The supremum of AA, denoted supA\sup A, is the least upper bound on AA, if this exists. That is, supA\sup A is a real number with two properties:

  1. (i)
    ​

    supA\sup A is an upper bound on AA (i.e. for all x∈Ax\in A, x≤supAx\leq\sup A), and

  2. (ii)
    ​

    no number less than supA\sup A is an upper bound on AA.

Similarly, the infimum of AA, denoted infA\inf A, is the greatest lower bound on AA, if this exists. That is, infA\inf A is a real number with two properties:

  1. (i)
    ​

    infA\inf A is a lower bound on AA (i.e. for all x∈Ax\in A, x≥infAx\geq\inf A), and

  2. (ii)
    ​

    no number greater than infA\inf A is a lower bound on AA.

One of the defining properties of ℝ{\mathbb{R}} is:

The Axiom of Completeness Every nonempty subset of ℝ{\mathbb{R}} which is bounded above has a supremum in ℝ{\mathbb{R}}.

Example 4.2

Let A=(−∞,1)A=(-\infty,1). This is nonempty and bounded above, by 11, for example. Hence, by the Axiom of Completeness, it must have a supremum. In fact, supA=1\sup A=1.

Proof.

We’ve already observed that 11 is an upper bound on AA. Let K<1K<1. Then x=(K+1)/2<(1+1)/2=1x=(K+1)/2<(1+1)/2=1 so x∈Ax\in A. But x>(K+K)/2=Kx>(K+K)/2=K, so KK is not an upper bound on AA. Hence 11 is the least upper bound on AA. ∎

Although “supremum” and “maximum” are not the same thing, they behave similarly in many respects. Here’s an example. If sets AA and BB both have maxima, and AA is a subset of BB, then clearly max⁡A≤max⁡B\max A\leq\max B. The same holds for suprema. If BB is bounded above and A⊆BA\subseteq B, then any upper bound on BB is also an upper bound on AA. In particular, supB\sup B is an upper bound on AA. Since supA\sup A is the least upper bound on AA, it follows that supA≤supB\sup A\leq\sup B. That is

A⊆B⇒supA≤supB.A\subseteq B\qquad\Rightarrow\qquad\sup A\leq\sup B.

By similar reasoning,

A⊆B⇒infA≥infB.A\subseteq B\qquad\Rightarrow\qquad\inf A\geq\inf B.
