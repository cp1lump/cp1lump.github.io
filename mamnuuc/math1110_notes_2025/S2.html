2 Sequences

A real sequence is a mapping a:ℤ+→ℝa:{\mathbb{Z}}^{+}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, that is, a rule which assigns to each positive integer nn some real number a⁢(n)a(n). Actually, for sequences, unlike pretty much all other functions, it’s conventional to write the image of nn under the mapping aa as ana_{n} instead of a⁢(n)a(n), and call it the 𝐧th{\bf n}^{\mbox{th}} term of the sequence, rather than the “image of nn.” It’s also conventional to denote the mapping as (an)n∈ℤ+(a_{n})_{n\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+}}, or just (an)(a_{n}) for short, rather than a:ℤ+→ℝa:{\mathbb{Z}}^{+}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}. We will follow these conventions.

Often we will specify a sequence just by giving a formula for its nthn^{\mbox{th}} term.

Example 2.1

an=n2+5n2a_{n}=\frac{n^{2}+5}{n^{2}} is a sequence whose first 4 terms are

6,94,149,2116.\displaystyle 6,\qquad\frac{9}{4},\qquad\frac{14}{9},\qquad\frac{21}{16}.

As nn gets very large, ana_{n} gets very close to 11: for example an=1a_{n}=1 to 3 decimal places for all n≥101n\geq 101. So for nn this large, we can approximate the sequence by the constant number 11, provided we’re happy to accept errors no larger than 5×10−45\times 10^{-4}. What if we’re a bit fussier than this, and need to know ana_{n} to at least 5 decimal places? How big must nn be before we can approximate ana_{n} by the constant 11? We need

0.999995≤an<1.000005\displaystyle 0.999995\leq a_{n}<1.000005\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad
⇔\displaystyle\Leftrightarrow 0.999995≤1+5n2<1.000005\displaystyle 0.999995\leq 1+\frac{5}{n^{2}}<1.000005
⇔\displaystyle\Leftrightarrow −0.000005≤5n2<0.000005\displaystyle-0.000005\leq\frac{5}{n^{2}}<0.000005
⇔\displaystyle\Leftrightarrow 5n2<5×10−6\displaystyle\frac{5}{n^{2}}<5\times 10^{-6}
⇔\displaystyle\Leftrightarrow n2>106\displaystyle n^{2}>10^{6}
⇔\displaystyle\Leftrightarrow n>1000.\displaystyle n>1000.

So, for all n≥1001n\geq 1001, an=1a_{n}=1 to 5 decimal places. In fact, however many decimal; places of accuracy we insist on, there is a point in the sequence beyond which, to that accuracy, ana_{n} is just 11.

Example 2.2

Contrast this with an=sin⁡na_{n}=\sin n, whose first 4 terms (to 5 decimal places) are

0.84147,0.90930,0.14112 and−0.75680.0.84147,\quad 0.90930,\quad 0.14112\quad\mbox{ and}-0.75680.

The behaviour of this sequence as nn grows large is not at all uniform; the terms bounce around seemingly at random with −1<an<1-1<a_{n}<1.

Sometimes it’s more convenient to define a sequence inductively. In its simplest form, this is when we specify the first term a1a_{1}, and give a rule for constructing an+1a_{n+1} from ana_{n}.

Example 2.3

Let (an)(a_{n}) be the sequence whose first term is a1=1a_{1}=1, for which

an+1=an1+an2.a_{n+1}=\frac{a_{n}}{1+a_{n}^{2}}.

The first 4 terms of this sequence are

a1\displaystyle a_{1} =\displaystyle= 1\displaystyle 1\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad
a2\displaystyle a_{2} =\displaystyle= 11+12=12\displaystyle\frac{1}{1+1^{2}}=\frac{1}{2}
a3\displaystyle a_{3} =\displaystyle= 1/21+(1/4)=25\displaystyle\frac{1/2}{1+(1/4)}=\frac{2}{5}
a4\displaystyle a_{4} =\displaystyle= 2/51+(4/25)=1029\displaystyle\frac{2/5}{1+(4/25)}=\frac{10}{29}

What happens to ana_{n} as nn gets very large? This is actually quite hard to answer directly, even using a computer package such as Maple, because the terms become very unwieldy as nn gets even moderately large. For example

a8=2684403867986594189884901099331737522548368039021,a_{8}=\frac{268440386798659418988490}{1099331737522548368039021},

and Maple has trouble computing even a30a_{30}, because the integers involved get so large. We can convert each ana_{n} to a truncated decimal expansion, using the command evalf, which greatly speeds up the calculation, but this gives rounding errors which accumulate as nn increases, so by the time we get to a1000a_{1000}, say,it’s not obvious whether the answer Maple produces (0.022342937050.02234293705, for what it’s worth) bears any relation to reality.

Another interesting question is whether the large nn behaviour of this sequence depends on our choice of initial term, a1=1a_{1}=1. What if a1=0a_{1}=0? Or a1=1000a_{1}=1000? We will develop methods which will allow us to show that, whatever a1a_{1} we choose, ana_{n} for large nn becomes very close to 0 – despite the fact that we have no idea how to write down ana_{n} in general!

2.1 Convergence of sequences

In Example 2.1, the terms of the sequence “tend to” 11 as nn gets arbitrarily large, whereas in Example 2.2, the terms bounce around indefinitely, without tending to a particular value. We say that an=(n2+5)/n2a_{n}=(n^{2}+5)/n^{2} converges to 11, while an=sin⁡na_{n}=\sin n does not converge. It is now time to make this concept of convergence precise.

What does this have to do with convergence of sequences? In saying that an=(n2+5)n2a_{n}=\frac{(n^{2}+5)}{n^{2}} converges to 11, we are saying, roughly, that the terms ana_{n} are close to 11 for large nn. In terms of absolute values, this means that |an−1||a_{n}-1| is small for large nn. More precisely, we make the following definition:

Definition 2.4

A real sequence (an)(a_{n}) converges to a real number LL if, for each ε>0\varepsilon>0 there exists some positive integer NN such that for all n≥Nn\geq N, |an−L|<ε|a_{n}-L|<\varepsilon. In this case, we will write an→La_{n}\rightarrow L. The number LL is called the limit of the sequence (an)(a_{n}), and is often denoted liman\lim a_{n} or limn→∞an\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}a_{n}.

Remarks

  • •
    ​

    This is the most important definition in this course, and it is vitally important that you understand it thoroughly. It is the first rigorous definition of limit we have encountered. Other definitions of limit (e.g. limx→af⁢(x)\lim_{x\rightarrow a}f(x)) will be constructed from it.

  • •
    ​

    The definition says that given any ε>0\varepsilon>0, no matter how small, there is a term in the sequence, aNa_{N}, after which all terms in the sequence lie within distance ε\varepsilon of LL.

  • •
    ​

    Note that |an−L|<ε|a_{n}-L|<\varepsilon is equivalent to L−ε<an<L+εL-\varepsilon<a_{n}<L+\varepsilon.

  • •
    ​

    If we imagine plotting the terms of the sequence ana_{n} on a graph (with nn along the xx axis and ana_{n} along the yy axis), we can construct a geometric picture of what the definition means. Given any positive number ε\varepsilon, construct the horizontal strip of height 2⁢ε2\varepsilon centred on the line y=Ly=L. Then there exists a point NN on the xx axis, to the right of which all points on the graph lie in the strip.

    This is true no matter how small we make ε\varepsilon. If we make ε\varepsilon smaller, the strip gets narrower, and the NN beyond which all terms lie in the strip (probably) moves to the right.

  • •
    ​

    So NN will, in general, depend on ε\varepsilon: if ε\varepsilon is large, a fairly small NN will probably work, whereas if ε\varepsilon is very small, NN will probably have to be very large. For our purposes the important thing is to establish that given any ε>0\varepsilon>0 there is a corresponding NN which works for that particular ε\varepsilon. The relationship between ε\varepsilon and NN is not important, nor is it important to know the smallest NN which will work for a given ε\varepsilon.

  • •
    ​

    Note that, directly from the definition, an→La_{n}\rightarrow L is and only if the sequence bn=an−Lb_{n}=a_{n}-L satisfies bn→0b_{n}\rightarrow 0.

If I ask you to prove from first principles that a given sequence (an)(a_{n}) converges to a given limit LL, I am asking you to show directly that, given any ε>0\varepsilon>0, there is some N∈ℤ+N\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} such that whenever n≥Nn\geq N, |an−L|<ε|a_{n}-L|<\varepsilon. The key to doing this is to “estimate” (that is, rigorously find an upper bound on) the quantity |an−L||a_{n}-L|.

Claim: The sequence an=1n→0a_{n}=\frac{1}{n}\rightarrow 0

We suppose that we are given ϵ>0\epsilon>0 - to prove that an→0a_{n}\rightarrow 0 we need to find an N∈ℕN\in\mathbb{N} such that if n>Nn>N then |an−0|=|an|<ϵ|a_{n}-0|=|a_{n}|<\epsilon. where we repeatedly used that if a>ba>b then 1b>1a\frac{1}{b}>\frac{1}{a}. ∎

Note that Definition 2.4 does not say that ana_{n} “gets closer and closer to LL as nn gets larger”, because this isn’t necessarily true. For example, the sequence

(2.1) (2.1) an={1nn odd1n2n evena_{n}=\left\{\begin{array}[]{ll}\frac{1}{n}&\mbox{$n$ odd}\\ &\\ \frac{1}{n^{2}}&\mbox{$n$ even}\end{array}\right.

Note that Definition 2.4 does not merely say that given any ε>0\varepsilon>0 there is some N∈ℤ+N\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} such that |aN−L|<ε|a_{N}-L|<\varepsilon. The definition certainly implies this, but is considerably stronger. For example, consider the sequence an=(−1)na_{n}=(-1)^{n}. Then given any ε>0\varepsilon>0, there is an N∈ℤ+N\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} such that |an−1|<ε|a_{n}-1|<\varepsilon. For example, N=2N=2 will do for any ε>0\varepsilon>0, since |a2−1|=|1−1|=0<ε|a_{2}-1|=|1-1|=0<\varepsilon. But clearly ana_{n} does not converge to 11. In fact, ana_{n} does not converge to anything. Can we prove this? Yes:

Example 2.8

Claim: the sequence an=(−1)na_{n}=(-1)^{n} does not converge (to any limit).

Proof.

Assume, to the contrary, that an→La_{n}\rightarrow L. Then, given any positive number ε\varepsilon, there exists N∈ℤ+N\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} such that |an−L|<ε|a_{n}-L|<\varepsilon for all n≥Nn\geq N. In particular, this must be true in the case ε=12\varepsilon=\frac{1}{2}: there exists N∈ℤ+N\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} such that, for all n≥Nn\geq N, |an−L|<12|a_{n}-L|<\frac{1}{2}. But then NN and N+1N+1 are consecutive integers, so one is odd and the other is even, and hence |aN+1−aN|=2|a_{N+1}-a_{N}|=2. But, by the triangle inequality,

|aN+1−aN|=|(aN+1−L)−(aN−L)|≤|aN+1−L|+|aN−L|<12+12=1|a_{N+1}-a_{N}|=|(a_{N+1}-L)-(a_{N}-L)|\leq|a_{N+1}-L|+|a_{N}-L|<\frac{1}{2}+% \frac{1}{2}=1

by the definition of NN. Hence 2<12<1, a contradiction. ∎

We next establish that two rather simple, but important, sequences converge to their expected limits.

Example 2.9

The constant sequence, an=ca_{n}=c for all nn, converges to cc.

Proof.

Given any ε>0\varepsilon>0 we can take N=1N=1, since for all n≥1n\geq 1, |an−c|=|c−c|=0<ε|a_{n}-c|=|c-c|=0<\varepsilon. ∎

Direct proofs of this kind are often called “ε\varepsilon–NN proofs”. We finish this section by showing how a direct ε\varepsilon–NN proof can be made even for a reasonably complicated sequence.

2.2 Some limit theorems

It’s very important that you get plenty of practice in proving directly from first principles that sequences converge (i.e. constructing ε\varepsilon–NN proofs) because this is the best way to get to grips with what Definition 2.4 really means. It is also good preparation for understanding the proofs of the following basic limit theorems. Once we’ve proved these, we can often use them, together with a few standard examples, such as Examples 2.9 and 2.5, to show indirectly that a given sequence converges, without having to give an ε\varepsilon–NN argument.

Proposition 2.11 (Uniqueness of Limits)

If a sequence converges, its limit is unique.

Proof.

Assume, to the contrary, that a sequence ana_{n} converges to both L1L_{1} and L2L_{2} with L1≠L2L_{1}\neq L_{2}. Let ε=12⁢|L1−L2|>0\displaystyle{\varepsilon=\frac{1}{2}{|L_{1}-L_{2}|}>0}. Since an→L1a_{n}\rightarrow L_{1}, there exists N1∈ℤ+N_{1}\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} such that, for all n≥N1n\geq N_{1},

|an−L1|<ε.|a_{n}-L_{1}|<\varepsilon.

Similarly, since an→L2a_{n}\rightarrow L_{2}, there exists N2∈ℤ+N_{2}\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} such that, for all n≥N2n\geq N_{2},

|an−L2|<ε.|a_{n}-L_{2}|<\varepsilon.

Now let nn be any positive integer greater than both N1N_{1} and N2N_{2} (for example n=N1+N2n=N_{1}+N_{2}). Then both the above inequalities hold, and so,

2⁢ε=|L2−L1|\displaystyle 2\varepsilon=|L_{2}-L_{1}| =\displaystyle= |(an−L1)−(an−L2)|\displaystyle|(a_{n}-L_{1})-(a_{n}-L_{2})|\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad
≤\displaystyle\leq |an−L1|+|an−L2|(by the Triangle Inequality)\displaystyle|a_{n}-L_{1}|+|a_{n}-L_{2}|\qquad\mbox{(by the Triangle % Inequality)}
<\displaystyle< ε+ε(since n>N1 and n>N2)\displaystyle\varepsilon+\varepsilon\qquad\mbox{(since $n>N_{1}$ and $n>N_{2}$)}

that is, 2⁢ε<2⁢ε2\varepsilon<2\varepsilon, a contradiction. Hence, the original assumption (that (an)(a_{n}) has two different limits) must be false. ∎

So we are justified in speaking of the limit of a convergent sequence.

Definition 2.12

We say that a real sequence (an)(a_{n}) is bounded above if its rangeis bounded above, that is, if there exists M∈ℝM\in{\mathbb{R}} such that for all n∈ℤ+n\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+}, an≤Ma_{n}\leq M. Similarly, (an)(a_{n}) is bounded below if there is a L∈ℝL\in\mathbb{R} such that for all n∈ℤ+n\in\mathbb{Z}^{+}, L≤anL\leq a_{n}. We will say that (an)(a_{n}) is bounded if it is bounded both above and below.

Proposition 2.13

If a sequence converges then it is bounded.

Proof.

Assume an→La_{n}\rightarrow L. Then given any ε>0\varepsilon>0, there exists N∈ℤ+N\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} such that, for all n≥Nn\geq N, |an−L|<ε|a_{n}-L|<\varepsilon. This is true, in particular, in the case ε=1\varepsilon=1. Hence, there exists N∈ℤ+N\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} such that, for all n≥Nn\geq N, |an−L|<1|a_{n}-L|<1. Let

K=max⁡{|a1−L|,|a2−L|,…,|aN−1−L|,1},K=\max\{|a_{1}-L|,|a_{2}-L|,\ldots,|a_{N-1}-L|,1\},

which exists since the set is finite. Then for all n∈ℤ+n\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+}, |an−L|≤K|a_{n}-L|\leq K, and hence, L−K≤an≤L+KL-K\leq a_{n}\leq L+K. Hence, the sequence (an)(a_{n}) is bounded (above by L+KL+K and below by L−KL-K). ∎

Of course, the converse of this Proposition is false: not every bounded sequence is convergent, as we have already seen (for example an=(−1)na_{n}=(-1)^{n} is bounded, but not convergent). If a sequence is convergent, it’s bounded above and below, and it’s not hard to show that its limit must lie between the upper and lower bounds:

Proposition 2.14

If K≤an≤MK\leq a_{n}\leq M for all n∈ℤ+n\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} and (an)(a_{n}) converges to LL, then K≤L≤MK\leq L\leq M.

Proof.

Exercise. The obvious strategy is a proof by contradiction. That is, assume an→La_{n}\rightarrow L but L∉[K,M]L\notin[K,M]. Then either (i) L>ML>M or (ii) L<KL<K. Now in each of these cases, show that a contradiction arises. ∎

Note that a sequence (an)(a_{n}) is bounded if and only if |an||a_{n}| is bounded above.

Starting from simple known facts, like 1/n→01/n\rightarrow 0, we can use Theorem 2.15 to deduce the convergence of a large collection of sequences. For example, 1/n21/n^{2}, 1/n31/n^{3}, 1/n4⁢…1/n^{4}\ldots all converge to 0:

Example 2.16

Claim: for any integer p∈ℤ+p\in\mathbb{Z}^{+}, the sequence an=1np\displaystyle{a_{n}=\frac{1}{n^{p}}} converges to 0.

Proof.

We will prove this by induction (on pp). The claim is true for p=1p=1, by Example 2.5. Assume the claim holds for p=kp=k. Then, in the case p=k+1p=k+1,

an=1n⁢1nk→0×0=0a_{n}=\frac{1}{n}\frac{1}{n^{k}}\rightarrow 0\times 0=0

by Theorem 2.15 (part (ii)). Hence, by induction, the claim holds for all p∈ℤ+p\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+}. ∎

Summary

  • •
    ​

    A real sequence is a mapping a:ℤ+→ℝa:{\mathbb{Z}}^{+}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}. We denote the image of n∈ℤ+n\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} under the mapping aa by ana_{n} and call it the nt⁢hn^{th} term of the sequence. We usually denote the sequence as a whole by (an)(a_{n}).

  • •
    ​

    A sequence (an)(a_{n}) converges to a real number LL if, for each ε>0\varepsilon>0 there exists a positive integer NN such that, for all n≥Nn\geq N, |an−L|<ε|a_{n}-L|<\varepsilon.

  • •
    ​

    If (an)(a_{n}) converges to LL, we write an→La_{n}\rightarrow L.

  • •
    ​

    The number LL is called the limit of the sequence, sometimes denoted liman\lim a_{n} or limn→∞an\displaystyle{\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}a_{n}}.

  • •
    ​

    An ε\varepsilon–NN proof of convergence is a direct argument showing that, given any positive number ε\varepsilon, there is a positive integer NN such that |an−L|<ε|a_{n}-L|<\varepsilon for all n≥Nn\geq N.

  • •
    ​

    The limit of a convergent sequence is unique.

  • •
    ​

    If a sequence is convergent, then it is bounded. The converse is false.

  • •
    ​

    The usual Algebra of Limits holds: if an→Aa_{n}\rightarrow A and bn→Bb_{n}\rightarrow B then

    • ∙\bullet
      ​

      an+bn→A+Ba_{n}+b_{n}\rightarrow A+B,

    • ∙\bullet
      ​

      an⁢bn→A⁢Ba_{n}b_{n}\rightarrow AB, and

    • ∙\bullet
      ​

      an/bn→A/Ba_{n}/b_{n}\rightarrow A/B (provided B≠0B\neq 0 and bnb_{n} is never 0).

  • •
    ​

    The Squeeze Rule: if an≤bn≤cna_{n}\leq b_{n}\leq c_{n} and an→La_{n}\rightarrow L and cn→Lc_{n}\rightarrow L, then bn→Lb_{n}\rightarrow L.
