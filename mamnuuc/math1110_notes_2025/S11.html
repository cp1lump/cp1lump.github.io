11 Differentiablity of power series

11.1 Power series define smooth functions

Recall that a power series is a series of the form

∑n=0∞an⁢xn\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n}

where xx is a real variable and a0,a1,a2,…a_{0},a_{1},a_{2},\ldots is a real sequence (often called the coefficients of the power series). So this is actually a collection of infinitely many series, one for each choice of value for xx. As usual, the series converges at a particular fixed value of xx if its sequence of partial sums

s0\displaystyle s_{0} =\displaystyle= a0\displaystyle a_{0}
s1\displaystyle s_{1} =\displaystyle= a0+a1⁢x\displaystyle a_{0}+a_{1}x
s2\displaystyle s_{2} =\displaystyle= a0+a1⁢x+a2⁢x2\displaystyle a_{0}+a_{1}x+a_{2}x^{2}
⋮\displaystyle\vdots

converges in the sense of Definition 2.4, and converges absolutely if the associated series ∑n=0∞|an⁢xn|\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}|a_{n}x^{n}| converges. Certainly this sequence converges if x=0x=0, since it is the constant sequence (a0,a0,a0,…)(a_{0},a_{0},a_{0},\ldots), but it may diverge for other values of xx. The set of values x∈ℝx\in{\mathbb{R}} for which the series converges is almost completely determined by its radius of convergence (Definition 5.28):

R=sup{|x|∈[0,∞):∑n=0∞|an⁢xn| converges}.R=\sup\{|x|\in[0,\infty):\mbox{$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}|a_{n}x^{n}|$ converges}\}.

This follows from Theorem 5.30: the series converges absolutely for all |x|<R|x|<R and diverges for all |x|>R|x|>R.

So, a power series with radius of convergence RR defines a function

f:(−R,R)→ℝ,f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn.f:(-R,R)\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\quad f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n}.

Switching viewpoint, its partial sums can be considered to be a sequence of polynomial functions:

s0⁢(x)\displaystyle s_{0}(x) =\displaystyle= a0\displaystyle a_{0}
s1⁢(x)\displaystyle s_{1}(x) =\displaystyle= a0+a1⁢x\displaystyle a_{0}+a_{1}x
s2⁢(x)\displaystyle s_{2}(x) =\displaystyle= a0+a1⁢x+a2⁢x2\displaystyle a_{0}+a_{1}x+a_{2}x^{2}
⋮\displaystyle\vdots

Each of these is certainly a differentiable function (on the whole of ℝ{\mathbb{R}}), so one might expect that their limit, f⁢(x)=limk→∞sk⁢(x)f(x)=\lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}s_{k}(x), is also a differentiable function, and that

f′⁢(x)=limk→∞sk′⁢(x)=limk→∞a1+x2⁢x+⋯⁢k⁢ak⁢xk−1=∑n=1∞n⁢an⁢xn−1.f^{\prime}(x)=\lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}s_{k}^{\prime}(x)=\lim_{k\rightarrow% \infty}a_{1}+x_{2}x+\cdots ka_{k}x^{k-1}=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}na_{n}x^{n-1}.

This turns out to be true, at least on (−R,R)(-R,R) where RR is the radius of convergence of ff, but proving it is no simple matter. The problem is that f′⁢(x)f^{\prime}(x) is really a double limit

f′⁢(x)=limy→xf⁢(y)−f⁢(x)y−x=limy→xlimk→∞∑n=0kan⁢yn−xny−x,f^{\prime}(x)=\lim_{y\rightarrow x}\frac{f(y)-f(x)}{y-x}=\lim_{y\rightarrow x}% \lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}\sum_{n=0}^{k}a_{n}\frac{y^{n}-x^{n}}{y-x},

and our expected formula for f′⁢(x)f^{\prime}(x) results from computing the double limit in the opposite order

limk→∞limy→x∑n=0kan⁢yn−xny−x.\lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}\lim_{y\rightarrow x}\sum_{n=0}^{k}a_{n}\frac{y^{n}-x% ^{n}}{y-x}.

But swapping the order in which we evaluate a double limit can, in general, change its value!

Consider the sequence of functions

sk:ℝ→ℝ,sk⁢(x)=k⁢x2−1k⁢x2+1.s_{k}:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad s_{k}(x)=\frac{kx^{2}-1}{kx^{% 2}+1}.

The two limits are different! □\Box

So computing

limk→∞limy→x∑n=0kan⁢yn−xny−x\lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}\lim_{y\rightarrow x}\sum_{n=0}^{k}a_{n}\frac{y^{n}-x% ^{n}}{y-x}

limy→xlimk→∞∑n=0kan⁢yn−xny−x.\lim_{y\rightarrow x}\lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}\sum_{n=0}^{k}a_{n}\frac{y^{n}-x% ^{n}}{y-x}.

We will need to work considerably harder!

We begin by showing that a power series and the obvious candidate for its derivative have the same radius of convergence.

We now show that g⁢(x)g(x) really is the derivative of f⁢(x)f(x).

Theorem 11.3

Suppose that f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xn\displaystyle{f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n}} has radius of convergence R>0R>0. Then ff is differentiable on (−R,R)(-R,R), and

∑n=2kn⁢(n−1)⁢|an|⁢ρn−2\sum_{n=2}^{k}n(n-1)|a_{n}|\rho^{n-2}

So the derivative of a power series exists on its open interval of convergence and is just the power series obtained by termwise differentiation. Since f′⁢(x)f^{\prime}(x) is also a power series with radius of convergence RR, we can apply Theorem 11.3 to f′⁢(x)f^{\prime}(x) and deduce that f:(−R,R)→ℝf:(-R,R)\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is actually twice differentiable. Further, f′′⁢(x)f^{\prime\prime}(x) also has radius of convergence RR, so is differentiable on (−R,R)(-R,R), that is, ff is three times differentiable. In fact, we can keep applying Theorem 11.3 as often as we like, and we conclude that ff is smooth.

Corollary 11.4

Let f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢xnf(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_{n}x^{n} have radius convergence R>0R>0. Then f:(−R,R)→ℝf:(-R,R)\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is a smooth function, and

an=f(n)⁢(0)n!.a_{n}=\frac{f^{(n)}(0)}{n!}.
Proof.

For each integer k≥0k\geq 0, define

gk⁢(x)=∑n=0∞(n+k)!n!⁢an+k⁢xn.g_{k}(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(n+k)!}{n!}a_{n+k}x^{n}.

I claim that each power series gk⁢(x)g_{k}(x) has radius of convergence RR and that, for all x∈(−R,R)x\in(-R,R), f(k)⁢(x)=gk⁢(x)f^{(k)}(x)=g_{k}(x). We prove this by induction on kk.

Certainly the claim holds for k=0k=0, since g0=fg_{0}=f. So, assume that the claim holds for some value k≥0k\geq 0, and consider gk+1g_{k+1}. Defining coefficients bn=(n+k)!n!⁢an+kb_{n}=\frac{(n+k)!}{n!}a_{n+k} so that gk⁢(x)=∑n=0∞bn⁢xng_{k}(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}b_{n}x^{n}, then

gk+1⁢(x)\displaystyle g_{k+1}(x) =\displaystyle= ∑n=0∞(n+k+1)!n!⁢an+k+1⁢xn=∑m=1∞(m+k)!(m−1)!⁢am+k⁢xm−1\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(n+k+1)!}{n!}a_{n+k+1}x^{n}=\sum_{m=1}^{% \infty}\frac{(m+k)!}{(m-1)!}a_{m+k}x^{m-1}
=\displaystyle= ∑m=1∞m⁢bm⁢xm−1.\displaystyle\sum_{m=1}^{\infty}mb_{m}x^{m-1}.

Hence, by Lemma 11.2, gk+1g_{k+1} has the same radius of convergence as gkg_{k}, and by Theorem 11.3, gk+1⁢(x)g_{k+1}(x) coincides with the derivative of gkg_{k}. But, by our induction hypothesis, gkg_{k} has radius of convergence RR and coincides with f(k)f^{(k)}, so gk+1g_{k+1} has radius of convergence RR and coincides with f(k+1)f^{(k+1)}. Hence, if the claim holds for some k≥0k\geq 0, it also holds for k+1k+1. Hence, by induction, the claim holds for all integers k≥0k\geq 0.

It follows that f(k)⁢(0)=gk⁢(0)=k!⁢akf^{(k)}(0)=g_{k}(0)=k!a_{k}, which completes the proof. ∎

Corollary 11.4 applies to any power series with non-zero radius of convergence so, in particular, it applies to the series defining exp\exp, sin\sin, and cos\cos.

Proposition 11.5

The functions exp:ℝ→ℝ\exp:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, sin:ℝ→ℝ\sin:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, cos:ℝ→ℝ\cos:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} are smooth, and their derivatives are

exp′=exp,sin′=cos,cos′=−sin.\exp^{\prime}=\exp,\qquad\sin^{\prime}=\cos,\qquad\cos^{\prime}=-\sin.
Proof.

That these functions are smooth follows immediately from Corollary 11.4. Furthermore, by Theorem 11.3

exp′⁡(x)=∑n=1∞n⁢(1n!)⁢xn−1=∑n=1∞xn−1(n−1)!=∑m=0∞xmm!=exp⁡(x)\exp^{\prime}(x)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}n\left(\frac{1}{n!}\right)x^{n-1}=\sum_{n=% 1}^{\infty}\frac{x^{n-1}}{(n-1)!}=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\frac{x^{m}}{m!}=\exp(x)

for all x∈ℝx\in{\mathbb{R}}, so exp′=exp\exp^{\prime}=\exp. The formulae for sin′\sin^{\prime} and cos′\cos^{\prime} follow from similar arguments. ∎

Smooth functions are differentiable, and hence continuous, so this restrospectively justifies our assumption that sin:ℝ→ℝ\sin:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} (for example) is continuous.

11.2 Exponentials and logarithms

Now that we know that exp\exp is differentiable, and its derivative is exp\exp, we can prove that it has all the nice properties you’re familiar with.

Proposition 11.6

For all x,y∈ℝx,y\in{\mathbb{R}}, exp⁡(x+y)=exp⁡(x)⁢exp⁡(y)\exp(x+y)=\exp(x)\exp(y).

Proof.

Choose any constant a∈ℝa\in{\mathbb{R}} and consider the function

f:ℝ→ℝ,f⁢(x)=exp⁡(x)⁢exp⁡(a−x).f:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad f(x)=\exp(x)\exp(a-x).

By the Product and Chain Rules, this is differentiable with derivative

f′⁢(x)=exp′⁡(x)⁢exp⁡(a−x)+exp⁡(x)⁢(−exp′⁡(a−x))=exp⁡(x)⁢exp⁡(a−x)−exp⁡(x)⁢exp⁡(a−x)=0.f^{\prime}(x)=\exp^{\prime}(x)\exp(a-x)+\exp(x)(-\exp^{\prime}(a-x))=\exp(x)% \exp(a-x)-\exp(x)\exp(a-x)=0.

Hence by Proposition 10.9, ff is constant, that is, for all x∈ℝx\in{\mathbb{R}}, f⁢(x)=f⁢(a)f(x)=f(a), that is

exp⁡(x)⁢exp⁡(a−x)=exp⁡(a)⁢exp⁡(0)=exp⁡(a).\exp(x)\exp(a-x)=\exp(a)\exp(0)=\exp(a).

This holds for all a∈ℝa\in{\mathbb{R}} and, in the case a=x+ya=x+y, yields the claimed identity. ∎

Proposition 11.7

exp\exp is a bijective map ℝ→(0,∞){\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow(0,\infty).

Proof.

We first note that there is no x∈ℝx\in{\mathbb{R}} such that exp⁡(x)=0\exp(x)=0 since, if there were, then Proposition 11.6 with y=−xy=-x would imply

11.3 Exactly summing (some) series

We finish this section with a nice trick. Sometimes we can exactly sum a series by thinking of it as a power series evaluated at a specific value xx. Here’s a simple example:

Example 11.13

Exactly sum ∑n=1∞n2n\displaystyle{\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{2^{n}}}.

Solution:  It’s easy to show (using the Ratio Test, for example) that the series converges, but this doesn’t tell us what its limit is. We need to be sneakier.

Consider the power series

f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞xn.f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}x^{n}.

This is the Geometric Series with common ratio xx. We showed previously that it has radius of convergence R=1R=1, and for all x∈(−1,1)x\in(-1,1), its limit is

(11.13) (11.13) f⁢(x)=11−x.f(x)=\frac{1}{1-x}.

f′⁢(x)=∑n=1∞n⁢xn−1.f^{\prime}(x)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}nx^{n-1}.

But, by the Chain Rule, we already know from (11.13) that ff is differentiable with derivative

f′⁢(x)=1(1−x)2.f^{\prime}(x)=\frac{1}{(1-x)^{2}}.

Hence, for all x∈(−1,1)x\in(-1,1),

∑n=1∞n⁢xn−1=1(1−x)2.\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}nx^{n-1}=\frac{1}{(1-x)^{2}}.

∑n=1∞n2n−1=1(1/2)2=4,\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{2^{n-1}}=\frac{1}{(1/2)^{2}}=4,

∑n=1∞n2n=12×4=2.\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{2^{n}}=\frac{1}{2}\times 4=2.
