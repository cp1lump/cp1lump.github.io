8 Continuous functions

In this second half of the module, we will take all the theory about convergence of sequences (and series) we’ve developed in Semester 1, and use it to develop a precise and rigorous theory of calculus (both differential and integral) of real-valued functions of a single real variable. Since we now have a precise definition of limit (see Definition 7.1) we’re already halfway there: we know precisely what we mean by the derivative f′⁢(a)f^{\prime}(a) of a differentiable function ff. (Of course, just defining the derivative isn’t the end of the story: we also want to prove that derivatives have all the properties you know and love, that they obey the Product and Chain rules and so on.) Before embarking on that endeavour we will retrench a little, however. Instead of diving straight into differentiability, it will turn out to be important to thoroughly understand a more rudimentary property of (some) functions: continuity.

8.1 Sequential continuity

Given a function f:ℝ→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, what does it mean to say that the function is continuous? It’s common, when first introduced to the notion of continuity, to be told that a function is continuous “if you can draw its graph without taking your pencil off the paper”. Clearly this is not an acceptable mathematical definition: what if you have no pencil, or paper, or hands? Does the continuity or otherwise of ff depend on your skill as a draughtsman? More importantly, can you imagine ever being able to prove statements about continuous functions starting from such a “definition”?

So our first job will be to define continuity precisely. In fact it’s quite easy to give a precise (and rather elegant) definition of continuity using nothing more than the notion of convergence of sequences. The definition works equally well when the domain of ff is a subset of ℝ{\mathbb{R}}, not necessarily the whole of ℝ{\mathbb{R}}, so that is how we will formulate it.

Given a set D⊆ℝD\subseteq\mathbb{R} we will say that a sequence ana_{n} is in DD if for every n∈ℤ+n\in\mathbb{Z}^{+}, an∈Da_{n}\in D.

Definition 8.1

Let D⊆ℝD\subseteq{\mathbb{R}}. A function f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is continuous at a point a∈Da\in D if, for all sequences (xn)(x_{n}) in DD such that xn→ax_{n}\rightarrow a, f⁢(xn)→f⁢(a)f(x_{n})\rightarrow f(a). If ff is not continuous at a∈Da\in D, we say that ff is discontinuous at aa. The function is continuous if it is continuous at aa for all a∈Da\in D.

So what is this definition saying? Roughly speaking, this is saying that for points that get arbitrarily close to a∈Da\in D, the images of these points under ff get arbitrarily close to f⁢(a)f(a).

Example 8.2

  1. (i)
    ​

    f:ℝ→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} with f⁢(x)=cf(x)=c (for cc any constant), is continuous.

    Proof.

    Let a∈ℝa\in{\mathbb{R}} and xn→ax_{n}\rightarrow a. Then f⁢(xn)=c→c=f⁢(a)f(x_{n})=c\rightarrow c=f(a). ∎

  2. (ii)
    ​

    f:ℝ→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} such that f⁢(x)=xf(x)=x is continuous.

    Proof.

    Let a∈ℝa\in{\mathbb{R}} and xn→ax_{n}\rightarrow a. Then f⁢(xn)=xn→a=f⁢(a)f(x_{n})=x_{n}\rightarrow a=f(a). ∎

  3. (iii)

    f:ℝ\{0}→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}{\backslash}\{0\}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} such that f⁢(x)=1/xf(x)=1/x is continuous.

    If you’ve been taught in the past that a continuous function is “one whose graph you can draw without taking your pen off the paper” then this example will be surprising at first sight. Nonetheless, this function really is continuous according to our (precise) definition:

    Proof.

    This one is also counterintuitive. In fact, it gets worse:

  5. (v)
    ​

    Every function f:ℤ→ℝf:{\mathbb{Z}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is continuous!

    Proof.

    Let a∈ℤa\in{\mathbb{Z}} and (xn)(x_{n}) be a sequence in ℤ{\mathbb{Z}} converging to aa.   6. (vi)

    This is an example of a function which is discontinuous at a single isolated point. It’s also possible to construct functions which are continuous at a single isolated point (and discontinuous everywhere else):

  7. (vii)
    ​

    Let f:ℝ→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} such that

    f⁢(x)={0x∈ℚxx∉ℚ.f(x)=\left\{\begin{array}[]{ll}0&x\in{\mathbb{Q}}\\ x&x\notin{\mathbb{Q}}.\end{array}\right.

    Proof.

    Let a∈ℝa\in{\mathbb{R}} and assume that ff is continuous at aa.     Let (xn)(x_{n}) be any sequence in ℝ{\mathbb{R}} such that xn→0x_{n}\rightarrow 0. Let ε>0\varepsilon>0 be given. Then, since xn→0x_{n}\rightarrow 0, there exists N∈ℤ+N\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} such that for all n≥Nn\geq N, |xn−0|<ε|x_{n}-0|<\varepsilon. But then, for all n≥Nn\geq N,

    |f⁢(xn)−0|=|f⁢(xn)|≤|xn|<ε|f(x_{n})-0|=|f(x_{n})|\leq|x_{n}|<\varepsilon

    since f⁢(xn)=xnf(x_{n})=x_{n} if xnx_{n} is irrational, and is 0 otherwise. Hence f⁢(xn)→0=f⁢(0)f(x_{n})\rightarrow 0=f(0). Hence, ff is continuous at 0. ∎

8.2 Properties of continuous functions

If we are given a complicated function such as

f:ℝ→ℝ,f⁢(x)=x3−7⁢xx2+1f:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad f(x)=\frac{x^{3}-7x}{x^{2}+1}

we can show directly from Definition 8.1 that ff is continuous, by considering an arbitrary sequence (xn)(x_{n}) converging to aa. To do this we will have to appeal at some point to Theorem 2.15 (the Algebra of Limits), to argue that

f⁢(xn)=xn3−7⁢xnxn2+1converges toa3−7⁢aa2+1=f⁢(a).f(x_{n})=\frac{x_{n}^{3}-7x_{n}}{x_{n}^{2}+1}\qquad\mbox{converges to}\qquad% \frac{a^{3}-7a}{a^{2}+1}=f(a).

Recall that a polynomial function is a function of the form

p⁢(x)=a0+a1⁢x+a2⁢x2+⋯+am⁢xmp(x)=a_{0}+a_{1}x+a_{2}x^{2}+\cdots+a_{m}x^{m}

where a0,…,ama_{0},\ldots,a_{m} are (real) constants. If am≠0a_{m}\neq 0, we say that pp has degree mm. Polynomials are a very nice class of functions. In particular they are always continuous:

Proposition 8.4

Every polynomial function p:ℝ→ℝp:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is continuous.

Proof.

We prove this by induction on mm, the degree of the polynomial pp. So, if m=0m=0, then p⁢(x)p(x) is a degree 0 polynomial, that is, p⁢(x)=a0p(x)=a_{0}, constant. Hence p⁢(x)p(x) is continuous (see Example 8.2). Now assume that every polynomial of degree kk is continuous. Let p⁢(x)p(x) be a degree k+1k+1 polynomial. Then p⁢(x)=x⁢q⁢(x)+a0p(x)=xq(x)+a_{0} where q⁢(x)q(x) is a polynomial of degree kk and so is, by assumption, continuous. Now f⁢(x)=xf(x)=x is continuous (see Example 8.2), so p⁢(x)=x⁢q⁢(x)+a0p(x)=xq(x)+a_{0} is continuous by Theorem 8.3(i),(ii). Hence, by induction every polynomial is continuous. ∎

A nice thing about the set of polynomials is that it is closed under function composition. That is, if pp and qq are polynomials, so is q∘pq\circ p (recall that (q∘p)⁢(x)=q⁢(p⁢(x))(q\circ p)(x)=q(p(x))). For example:

p⁢(x)\displaystyle p(x) =\displaystyle= x3+1,q⁢(x)=3⁢x2−2\displaystyle x^{3}+1,\qquad q(x)=3x^{2}-2
⇒(q∘p)⁢(x)\displaystyle\Rightarrow(q\circ p)(x) =\displaystyle= q⁢(x3+1)\displaystyle q(x^{3}+1)
=\displaystyle= q⁢(x3+1)\displaystyle q(x^{3}+1)
=\displaystyle= 3⁢(x6+2⁢x3+1)−2\displaystyle 3(x^{6}+2x^{3}+1)-2
=\displaystyle= 3⁢x6+6⁢x3+1.\displaystyle 3x^{6}+6x^{3}+1.

So given a pair of polynomials (certainly continuous), their composition is a polynomial (and hence is continuous). Is this a special feature of polynomials? No: it follows almost immediately from our definition of continuity that the composition of two continuous functions is continuous.

Theorem 8.5

Let D,E⊆ℝD,E\subseteq{\mathbb{R}}, f:D→Ef:D\rightarrow E and g:E→ℝg:E\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}. If ff is continuous at a∈Da\in D and gg is continuous at f⁢(a)∈Ef(a)\in E, then g∘fg\circ f is continuous at aa.

Proof.

Let (xn)(x_{n}) be any sequence in DD which converges to aa. Then f⁢(xn)→f⁢(a)f(x_{n})\rightarrow f(a) (since ff is continuous at aa), so (g∘f)⁢(xn)=g⁢(f⁢(xn))→g⁢(f⁢(a))=(g∘f)⁢(a)(g\circ f)(x_{n})=g(f(x_{n}))\rightarrow g(f(a))=(g\circ f)(a) (since gg is continuous at f⁢(a)f(a)). Hence g∘fg\circ f is continuous at aa. ∎

8.3 The Intermediate Value Theorem

To say that this theorem is “important” would be something of an understatement. It is the basis of a useful method for approximately solving equations (the bisection method) and, as we will see, implies the existence of square roots and other similar functions.

Theorem 8.6 (Intermediate Value Theorem)

Let f:[a,b]→ℝf:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be continuous, and yy be any real number between f⁢(a)f(a) and f⁢(b)f(b). Then there exists c∈[a,b]c\in[a,b] such that f⁢(c)=yf(c)=y.

Proof.

There are three possibilities: (i) f⁢(a)=f⁢(b)f(a)=f(b), (ii) f⁢(a)<f⁢(b)f(a)<f(b) and (iii) f⁢(a)>f⁢(b)f(a)>f(b).

(i) If f⁢(a)=f⁢(b)f(a)=f(b) then the only real number between them is y=f⁢(a)y=f(a), so c=ac=a will do.

(ii) Assume f⁢(a)<f⁢(b)f(a)<f(b). If f⁢(b)=yf(b)=y then c=bc=b will do. So assume y<f⁢(b)y<f(b). Define the set

A={x∈[a,b]:f⁢(x)≤y},A=\{x\in[a,b]\>:\>f(x)\leq y\},

which is nonempty (since a∈Aa\in A) and bounded above (by bb). Hence by Axiom 3.11, there exists cc, the supremum of AA. We will prove that c∈[a,b]c\in[a,b] and that f⁢(c)=yf(c)=y, which completes case (ii). cc is an upper bound on AA and a∈Aa\in A, so a≤ca\leq c. Further, bb is also an upper bound on AA, and cc is the least upper bound on AA, so c≤bc\leq b. Hence c∈[a,b]c\in[a,b]. Consider c−1/nc-1/n, where n∈ℤ+n\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+}. Since this is less than cc, it is not an upper bound on AA, so there exists xn∈Ax_{n}\in A with xn>c−1/nx_{n}>c-1/n. But then c−1/n≤xn≤cc-1/n\leq x_{n}\leq c, so xn→cx_{n}\rightarrow c by the Squeeze Rule. ff is continuous at cc, so f⁢(xn)→f⁢(c)f(x_{n})\rightarrow f(c). But each xn∈Ax_{n}\in A, so f⁢(xn)≤yf(x_{n})\leq y for all nn, and hence its limit cannot exceed yy, by Proposition 2.14. Hence f⁢(c)≤yf(c)\leq y. Hence c∈Ac\in A and c≠bc\neq b, so c<bc<b. Consider now the sequence zn=c+(b−c)/nz_{n}=c+(b-c)/n. This is a sequence in [a,b][a,b] each term of which exceeds cc, so for all nn, zn∉Az_{n}\notin A (recall cc is an upper bound on AA). Hence f⁢(zn)≥yf(z_{n})\geq y for all nn. But zn→cz_{n}\rightarrow c and ff is continuous, so f⁢(zn)→f⁢(c)f(z_{n})\rightarrow f(c). Hence, by Proposition 2.14, f⁢(c)≥yf(c)\geq y. Since we’ve already shown that f⁢(c)≤yf(c)\leq y, it follows that f⁢(c)=yf(c)=y.

(iii) If f⁢(a)>f⁢(b)f(a)>f(b), define f~:[a,b]→ℝ\widetilde{f}:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} such that f~⁢(x)=−f⁢(x)\widetilde{f}(x)=-f(x) and y~=−y\widetilde{y}=-y. Then f~\widetilde{f} is continuous, f~⁢(a)<f~⁢(b)\widetilde{f}(a)<\widetilde{f}(b), and y~\widetilde{y} is between f~⁢(a)\widetilde{f}(a) and f~⁢(b)\widetilde{f}(b), so, as proved in part (ii), there exists c∈[a,b]c\in[a,b] such that f~⁢(c)=y~\widetilde{f}(c)=\widetilde{y}. But then f⁢(c)=yf(c)=y.

∎

Note that the assumption that the domain of ff is an interval is crucial:

Example 8.7

f:ℝ\{0}→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}{\backslash}\{0\}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, f⁢(x)=1/xf(x)=1/x is continuous (by Example 8.2). Let a=−1a=-1 and b=1b=1. Then f⁢(a)=−1f(a)=-1 and f⁢(b)=1f(b)=1 and so 0 is a number between f⁢(a)f(a) and f⁢(b)f(b). But there is no cc such that f⁢(c)=0f(c)=0 (1/c=01/c=0 has no solution). This is not a counterexample to Theorem 8.6, since ff is not continuous on [−1,1][-1,1].

Example 8.8

Let f:ℝ→ℝf:{\mathbb{R}}\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} such that f⁢(x)=x2−3f(x)=x^{2}-3. Then ff is continuous. In particular, it is continuous on [0,2][0,2]. Now f⁢(0)=−3f(0)=-3 and f⁢(2)=1f(2)=1, so 0 is a number between f⁢(0)f(0) and f⁢(2)f(2). Hence, by the Intermediate Value Theorem, there exists c∈[0,2]c\in[0,2] such that f⁢(c)=0f(c)=0. That is, there exists c∈[0,2]c\in[0,2] such that c2=3c^{2}=3.

Note that the Intermediate Value Theorem only tells us that cc exists, not what it is, or whether it is unique (maybe there are hundreds of different cc’s!). However, by repeatedly applying the theorem, we can find an approximate solution to f⁢(c)=0f(c)=0 to arbitrarily high accuracy. We know that f⁢(0)<0f(0)<0 and f⁢(2)>0f(2)>0, so 0 is a number between f⁢(0)f(0) and f⁢(2)f(2), and hence, there is some c∈[0,2]c\in[0,2] such that f⁢(c)=0f(c)=0. Now test the midpoint of [0,2][0,2]:

f⁢(1)\displaystyle f(1) =\displaystyle= 1−3<0\displaystyle 1-3<0\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad

So 0 is a number between f⁢(1)f(1) and f⁢(2)f(2), and ff is continuous on [1,2][1,2], so by the IVT, there exists c∈[1,2]c\in[1,2] such that f⁢(c)=0f(c)=0. (Had f⁢(1)f(1) been positive, we’d have deduced that c∈[0,1]c\in[0,1]. Had it been 0, we’d have deduced that c=1c=1 exactly!) Now test the midpoint of [1,2][1,2], and so on:

f⁢(1.5)\displaystyle f(1.5) =\displaystyle= −0.75<0⇒c∈[1.5,2]\displaystyle-0.75<0\qquad\Rightarrow\qquad c\in[1.5,2]
f⁢(1.75)\displaystyle f(1.75) =\displaystyle= 0.0625>0⇒c∈[1.5,1.75]\displaystyle 0.0625>0\qquad\Rightarrow\qquad c\in[1.5,1.75]
f⁢(1.625)\displaystyle f(1.625) =\displaystyle= −0.359375<0⇒c∈[1.625,1.75]\displaystyle-0.359375<0\qquad\Rightarrow\qquad c\in[1.625,1.75]

This is called the “bisection method”. Each time we test the midpoint and apply the IVT, we halve the width of the interval in which we’ve “trapped” our solution cc. We initially know cc is in [0,2][0,2] an interval of width 22, so after nn iterations of the method, we know that cc is in some interval of width 2×(1/2)n=1/2n−12\times(1/2)^{n}=1/2^{n-1} (check: after n=4n=4 iterations, we should have cc trapped in an interval of width 1/81/8 — correct!). Let’s say we need to know cc to within an error of 5×10−55\times 10^{-5}. Then we need to trap it in an interval of width less than 0.000050.00005. How many iterations of the method do we need? The answer is any nn for which

12n−1<0.00005.\frac{1}{2^{n-1}}<0.00005.

will do. We know that such an nn exists because the sequence 1/2n−11/2^{n-1} converges to 0. If we allow ourselves to use logarithms, we can solve the above inequality. Otherwise, trial and error (substituting values of nn) shows that n=16n=16 is the smallest positive integer satisfying the inequality. So, not only will the bisection method give us the (approximate) solution to the accuracy we require, we can figure out before we start how many iterations we’ll need to make to get this accuracy.

By the way, we’ve just proved that 3\sqrt{3} exists! We can easily generalize the argument above to show that every x≥0x\geq 0 has a square root x≥0\sqrt{x}\geq 0, and that this number is unique.

Proposition/Definition 8.9

For each x∈[0,∞)x\in[0,\infty) there exists a unique y∈[0,∞)y\in[0,\infty) such that y2=xy^{2}=x. We denote this number x\sqrt{x} and call it the square root of xx.

Proof.

Consider the function f:[0,∞)→ℝf:[0,\infty)\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},

noting that it is polynomial and hence continuous. Then f⁢(0)=−x≤0f(0)=-x\leq 0 and

f⁢(x+12)=(x+12)2−x=x2+14≥14,f(x+\frac{1}{2})=\left(x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2}-x=x^{2}+\frac{1}{4}\geq\frac{1% }{4},

so 0 is a number between f⁢(0)f(0) and f⁢(x+12)f(x+\frac{1}{2}). Hence, by the Intermediate Value Theorem, there exists y∈[0,x+12]y\in[0,x+\frac{1}{2}] such that f⁢(y)=0f(y)=0. Then y2=xy^{2}=x, by the definition of ff, so x\sqrt{x} exists. It remains to prove that it is unique.

Let z∈[0,∞)z\in[0,\infty) satisfy f⁢(z)=0f(z)=0 also. Then

0=f⁢(y)−f⁢(z)=y2−z2=(y−z)⁢(y+z),0=f(y)-f(z)=y^{2}-z^{2}=(y-z)(y+z),

so z=yz=y or z=−yz=-y. But since z≥0z\geq 0 and y≥0y\geq 0, z=−yz=-y implies z=0=yz=0=y. So f⁢(z)=f⁢(y)f(z)=f(y) implies that z=yz=y. Hence the square root of xx is unique. ∎

We just proved that there exists a function

f:[0,∞)→[0,∞),f⁢(x)=x.f:[0,\infty)\rightarrow[0,\infty),\qquad f(x)=\sqrt{x}.

What properties does it have? Is it injective? Surjective? Continuous? Can you prove your answers?

8.4 Maxima, minima and the Extreme Value Theorem

We have seen that continuous functions on closed bounded intervals [a,b][a,b] have the very useful and fundamental “intermediate value” property: if they attain two values y1y_{1} and y2y_{2}, they attain all values between y1y_{1} and y2y_{2}. In this section we will prove they have another fundamental property: they are bounded (above and below), and attain both a maximum and a minimum value. Before proceeding further, we need to make all these terms precise. Recall that the range of a function f:D→Ef:D\rightarrow E is the subset

f⁢(D)={f⁢(x)∈E:x∈D}f(D)=\{f(x)\in E:x\in D\}

of EE.

Definition 8.10

A function f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is bounded above if its range f⁢(D)⊆ℝf(D)\subseteq{\mathbb{R}} is bounded above, that is, if there exists L∈ℝL\in{\mathbb{R}} such that, for all x∈Dx\in D, f⁢(x)≤Lf(x)\leq L. We call any such L∈ℝL\in{\mathbb{R}} an upper bound on ff.

Similarly, ff is bounded below if f⁢(D)⊆ℝf(D)\subseteq{\mathbb{R}} is bounded below, that is, if there exists K∈ℝK\in{\mathbb{R}} such that, for all x∈Dx\in D, f⁢(x)≥Kf(x)\geq K. We call any such K∈ℝK\in{\mathbb{R}} a lower bound on ff.

Definition 8.11

A function f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} attains a maximum at c∈Dc\in D if, for all x∈Dx\in D, f⁢(x)≤f⁢(c)f(x)\leq f(c). It attains a minimum at c∈Dc\in D if, for all x∈Dx\in D, f⁢(x)≥f⁢(c)f(x)\geq f(c).

Remark Note that these definitions have absolutely nothing to do with calculus! In fact, they make sense even if DD is not a subset of ℝ{\mathbb{R}}. For example, DD could be the set of books on my bookshelf, and ff could be the map which assigns to each book xx the number of pages it contains. Clearly this function will attain both a maximum and a minimum (my bookshelf will contain both a longest and shortest book), but it’s absurd to try to define its derivative.

Note also that, if a function attains a maximum, at cc say, it is certainly bounded above, by f⁢(c)f(c). Similarly, if it attains a minimum at bb, it is bounded below by f⁢(b)f(b). But it’s quite possible for a function to be bounded above but not attain a maximum, or bounded below and not attain a minimum. For example

f:(0,∞)→ℝ,f⁢(x)=1xf:(0,\infty)\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad f(x)=\frac{1}{x}

is bounded below, by 0 for example, but does not attain a minimum: whatever c∈(0,∞)c\in(0,\infty) we try, there is always some other point x∈(0,∞)x\in(0,\infty) with f⁢(x)<f⁢(c)f(x)<f(c): we could take x=c+1x=c+1, for example.

If ff is bounded above, then its range f⁢(D)f(D) is a nonempty subset of ℝ{\mathbb{R}} that is bounded above which, by the Axiom of Completeness, has a supremum. We often refer to this number as the supremum of the function ff. Similarly, if ff is bounded below, we define its infimum to tbe the infimum of its range.

Finally, note that if ff attains a maximum at cc, then its supremum is precisely f⁢(c)f(c): for all x∈Dx\in D, f⁢(x)≤f⁢(c)f(x)\leq f(c), so f⁢(c)f(c) is an upper bound on ff, and any number K<f⁢(c)K<f(c) is not an upper bound on ff, since f⁢(c)>Kf(c)>K! Similarly, if ff attains a minimum at bb then its infimum is f⁢(b)f(b).

We may now prove our next big theorem:

Theorem 8.12 (Extreme Value Theorem)

Let f:[a,b]→ℝf:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be continuous. Then ff is bounded and attains both a maximum and a minimum.

Proof.

We first prove that ff is bounded above. Assume, towards a contradiction that ff is not bounded above. Then every n∈ℤ+n\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} is not an upper bound on ff, so there exists xn∈[a,b]x_{n}\in[a,b] such that f⁢(xn)>nf(x_{n})>n. But (xn)(x_{n}) is a bounded sequence (above by bb, below by aa), so has a convergent subsequence (xnk)(x_{n_{k}}) by the Bolzano-Weiertsrass Theorem. Since a≤xnk≤ba\leq x_{n_{k}}\leq b, its limit cc, say, is also in [a,b][a,b]. Since xnk→c∈[a,b]x_{n_{k}}\rightarrow c\in[a,b] and ff is continuous (at cc, in particular), f⁢(xnk)→f⁢(c)f(x_{n_{k}})\rightarrow f(c). Since f⁢(xnk)f(x_{n_{k}}) converges, it is bounded above. But f⁢(xnk)>nk≥kf(x_{n_{k}})>n_{k}\geq k, a contradiction. Hence, ff is bounded above.

We now prove that ff attains its supremum.

Since the set A={f⁢(x):x∈[a,b]}A=\{f(x):x\in[a,b]\} is bounded above, and is nonempty, it has a supremum LL, say, by the Axiom of Completeness. Now, for each n∈ℤ+n\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+}, L−1/n<LL-1/n<L, so is not an upper bound on AA. Hence, for each n∈ℤ+n\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+}, there exists yn∈[a,b]y_{n}\in[a,b] such that f⁢(yn)>L−1/nf(y_{n})>L-1/n. Clearly f⁢(yn)≤Lf(y_{n})\leq L so, by the Squeeze Rule, f⁢(yn)→Lf(y_{n})\rightarrow L. Since (yn)(y_{n}) is bounded, it too has a convergent subsequence ymky_{m_{k}} say, converging to some limit d∈[a,b]d\in[a,b], by the Bolzano-Weiertsrass Theorem. Since ff is continuous at dd, f⁢(ymk)→f⁢(d)f(y_{m_{k}})\rightarrow f(d). But f⁢(ymk)→Lf(y_{m_{k}})\rightarrow L, since it is a subsequence of f⁢(yn)f(y_{n}). Limits are unique, so f⁢(d)=L=supAf(d)=L=\sup A. Hence, for all x∈[a,b]x\in[a,b], f⁢(x)≤f⁢(d)f(x)\leq f(d), that is, ff attains a maximum (at dd).

It follows immediately that ff attains a minimum also: let g:[a,b]→ℝg:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}}, g⁢(x)=−f⁢(x)g(x)=-f(x). This is continuous so, by the work above, attains a maximum at some point d∈[a,b]d\in[a,b]. But then ff attains a minimum at dd. ∎

Note that all the hypotheses are important for this theorem. If f:[a,b]→ℝf:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is not continuous, it may fail to be bounded:

f:[0,1]→ℝ,f⁢(x)={1x,x∈(0,1]0,x=0,f:[0,1]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad f(x)=\left\{\begin{array}[]{cc}\frac{1}{% x},&x\in(0,1]\\ 0,&x=0,\end{array}\right.

or be bounded but fail to attain a maximum or minimum:

f:[0,1]→ℝ,f⁢(x)={x,x∈(0,1)1/2,x∈{0,1}.f:[0,1]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad f(x)=\left\{\begin{array}[]{cc}x,&x\in(0% ,1)\\ 1/2,&x\in\{0,1\}.\end{array}\right.

Similarly, if the domain is not a closed bounded interval, the conclusion may fail, even if ff is continuous:

f:(0,1)→ℝ,f⁢(x)=1xf:(0,1)\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad f(x)=\frac{1}{x}

is unbounded, and

f:(0,1)→ℝ,f⁢(x)=xf:(0,1)\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},\qquad f(x)=x

is bounded but attains neither a maximum nor a minimum. These examples are depicted below:

8.5 An equivalent definition of continuity

We have defined continuity using convergence of sequences: f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is continuous at a∈Da\in D if, for all sequences (xn)(x_{n}) in DD that converge to aa, f⁢(xn)→f⁢(a)f(x_{n})\rightarrow f(a). Many mathematicians call this property “sequential continuity”, and define continuity at a∈Da\in D in a rather different way, by means of what is known as an “ε\varepsilon–δ\delta criterion.”

Definition 8.13 (The ϵ\epsilon–δ\delta definition of continuity)

A function f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow\mathbb{R} is ϵ\epsilon–δ\delta continuous at a point a∈Da\in D if for each ϵ>0\epsilon>0 there exists δ>0\delta>0 so that, for all x∈Dx\in D, if |x−a|<δ|x-a|<\delta then

|f⁢(x)−f⁢(a)|<ϵ.|f(x)-f(a)|<\epsilon\ .

It turns out that these two ways to define continuity are exactly equivalent.

Theorem 8.14

A function f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is continuous at a∈Da\in D if and only it is ϵ\epsilon–δ\delta continuous at aa.

Proof.

Assume ff is ϵ\epsilon–δ\delta continuous at a∈Da\in D, and let (xn)(x_{n}) be any sequence in DD such that xn→ax_{n}\rightarrow a. We must show that f⁢(xn)→f⁢(a)f(x_{n})\rightarrow f(a), so ff is continuous at aa. We will do this by formulating a direct ε\varepsilon–NN proof of convergence.

So, let ε∈(0,∞)\varepsilon\in(0,\infty) be given. Since ff is ϵ\epsilon–δ\delta continuous at aa, there exists δ>0\delta>0 such that, for all x∈Dx\in D with |x−a|<δ|x-a|<\delta, |f⁢(x)−f⁢(a)|<ε|f(x)-f(a)|<\varepsilon. Choose such a number δ>0\delta>0. Since xn→ax_{n}\rightarrow a, by definition there exists N∈ℤ+N\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+} such that, for all n≥Nn\geq N, |xn−a|<δ|x_{n}-a|<\delta. But then, for all n≥Nn\geq N, xn∈Dx_{n}\in D and |xn−a|<δ|x_{n}-a|<\delta, so f(xn)−f(a)|<εf(x_{n})-f(a)|<\varepsilon (recall we chose δ\delta specifically to ensure this was true!). Hence f⁢(xn)→f⁢(a)f(x_{n})\rightarrow f(a), as was to be proved.

Conversely, assume that ff is not ε\varepsilon–δ\delta continuous at a∈Da\in D. Then there exists some ε∗>0\varepsilon_{*}>0 such that, for all δ>0\delta>0, there is some x∈Dx\in D with |x−a|<δ|x-a|<\delta such that |f⁢(x)−f⁢(a)|>ε∗|f(x)-f(a)|>\varepsilon_{*}. Such an x∈Dx\in D exists for each δ>0\delta>0 so, in particular, for each δ=1/n\delta=1/n, where n∈ℤ+n\in{\mathbb{Z}}^{+}, there exists xn∈Dx_{n}\in D such that |xn−a|<1/n|x_{n}-a|<1/n but |f⁢(xn)−f⁢(a)|>ε∗|f(x_{n})-f(a)|>\varepsilon_{*}. Clearly xn→ax_{n}\rightarrow a (by the Squeeze Rule) but, equally clearly f⁢(xn)↛f⁢(a)f(x_{n})\nrightarrow f(a) (it fails the ε\varepsilon–NN criterion for convergence when ε=ε∗\varepsilon=\varepsilon_{*}). Hence ff is not continuous at aa. ∎
