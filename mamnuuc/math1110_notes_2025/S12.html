12.1 Dissections and Riemann sums

How do we define the area of a bounded subset of the plane ℝ2{\mathbb{R}}^{2}? If the subset is a rectangle, the answer is easy: its area is its length times its width. Similarly, if the subset is a union of non-overlapping rectangles it’s easy: we just add up the areas of all the constituent rectangles. But what if the subset is more complicated: the region bounded by the xx-axis, the vertical lines x=ax=a and x=b>ax=b>a and the graph y=f⁢(x)y=f(x) of some non-constant function, for example? One approach is to define the area of such a region to be the unique real number (if it exists) which is no bigger than the total area of any collection of rectangles which covers the region, and no smaller than the total area of any collection of rectangles which is covered by the region. This is the underlying idea that leads to the Riemann integral. We begin by identifying the collections of rectangles we will use. These are determined by dissecting the interval [a,b][a,b] into a finite collection of subintervals.

Definition 12.1

A dissection of a closed bounded interval [a,b][a,b] is a finite subset 𝒟\mathscr{D} of [a,b][a,b] containing both aa and bb. By convention, if 𝒟\mathscr{D} has n+1n+1 elements, we label these a0,a1,…,ana_{0},a_{1},\ldots,a_{n}, so that

a=a0<a1<a2<⋯<an=b,a=a_{0}<a_{1}<a_{2}<\cdots<a_{n}=b,

and say that 𝒟\mathscr{D} is a dissection of size nn. We say that 𝒟\mathscr{D} is a regular dissection if aj−aj−1=(b−a)/na_{j}-a_{j-1}=(b-a)/n for all jj, that is, if the points in the dissection are regularly spaced.

Recall that a function f:D→ℝf:D\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is bounded if its range f⁢(D)⊆ℝf(D)\subseteq{\mathbb{R}} is a bounded set, that is, if there exist M,m∈ℝM,m\in{\mathbb{R}} such that, for all x∈Dx\in D, m≤f⁢(x)≤Mm\leq f(x)\leq M.

l𝒟⁢(f)=∑j=1nmj⁢(aj−aj−1),l_{\mathscr{D}}(f)=\sum_{j=1}^{n}m_{j}(a_{j}-a_{j-1}),

u𝒟⁢(f)=∑j=1nMj⁢(aj−aj−1).u_{\mathscr{D}}(f)=\sum_{j=1}^{n}M_{j}(a_{j}-a_{j-1}).

If f⁢(x)≥0f(x)\geq 0 for all xx, then the lower Riemann sum can be visualized as the total area of the tallest rectangles with bases [aj−1,aj][a_{j-1},a_{j}] which fit under the graph y=f⁢(x)y=f(x) between x=ax=a and x=bx=b. So l𝒟⁢(f)l_{\mathscr{D}}(f) is an underestimate of the area under the graph. Similarly, the upper Riemann sum can be visualized as the total area of the shortest rectangles with bases [aj−1,aj][a_{j-1},a_{j}] which the graph y=f⁢(x)y=f(x) between x=ax=a and x=bx=b fits under and is thus an overestimate of the area under the graph. The set of all dissections of a fixed interval [a,b][a,b] is a very large and complicated set. To tame this beast, we need to equip it with some extra structure. The crucial idea turns out to be rather simple: that of refinement.

Definition 12.4

Given dissections 𝒟,𝒟′\mathscr{D},\mathscr{D}^{\prime} of [a,b][a,b], we say that 𝒟′\mathscr{D}^{\prime} is a refinement of 𝒟\mathscr{D} if 𝒟⊆𝒟′\mathscr{D}\subseteq\mathscr{D}^{\prime}. If 𝒟′\𝒟\mathscr{D}^{\prime}{\backslash}\mathscr{D} contains kk points, we say that 𝒟′\mathscr{D}^{\prime} is a kk-point refinement of 𝒟\mathscr{D}.

The idea is that a refinement 𝒟′\mathscr{D}^{\prime} of 𝒟\mathscr{D} contains all the points in 𝒟\mathscr{D} and (unless 𝒟′=𝒟\mathscr{D}^{\prime}=\mathscr{D}) some more points too, so it splits [a,b][a,b] up into more subintervals, at least some of which are narrower. Intuitively, one expects that passing from 𝒟\mathscr{D} to a refinement of 𝒟\mathscr{D} can only improve, that is, increase, the underestimate l𝒟⁢(f)l_{\mathscr{D}}(f). Similarly, passing to a refinement of 𝒟\mathscr{D}, one expects, can only reduce the overestimate u𝒟⁢(f)u_{\mathscr{D}}(f). This expectation turns out to be essentially correct and is fundamental.

It follows immediately from the Refinement Lemma that every upper Riemann sum is at least as large as every lower Riemann sum, whatever (possibly different) dissections we use to compute them.

Lemma 12.6

Let 𝒟,𝒟′\mathscr{D},\mathscr{D}^{\prime} be two dissections of [a,b][a,b] and f:[a,b]→ℝf:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be bounded. Then l𝒟⁢(f)≤u𝒟′⁢(f)l_{\mathscr{D}}(f)\leq u_{\mathscr{D}^{\prime}}(f).

𝒟′′=𝒟∪𝒟′\mathscr{D}^{\prime\prime}=\mathscr{D}\cup\mathscr{D}^{\prime} is a refinement of both 𝒟\mathscr{D} and 𝒟′\mathscr{D}^{\prime}, so by the Refinement Lemma,

l𝒟⁢(f)≤l𝒟′′⁢(f)≤u𝒟′′⁢(f)≤u𝒟′⁢(f).l_{\mathscr{D}}(f)\leq l_{\mathscr{D}^{\prime\prime}}(f)\leq u_{\mathscr{D}^{% \prime\prime}}(f)\leq u_{\mathscr{D}^{\prime}}(f).

∎

Note that we did not assume that either of 𝒟,𝒟′\mathscr{D},\mathscr{D}^{\prime} was a refinement of the other. The result holds completely generally!

12.2 Definition of the Riemann integral

To define the Riemann integral we will use sequences of dissections.

Definition 12.8

A bounded function f:[a,b]→ℝf:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is Riemann integrable if there exists a sequence (𝒟n)(\mathscr{D}_{n}) of dissections of [a,b][a,b] and a real number LL such that

u𝒟n⁢(f)→Landl𝒟n⁢(f)→L.u_{\mathscr{D}_{n}}(f)\rightarrow L\quad\mbox{and}\quad l_{\mathscr{D}_{n}}(f)% \rightarrow L.

In this case, we call LL the Riemann integral of ff over the interval [a,b][a,b], and denote it

∫abf.\int_{a}^{b}f.

12.3 Continuous functions are integrable

We have seen that all differentiable functions are continuous (Theorem 9.8), but not all continuous functions are differentiable. We next prove that every continuous function (on a closed bounded interval) is Riemann integrable. But, for each nn, l𝒟n⁢(g)≥l𝒟n⁢(f)l_{\mathscr{D}_{n}}(g)\geq l_{\mathscr{D}_{n}}(f) since infg≥inff\inf g\geq\inf f on any subset of [a,b][a,b]. Hence, ∫abg−∫abf\int_{a}^{b}g-\int_{a}^{b}f, being the limit of a convergent non-negative sequence, is ≥0\geq 0 (Proposition 2.14). ∎

Recall that the triangle inequality asserts that, for any pair of real numbers x1,x2x_{1},x_{2},

|x1+x2|≤|x1|+|x2|.|x_{1}+x_{2}|\leq|x_{1}|+|x_{2}|.

It is but the work of a moment to prove (by induction) that this extends to arbitrary finite sets of real numbers:

|x1+x2+⋯+xn|≤|x1|+|x2|+⋯+|xn|.|x_{1}+x_{2}+\cdots+x_{n}|\leq|x_{1}|+|x_{2}|+\cdots+|x_{n}|.

Actually, with a bit more work one can show that the same claim holds for infinite sums, that is, series. That is, if a series ∑xn\sum x_{n} converges absolutely, then

|∑n=1∞xn|≤∑n=1∞|xn|.\left|\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}x_{n}\right|\leq\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}|x_{n}|.

(Prove it!) Roughly speaking, we can think of an integral as being a kind of “continuously indexed infinite sum.” So it’s natural to ask whether a Triangle Inequality holds for integrals too.

Theorem 12.18 (The Triangle Inequality for Riemann Integrals)

If f:[a,b]→ℝf:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} is continuous, then

|∫abf|≤∫ab|f|.\left|\int_{a}^{b}f\right|\leq\int_{a}^{b}|f|.
Proof.

For all x∈[a,b]x\in[a,b], −|f⁢(x)|≤f⁢(x)≤|f⁢(x)|-|f(x)|\leq f(x)\leq|f(x)| so, by Proposition 12.17,

−∫ab|f|≤∫abf≤∫ab|f|,-\int_{a}^{b}|f|\leq\int_{a}^{b}f\leq\int_{a}^{b}|f|,

whence the claim immediately follows. ∎

12.5 The Fundamental Theorem of the Calculus

So far we have developed some powerful theoretical tools to show that a given function is integrable and to relate the integrals of related integrable functions, but we don’t have any really convenient techniques for actually computing Riemann integrals. To compute

∫01x2⁢𝑑x,\int_{0}^{1}x^{2}\,dx,

for example, we had to resort to exhibiting a sequence of dissections whose upper and lower sums converge to a common limit, in this case, 13\frac{1}{3} (see Example 12.10). In principle, we can always compute Riemann integrals like this, but in practice this method of computing integrals is very onerous and, unless the integrand (the function to be integrated) is fairly simple, is likely to be intractable. Consider, for example, attempting to compute

∫0πsin⁡x⁢d⁢x\int_{0}^{\pi}\sin x\,dx

using this method. We know that this integral exists (Theorem 12.13) because we know that sin\sin is continuous, but we have no hope of computing it directly using Riemann sums. In this section, we establish a fundamental connexion between Riemann integration and differentiation which will give us a convenient means of computing ∫abf\int_{a}^{b}f whenever we can dream up a function whose derivative equals ff. Once we have done this we will rarely have to resort to computing sequences of Riemann sums to compute integrals.

Theorem 12.19 (Fundamental Theorem of the Calculus version 1)

Let I⊆ℝI\subseteq{\mathbb{R}} be an interval, f:I→ℝf:I\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be continuous and a∈Ia\in I. Define F:I→ℝF:I\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} by

F⁢(x)=∫axf.F(x)=\int_{a}^{x}f.

Then FF is differentiable, and F′=fF^{\prime}=f.

Proof.

First note that FF is well-defined (that is, the integral defining F⁢(x)F(x) exists for all x∈Ix\in I) by Theorem 12.13 since ff is continuous on [a,x]⊆I[a,x]\subseteq I if x≥ax\geq a, and continuous on [x,a]⊆I[x,a]\subseteq I if x<ax<a. We wish to compute

limy→xF⁢(y)−F⁢(x)y−x.\lim_{y\rightarrow x}\frac{F(y)-F(x)}{y-x}.

So, let (yn)(y_{n}) be any sequence in I\{x}I{\backslash}\{x\} such that yn→xy_{n}\rightarrow x, and

sn=F⁢(yn)−F⁢(x)yn−x.s_{n}=\frac{F(y_{n})-F(x)}{y_{n}-x}.

We must show that sn→f⁢(x)s_{n}\rightarrow f(x).

For each nn, either yn>xy_{n}>x or yn<xy_{n}<x. If yn>xy_{n}>x then, by Theorem 12.14,

sn=1yn−x⁢∫xynf,s_{n}=\frac{1}{y_{n}-x}\int_{x}^{y_{n}}f,

whereas if yn<xy_{n}<x,

sn=1x−yn⁢∫ynxf.s_{n}=\frac{1}{x-y_{n}}\int_{y_{n}}^{x}f.

In either case, by the Extreme Value Theorem, there exist wnw_{n} and znz_{n} between xx and yny_{n} such that f⁢(wn)f(w_{n}) is the minimum value of ff on the closed interval with endpoints xx and yny_{n}, and f⁢(zn)f(z_{n}) is the maximum value of ff on this interval. Hence, by Proposition 12.17,

1yn−x⁢f⁢(wn)⁢(yn−x)≤\displaystyle\frac{1}{y_{n}-x}f(w_{n})(y_{n}-x)\leq sn\displaystyle s_{n} ≤1yn−x⁢f⁢(zn)⁢(yn−x)if yn>x,\displaystyle\leq\frac{1}{y_{n}-x}f(z_{n})(y_{n}-x)\qquad\mbox{if $y_{n}>x$,}
and1x−yn⁢f⁢(wn)⁢(x−yn)≤\displaystyle\mbox{and}\qquad\frac{1}{x-y_{n}}f(w_{n})(x-y_{n})\leq sn\displaystyle s_{n} ≤1x−yn⁢f⁢(zn)⁢(x−yn)if yn<x.\displaystyle\leq\frac{1}{x-y_{n}}f(z_{n})(x-y_{n})\qquad\mbox{if $y_{n}<x$.}

In either case, we conclude that

f⁢(wn)≤sn≤f⁢(zn).f(w_{n})\leq s_{n}\leq f(z_{n}).

Now, yn→xy_{n}\rightarrow x so wn→xw_{n}\rightarrow x and zn→xz_{n}\rightarrow x also, by the Squeeze Rule, and ff is continuous, so f⁢(wn)→f⁢(x)f(w_{n})\rightarrow f(x) and f⁢(zn)→f⁢(x)f(z_{n})\rightarrow f(x). Hence, by the Squeeze Rule again, sn→f⁢(x)s_{n}\rightarrow f(x), which completes the proof. ∎

As a by-product of this theorem we see that any function which is continuous on an interval is the derivative of some differentiable function on that interval, an interesting and far from obvious fact. Even better, Theorem 12.19 immediately implies a second theorem which renders the job of computing many Riemann integrals almost trivial:

Theorem 12.20 (Fundamental Theorem of the Calculus version 2)

Let f:[a,b]→ℝf:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be continuous and F:[a,b]→ℝF:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}} be any differentiable function such that F′=fF^{\prime}=f. Then

∫abf=F⁢(b)−F⁢(a).\int_{a}^{b}f=F(b)-F(a).
Proof.

Define the function g:[a,b]→ℝg:[a,b]\rightarrow{\mathbb{R}},

g⁢(x)=(∫axf)−F⁢(x).g(x)=\left(\int_{a}^{x}f\right)-F(x).

Then, by Theorem 12.19 and the definition of FF,

g′⁢(x)=f⁢(x)−f⁢(x)=0g^{\prime}(x)=f(x)-f(x)=0

for all x∈[a,b]x\in[a,b]. Hence, by Proposition 10.9, gg is constant, so g⁢(b)=g⁢(a)g(b)=g(a), that is

(∫abf)−F⁢(b)=(∫aaf)−F⁢(a)=0−F⁢(a),\left(\int_{a}^{b}f\right)-F(b)=\left(\int_{a}^{a}f\right)-F(a)=0-F(a),

and the result immediately follows. ∎

Having established Theorem 12.20, we see that we can explicitly compute

∫abf⁢(x)⁢𝑑x\int_{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx

if we can think of an antiderivative of ff, that is, any function whose derivative is f⁢(x)f(x). This trick is so pervasive in integral calculus that it leads the unwary to identify integration of ff (that is, the process of computing a Riemann integral ∫abf\int_{a}^{b}f) with the process of writing down an antiderivative of ff. Indeed, it is common practice to call an antiderivative of ff an indefinite integral of ff and to denote any such function by the symbol

∫f⁢(x)⁢𝑑x.\int f(x)\,dx.

This notation has some practical advantages, but it also, unfortunately, generates a huge amount of confusion. Note that

∫abf⁢(x)⁢𝑑x\int_{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx

is a single number and that its definition has nothing whatsoever to do with antiderivatives of ff: it is the unique real number which is no greater than any upper Riemann sum of ff on [a,b][a,b] and no less than any lower Riemann sum of ff on [a,b][a,b]. In particular, it is not a function of the “variable” xx. Indeed, we could equally well have written it ∫abf⁢(y)⁢𝑑y\int_{a}^{b}f(y)\,dy or ∫abf⁢(Γℵ^)⁢𝑑Γℵ^\int_{a}^{b}f(\Gamma_{\widehat{\aleph}})\,d\Gamma_{\widehat{\aleph}}, which is one reason to prefer the simpler notation ∫abf\int_{a}^{b}f. It is a function of aa and bb, however.

By contrast, ∫f⁢(x)⁢𝑑x\int f(x)\,dx is just a (slightly ambiguous) symbol denoting any function whose derivative (at xx) is f⁢(x)f(x). It is a function of xx, not a single number, and its definition has nothing to do with Riemann sums. That these two things turn out to be closely related is (as its name suggests) a very important theorem: the Fundamental Theorem of the Calculus. To understand calculus properly it is important to maintain a clear conceptual distinction between the Riemann integral ∫abf\int_{a}^{b}f and any antiderivative that one might use to compute it.

We have now rigorously developed all the fundamental concepts of differential and integral calculus. It would be a straightforward matter to combine the Fundamental Theorem of the Calculus with the Product Rule of differentiation to develop the technique of integration by parts, or with the Chain Rule to develop the technique of integration by substitution. Such development requires no new analytic insight and so is better placed in a course on the methods of calculus, rather than a course on real analysis such as this.
